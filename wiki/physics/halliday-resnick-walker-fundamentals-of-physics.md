# Fundamentals of Physics (Halliday, Resnick & Walker)

The standard calculus-based introductory physics textbook for science and
engineering majors, originally by David Halliday and Robert Resnick and, in its
modern editions, revised by Jearl Walker. It is the workhorse of first-year
university physics: comprehensive, heavily worked, and organized around building
problem-solving fluency across the whole classical canon before quantum ideas
appear.

## Scope and approach

The book assumes concurrent single-variable calculus and develops physics
topic by topic with a large volume of worked examples, checkpoints, and
end-of-chapter problems. Its coverage is broad rather than deep, spanning:

- **Mechanics** — kinematics, Newton's laws, work and energy, momentum,
  rotation, equilibrium, gravitation, fluids, oscillations, and waves. This is
  the core that anchors [classical-mechanics.md](classical-mechanics.md).
- **Thermodynamics** — temperature, heat, the kinetic theory of gases, and the
  laws of thermodynamics, connecting to [thermodynamics.md](thermodynamics.md).
- **Electromagnetism** — electric charge and fields, Gauss's law, potential,
  capacitance, current and circuits, magnetic fields, induction, and Maxwell's
  equations, anchoring [electromagnetism.md](electromagnetism.md).
- **Optics and modern physics** — geometric and physical optics, followed by
  introductory relativity and quantum topics in the extended editions.

## Why it matters

Its value is pedagogical scaffolding: consistent problem-solving strategies,
graded exercises, and clear diagrams that build calculational competence. The
mathematics stays at the level of introductory
[../math/calculus.md](../math/calculus.md), with vector operations introduced as
needed. Where Feynman aims at insight, Halliday-Resnick-Walker aims at coverage
and drill — the two complement each other well as a first pass through classical
physics.
