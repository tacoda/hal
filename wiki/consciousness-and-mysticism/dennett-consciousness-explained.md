# Consciousness Explained

Daniel Dennett (1991). The landmark statement of the **deflationary** (later "illusionist") approach:
the claim that the "hard problem" is a confusion, and that once you explain all the functions and
behaviors, there is nothing extra — no inner show — left to explain.

## The argument

- **Against the Cartesian Theater.** Dennett attacks the intuitive picture of a central place in the
  mind where "it all comes together" and is presented to an inner audience. There is no such theater
  and no homunculus watching it.
- **The Multiple Drafts Model.** Instead, the brain runs many parallel processes continuously editing
  "drafts" of content; there is no single canonical stream and no precise moment when something
  "becomes conscious." What we call consciousness is the ongoing activity of these distributed
  processes, not a special added property.
- **Qualia dissolved.** Dennett argues the philosophers' **qualia** — private, ineffable, intrinsic
  feels — do not exist as advertised; the felt richness of experience is a kind of user-illusion the
  brain generates. Reports of experience are real; the mysterious inner glow is not.

## Why it matters

*Consciousness Explained* is the principal foil to [Chalmers](chalmers-the-conscious-mind.md) and
[Nagel](nagel-what-is-it-like-to-be-a-bat.md): where they hold subjectivity irreducible, Dennett holds
it a well-explained bag of functional tricks (critics retort the book is "consciousness explained
*away*"). The Dennett–Chalmers axis — **illusionism vs. the hard problem** — is the central fault line
in [philosophy of mind](../philosophy/philosophy-of-mind.md), and Dennett's functionalism is
congenial to strong claims about [machine minds](../philosophy/philosophy-of-ai.md).
