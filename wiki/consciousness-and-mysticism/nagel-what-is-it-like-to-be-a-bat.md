# What Is It Like to Be a Bat?

Thomas Nagel (1974), a short philosophy paper that became the most cited touchstone in the modern
study of consciousness. Its lasting contribution is a criterion and a challenge that frame the whole
field.

## The argument

- **The mark of consciousness.** An organism is conscious if and only if there is **"something it is
  like" to be it** — a subjective, first-person character to its experience. This phrase became the
  standard gloss for consciousness itself.
- **The bat.** A bat navigates by echolocation, a sensory modality utterly unlike ours. We can know
  everything about bat neurophysiology and still not know *what it is like* to experience the world
  as a bat. The subjective point of view is not captured by any amount of objective, third-person
  fact.
- **The limits of reductionism.** Objective science describes reality from no particular point of
  view; but consciousness *is* a point of view. So a purely physical, third-person account seems to
  leave out exactly the thing to be explained — the subjective character of experience. Nagel does
  not conclude that physicalism is false, but that we lack any conception of how it could be true.

## Why it matters

Nagel crystallized the **subjectivity problem** that David Chalmers later named the
[hard problem](chalmers-the-conscious-mind.md), and put **qualia** (the felt quality of experience) at
the center of [philosophy of mind](../philosophy/philosophy-of-mind.md). Every subsequent theory —
[Dennett's deflation](dennett-consciousness-explained.md), [IIT](tononi-integrated-information-theory.md),
[predictive processing](seth-being-you.md) — is in part a response to Nagel's challenge. It also bears
on the limits of [scientific explanation](../science/what-is-science.md) itself.
