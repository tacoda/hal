# The Book: On the Taboo Against Knowing Who You Are

Alan Watts (1966). Watts's most concentrated statement of his central idea, written as the book he
wished he could give a child to explain "who you really are." Its source is
[Vedanta](../eastern-philosophy/hindu-philosophy.md) — the identity of the self with ultimate reality
— rendered in plain modern language.

## What it argues

- **The skin-encapsulated ego is an illusion.** Western culture teaches us to feel like a separate
  self, an ego "inside" the body looking out at an alien world. Watts calls this the deepest and most
  socially reinforced illusion — a "taboo" against realizing otherwise.
- **You are the universe experiencing itself.** Drawing on the Vedantic identity of
  [Atman and Brahman](../eastern-philosophy/the-upanishads.md) ("*that thou art*"), Watts argues the
  self and the cosmos are not two. Organism and environment are a single process; the individual is
  the whole universe "peopling" in a particular place, the way an apple tree "apples." The sense of
  being a separate fragment is a trick of social conditioning.
- **The world as play.** Reality is better understood as *lila* — cosmic play — than as a serious
  problem to be solved; taking the separate self seriously is the root confusion.

## Why it matters

*The Book* is the clearest popular statement of **non-dual** metaphysics — the claim that the
deepest self and ultimate reality are one — the [Hindu Vedanta](../eastern-philosophy/hindu-philosophy.md)
position, aimed squarely at the Western intuition of the isolated ego. It bears directly on the
[self / non-self debate](../eastern-philosophy/eastern-and-western-philosophy-compared.md) and on
[philosophy of mind](../philosophy/philosophy-of-mind.md).
