# The Varieties of Religious Experience

William James (1902), the published Gifford Lectures. The founding work of the **psychology of
religion** and still the classic empirical study of **mystical and religious states of
consciousness** — by one of the founders of modern [psychology](../psychology/index.md).

## The argument

- **Study the experience, not the institution.** James brackets theology and churches to examine
  **first-person religious experience** — conversions, saintliness, mysticism — as psychological
  phenomena, gathering hundreds of personal accounts and treating them as data.
- **The marks of mystical experience.** He identifies four hallmarks: **ineffability** (it resists
  description), a **noetic quality** (it feels like *knowledge*, a revelation of truth), **transiency**
  (it does not last), and **passivity** (it comes upon one, as if grasped by something greater).
  Across traditions and across drug-induced, meditative, and spontaneous cases, the core state is
  recognizably the same.
- **Pragmatic evaluation.** True to his [pragmatism](../philosophy/index.md), James judges such
  experiences by their **fruits** — their effect on how a person lives — rather than by their
  metaphysical pedigree. He takes them seriously as genuine, life-transforming data about the range of
  consciousness, without dogmatically ruling on their ultimate cause.

## Why it matters

James legitimized the study of altered and mystical states as a psychological subject, more than a
century before the current revival. His four marks remain the standard description of mystical
consciousness, and his open, empirical, fruits-first stance models how to take
[mysticism](huxley-the-doors-of-perception.md) seriously without credulity. It anchors the folder's
[mystical](../eastern-philosophy/index.md) strand in rigorous psychology and connects to
[religious experience](../religion/index.md).
