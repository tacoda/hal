# The Feeling of What Happens

Antonio Damasio (1999), subtitle *Body and Emotion in the Making of Consciousness*. A neuroscientist's
account that puts the **body and emotion** — not abstract cognition — at the root of consciousness and
the self.

## The argument

- **Consciousness is built on feeling.** For Damasio, consciousness begins not in language or reason
  but in the brain's continuous mapping of the **body's own state**. Emotions are bodily responses;
  **feelings** are the brain's perception of those bodily changes. This felt, embodied substrate is
  the ground floor of being conscious.
- **A hierarchy of self.** He distinguishes layers:
  - **Proto-self** — nonconscious neural maps of the body's moment-to-moment condition.
  - **Core consciousness** — a transient, here-and-now sense of self that arises as the brain
    registers *how the organism is changed by* engaging an object; the pulse of "the feeling of what
    happens."
  - **Extended consciousness / autobiographical self** — the full, memory-laden identity built on top,
    with a past and a projected future.
- **Feeling before thinking.** Reversing the intellectualist tradition, Damasio makes affect and the
  body foundational; the reasoning self is a later, dependent construction (his earlier *Descartes'
  Error* argues emotion is essential to rational decision-making).

## Why it matters

Damasio grounds consciousness and selfhood in **homeostasis and emotion**, an embodied,
biologically continuous story that contrasts with computational and purely cognitive accounts. It
complements [Seth's interoceptive self](seth-being-you.md), enriches the layered picture of
[the self](metzinger-the-ego-tunnel.md), and anchors the affective side of
[neuroscience](../neuroscience/index.md) and [philosophy of mind](../philosophy/philosophy-of-mind.md).
