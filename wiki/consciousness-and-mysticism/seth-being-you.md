# Being You

Anil Seth (2021), subtitle *A New Science of Consciousness*. A contemporary neuroscientist's synthesis
built on **predictive processing** — the idea that the brain is fundamentally a prediction machine,
and that perception and selfhood are its "controlled hallucinations."

## The argument

- **Perception as controlled hallucination.** The brain never accesses the world directly; it
  generates **predictions** about the causes of its sensory input and updates them against incoming
  signals (prediction error). What we experience is the brain's *best guess* — a controlled
  hallucination "reined in" by the senses. Reality as we live it is a construction, not a readout.
- **The real problem.** Seth sets aside both [Chalmers's hard problem](chalmers-the-conscious-mind.md)
  and [Dennett's dismissal](dennett-consciousness-explained.md) for the **"real problem"**: explain,
  predict, and control the *properties* of conscious experiences in terms of brain mechanisms, and
  let the metaphysics follow. Pragmatic, empirically tractable middle path.
- **The self as a perception too.** The **self** — body ownership, agency, identity, the feeling of
  being someone — is itself a bundle of predictive models the brain constructs, not a fixed inner
  essence. Rooted in **interoception** (predicting the body's internal state), selfhood is a
  controlled hallucination aimed at keeping the organism alive. This resonates strikingly with
  [Metzinger's self-model](metzinger-the-ego-tunnel.md) and with [Buddhist
  anatta](../eastern-philosophy/buddhism.md).

## Why it matters

*Being You* is the most accessible statement of the **predictive-processing** paradigm now dominant in
cognitive neuroscience, and it reframes both perception and the self as active constructions. It sits
beside [global workspace](dehaene-consciousness-and-the-brain.md) and
[IIT](tononi-integrated-information-theory.md) as a major current theory, and connects
[neuroscience](../neuroscience/index.md) to the ancient [no-self](../eastern-philosophy/eastern-and-western-philosophy-compared.md)
intuition.
