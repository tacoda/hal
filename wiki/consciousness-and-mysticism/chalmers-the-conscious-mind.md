# The Conscious Mind

David Chalmers (1996), subtitle *In Search of a Fundamental Theory*. The book that named the **"hard
problem of consciousness"** and made it the organizing question of the field. Rigorous and analytic,
it argues that consciousness cannot be reduced to the physical and must be taken as fundamental.

## The argument

- **Easy problems vs. the hard problem.** The **easy problems** are explaining cognitive *functions* —
  perception, attention, reporting, control — which yield to standard science. The **hard problem** is
  why any of this functioning is accompanied by *subjective experience* at all. Explaining the
  functions leaves untouched the question of why it *feels like* something (building on
  [Nagel](nagel-what-is-it-like-to-be-a-bat.md)).
- **The zombie argument.** Chalmers argues a **philosophical zombie** — a being physically identical
  to a conscious person but with no inner experience — is *conceivable*, and hence (he argues)
  metaphysically possible. If so, consciousness is not logically entailed by the physical facts, so
  physicalism is false.
- **Naturalistic dualism.** His positive view: experience is a **fundamental feature** of reality,
  like mass or charge, linked to the physical by basic psychophysical laws. He floats
  **panpsychism** (consciousness pervasive at some basic level) as a serious option, and proposes
  information as the bridge.

## Why it matters

*The Conscious Mind* set the modern agenda: "the hard problem" is now the standard framing, and every
theory of consciousness positions itself for or against Chalmers. It is the sharp opposite of
[Dennett's deflationary view](dennett-consciousness-explained.md) that there is no hard problem, and a
key text in [philosophy of mind](../philosophy/philosophy-of-mind.md) with implications for
[philosophy of AI](../philosophy/philosophy-of-ai.md) (could a machine be conscious, or a zombie?).
