# Consciousness and the Brain

Stanislas Dehaene (2014), subtitle *Deciphering How the Brain Codes Our Thoughts*. A leading
cognitive neuroscientist's account of what experiment has actually established about consciousness —
the empirical, lab-driven complement to the philosophy.

## The argument

- **Studying consciousness scientifically.** Dehaene shows how the field became rigorous by using
  **contrastive methods**: present the *same* stimulus so that it is sometimes consciously perceived
  and sometimes not (masking, attentional blink, binocular rivalry), and compare brain activity. The
  difference reveals the **neural signatures of consciousness**.
- **The signatures of awareness.** Conscious access is marked by a late (~300 ms), sudden "ignition"
  — a burst of widespread, sustained, synchronized activity spreading to prefrontal and parietal
  cortex — whereas unconscious processing stays local and brief. Consciousness is a global broadcast.
- **Global Neuronal Workspace theory.** Dehaene (with Baars and Changeux) frames this as the **Global
  Neuronal Workspace**: the brain has many specialized unconscious processors, and information becomes
  *conscious* when it is selected and broadcast widely, making it available to the whole system for
  report, memory, and flexible use. Consciousness is global availability of information.

## Why it matters

Dehaene grounds consciousness studies in reproducible experiment, focusing on **access
consciousness** (information globally available) — arguably sidestepping
[Chalmers's hard problem](chalmers-the-conscious-mind.md) rather than solving it, which is the standard
critique. It is a principal rival framework to [IIT](tononi-integrated-information-theory.md) and a
model of how [neuroscience](../neuroscience/index.md) makes a slippery phenomenon
[measurable](../science/observation-and-measurement.md).
