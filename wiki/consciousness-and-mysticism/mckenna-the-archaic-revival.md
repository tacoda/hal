# The Archaic Revival

Terence McKenna (1991), subtitle *Speculations on Psychedelic Mushrooms, the Amazon, Virtual Reality,
UFOs, Evolution, Shamanism, the Rebirth of the Goddess, and the End of History*. A collection of
essays and interviews that gathers McKenna's central themes; a good entry point to his thought.

## What it argues

- **The archaic revival.** McKenna's organizing idea: modern industrial culture is spiritually
  bankrupt, and its renewal requires a *return* to "archaic" — pre-modern, shamanic, ecstatic — modes
  of experience. He reads twentieth-century phenomena (surrealism, rock music, psychedelia, interest
  in the "goddess," body-piercing, virtual reality) as symptoms of a culture unconsciously reaching
  back toward the archaic.
- **Shamanism and the psychedelic experience.** Central is the [shaman](../religion/index.md) as
  technician of ecstasy who uses plant hallucinogens to enter other realms. McKenna vividly describes
  the DMT experience and its recurring "self-transforming machine elves" — autonomous entities
  encountered in the trance — treating the psychedelic state as a genuine, information-rich mode of
  consciousness rather than mere hallucination.
- **Speculation, openly labeled.** The subtitle's "speculations" is honest: the essays range from
  serious ethnobotany and cultural criticism to frank conjecture about UFOs, evolution, and the "end
  of history" (his [novelty theory](mckenna-the-invisible-landscape.md)). McKenna presents these as
  provocations, not proofs.

## Why it matters

*The Archaic Revival* is the most convenient survey of McKenna's worldview and coined the phrase that
names it. It captures his core claim — that expanded states of consciousness, long cultivated by
[shamanic traditions](../anthropology/ritual-symbolism-and-religion.md), are a resource modernity has
foolishly discarded. Engage it as cultural criticism and imaginative speculation, weighing the
[psychology of altered states](../psychology/index.md) against the unfalsifiable claims.
