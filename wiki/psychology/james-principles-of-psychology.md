# The Principles of Psychology (William James, 1890)

William James's two-volume *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) is the founding
text of American scientific psychology. Written over roughly a decade, it set out
to establish psychology as a **natural science of mental life** — grounded in
biology and physiology, but honest about the introspective data of a conscious
mind. Its twenty-eight chapters range from brain function and reflex action to
the will, and it became the canonical reference against which the young
discipline defined itself. James is the intellectual root of **functionalism**:
the view that mental processes should be studied for what they *do* — how they
help an organism adapt to its environment — rather than merely decomposed into
static elements. This is a direct contrast to the structuralism of Wundt and
Titchener that dominated the same era.

## Central ideas

- **The stream of consciousness.** James's most durable metaphor: consciousness
  is not a chain of discrete "ideas" strung together (as the associationists
  held) but a continuous, flowing, ever-changing *stream* of thought. It is
  personal (always someone's), continuous (no gaps felt from within), constantly
  changing, selective (attending to some things and ignoring others), and always
  dealing with objects independent of itself. This reframing pushed against the
  atomistic "mind-stuff" theories he criticizes elsewhere in the book.

- **Habit.** James devotes a famous chapter to habit as the "enormous
  flywheel of society." Behavior repeated becomes automatic, offloading effort
  from conscious attention and freeing the mind for higher work. His practical
  maxims — launch a new habit with strong initiative, never allow an exception
  until it is rooted, seize the first chance to act on every resolution — treat
  character as a bundle of habits laid down in the nervous system's plasticity.

- **Emotion (the James–Lange theory).** James inverts common sense: we do not
  tremble because we are afraid; we feel afraid *because* we tremble. The bodily
  response — racing heart, tensed muscles, fleeing — comes first, and the felt
  emotion is our perception of those visceral and motor changes. This
  peripheral-feedback theory made emotion an empirical, physiological subject.

- **Attention and will.** Attention is the mind's selective focusing among many
  possible objects; James treats "voluntary attention" — the effort of holding a
  faint or uninteresting idea before the mind — as the core act of will and the
  seat of moral effort.

- **The self.** James distinguishes the **"I"** (the knowing self, the stream of
  thought as it flows) from the **"Me"** (the self as known — the material,
  social, and spiritual constituents a person identifies with).

These ideas anchor the [functionalist and pre-experimental era in the
history of the discipline](history-and-schools-of-psychology.md), and James's
concern with method — introspection and its "snares," the physiological
substrate of mind — prefigures later [research methods in
psychology](research-methods-in-psychology.md). His treatment of habit and
conditioning-adjacent automaticity connects to the later behaviorist program on
[learning and conditioning](learning-and-conditioning.md), even though James
himself worked before that framework. His account of the mind as embodied and
adaptive links to broader questions in [neuroscience](../neuroscience/index.md)
and [philosophy of mind](../philosophy/index.md).
