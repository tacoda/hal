# Feeling Good — The New Mood Therapy (David D. Burns)

*Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy* (1980) is psychiatrist David D. Burns's
enormously popular translation of **cognitive therapy** — the approach developed
by his mentor Aaron T. Beck — into a self-help book for a general audience. It is
one of the most widely recommended books in **bibliotherapy** for depression, and
it did more than any other single work to bring the ideas of cognitive behavioral
therapy (CBT) out of the clinic and into ordinary readers' hands.

## Central ideas

- **The cognitive model: thoughts drive feelings.** Burns's foundational claim is
  that emotions do not arise directly from events but from the *thoughts* we have
  about them. Depression and anxiety are sustained by a stream of distorted,
  negative automatic thoughts. Change the thinking and the feeling changes — the
  chain runs **event → thought → feeling**.

- **Cognitive distortions.** The book's best-known contribution is its catalog of
  systematic thinking errors that fuel low mood, including:
  **all-or-nothing thinking**, **overgeneralization**, **mental filtering**,
  **disqualifying the positive**, **jumping to conclusions** (mind-reading and
  fortune-telling), **magnification/catastrophizing and minimization**,
  **emotional reasoning** ("I feel worthless, so I must be"),
  **"should" statements**, **labeling and mislabeling**, and
  **personalization**. Learning to spot these by name is the first therapeutic
  step.

- **Practical techniques.** Burns operationalizes the therapy with concrete
  tools — most famously the **triple-column / daily mood log**: write down the
  automatic negative thought, identify which distortion it commits, then compose a
  rational response. Behavioral methods (activity scheduling to counter
  procrastination and inactivity) complement the cognitive work.

- **Empowerment and self-help.** The book's stance is that these skills can be
  learned and practiced by the reader, not reserved for the therapist's office —
  though it does not position itself as a replacement for treatment of severe
  illness.

## Significance and cross-links

*Feeling Good* sits within [clinical and abnormal
psychology](clinical-and-abnormal-psychology.md) as the popular gateway to CBT,
the most empirically supported psychotherapy for depression and anxiety. Its
cognitive model — that maladaptive interpretations, not events, produce distress,
and that they can be restructured — is one of the pillars of the therapy section
in any [introductory psychology survey](myers-psychology.md). Burns later
extended this framework into **TEAM-CBT** (Testing, Empathy, Agenda-setting,
Methods) in his subsequent books.
