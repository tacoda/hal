# Influence — The Psychology of Persuasion (Robert Cialdini)

Robert Cialdini's *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (first published 1984,
extensively revised since) is the definitive popular-science synthesis of **why
people say yes**. Cialdini, a social psychologist, spent years going undercover
in the "compliance professions" — sales, fundraising, advertising, recruiting —
to catalog the tactics practitioners use, then traced each back to an underlying
psychological principle validated by experiment. The book's thesis is that most
compliance runs on a handful of deeply wired **automatic shortcuts**: heuristics
that usually serve us well but that can be triggered mechanically, like a
"click-whirr" fixed-action pattern, to manufacture agreement.

## The six principles of persuasion

- **Reciprocity.** We feel obligated to return favors, gifts, and concessions.
  An unsolicited gift or an initial concession creates a felt debt that biases us
  toward compliance — even when the return far outweighs the original favor.

- **Commitment and consistency.** Once we take a stand or make a small
  commitment — especially publicly, effortfully, and voluntarily — we feel
  pressure to behave consistently with it. Persuaders secure a small initial
  agreement (the "foot in the door") to make a larger one feel congruent.

- **Social proof.** We decide what is correct by looking at what others,
  especially similar others, are doing. Uncertainty and similarity amplify the
  effect ("most people in your situation chose this").

- **Liking.** We prefer to say yes to people we like. Liking is boosted by
  physical attractiveness, similarity, compliments, familiarity through repeated
  contact and cooperation, and association with good things.

- **Authority.** We defer to legitimate experts and to the symbols of
  authority — titles, clothing, credentials — often without evaluating the
  substance behind them.

- **Scarcity.** Opportunities seem more valuable as they become less available;
  the "limited quantity" and "deadline" tactics exploit our aversion to loss and
  our reaction against restricted freedom.

Later editions add a seventh principle, **unity** — the shared identity ("one of
us," family, place, tribe) that makes influence especially potent.

## Significance and cross-links

*Influence* is the most widely read applied treatment of [social
psychology](social-psychology.md), turning laboratory findings on conformity,
compliance, and attitude change into practical, ethically framed rules. Cialdini
stresses recognizing when the shortcuts are being exploited against us, so we can
refuse manipulation while still using the heuristics honestly. The principles
overlap heavily with the systematic-vs-heuristic reasoning and choice architecture
studied in [behavioral economics](../economics/behavioral-economics.md): both
fields describe how predictable mental shortcuts shape decisions under uncertainty.
