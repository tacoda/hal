# Psychology (David G. Myers & C. Nathan DeWall)

David Myers's *Psychology* — now co-authored with C. Nathan DeWall and in its
thirteenth-plus edition — is the best-selling introductory psychology textbook in
North America and the de facto standard survey of the field for undergraduates.
It is not an argument for a single theory; it is a **comprehensive, evidence-first
tour of the whole discipline**, organized to teach a beginner how psychologists
think before what they have concluded. Myers writes for the general student, and
the book is known for weaving critical-thinking habits and real-world application
throughout rather than treating them as an appendix.

## Scope

The book surveys, roughly in order, the field's major domains:

- **The story of psychology** — its history and the schools that shaped it.
- **Research strategies** — how psychologists ask and answer questions.
- **The biology of mind** — neurons, hormones, brain structures, the cortex.
- **Consciousness** — sleep, dreams, drugs, the two-track mind.
- **Nature, nurture, and human diversity** — behavior genetics, evolutionary
  psychology, culture and gender.
- **Development across the life span** — infancy through late adulthood.
- **Sensation and perception, learning, memory, thinking and language,
  intelligence, motivation and emotion.**
- **Personality, psychological disorders and therapy, social psychology.**

## Central pedagogical commitments

- **Science as the throughline.** The book opens by teaching the scientific
  attitude — curiosity, skepticism, humility — and returns to it constantly.
  Its early modules on research strategies and statistical reasoning make it a
  natural anchor for [research methods in psychology](research-methods-in-psychology.md):
  the case study, survey, and naturalistic observation for description;
  correlation for prediction; the experiment with random assignment for
  identifying cause; and statistical reasoning for judging what the data mean.

- **Critical thinking against intuition.** A recurring theme is how commonsense
  intuition (hindsight bias, overconfidence, perceiving patterns in randomness)
  misleads, and why controlled evidence is needed to correct it.

- **Breadth over depth.** As a survey it introduces every subfield rather than
  arguing any one in detail, making it the map from which HAL's other psychology
  notes descend — the [history and schools](history-and-schools-of-psychology.md),
  [learning and conditioning](learning-and-conditioning.md),
  [social psychology](social-psychology.md),
  [developmental psychology](developmental-psychology.md),
  [clinical and abnormal psychology](clinical-and-abnormal-psychology.md), and
  [personality](personality.md).

Because it covers the field end to end, this text is the entry point that frames
where every other canonical work in the folder fits.
