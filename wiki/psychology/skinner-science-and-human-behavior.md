# Science and Human Behavior (B. F. Skinner, 1953)

*Science and Human Behavior* is B. F. Skinner's systematic statement of **radical
behaviorism** and his most complete attempt to show that the concepts of the
experimental laboratory can explain the whole range of human conduct — not just a
rat pressing a lever, but language, thinking, self-control, government, religion,
economics, education, and culture. Written to serve as a general text, it argues
that behavior is lawful, that its causes lie in the environment and an
individual's history of consequences, and that a genuine science of behavior is
therefore both possible and useful for designing better social arrangements.

## Central ideas

- **Behavior is a function of its consequences.** Skinner's core unit is the
  **operant**: behavior that operates on the environment and is shaped by what
  follows it. Behavior followed by **reinforcement** grows more likely; behavior
  followed by nothing (or by aversive consequences) grows less likely. This is
  **operant conditioning**, distinct from the reflexive respondent conditioning
  of Pavlov and Watson, and it is developed in depth in the note on
  [learning and conditioning](learning-and-conditioning.md).

- **Prediction and control, not inner states.** Radical behaviorism does not deny
  that feelings and thoughts exist, but it refuses to treat them as *explanations*.
  A "mental state" is itself behavior (or a bodily condition) to be explained by
  environmental history, not a cause you can stop at. The goal of the science is
  to predict and control behavior by identifying the variables of which it is a
  function.

- **Reinforcement schedules.** *When* and *how often* reinforcement follows
  behavior — continuous vs. intermittent, ratio vs. interval — powerfully shapes
  how behavior is acquired, maintained, and extinguished. Intermittent schedules
  produce behavior highly resistant to extinction.

- **Shaping and complex behavior.** Elaborate behavior is built by reinforcing
  successive approximations toward a target, so that novel and intricate acts can
  emerge from simple conditioning principles.

- **Extending to the social.** The later half of the book applies these
  principles outward: the individual's self-control, then the controlling
  agencies of society — government and law, religion, economic control,
  education, psychotherapy, and the design of a culture. Aversive control
  (punishment) is analyzed critically; Skinner argues it is widespread but
  produces poor and unintended side effects compared with positive reinforcement.

## Significance

The book is a landmark in the [history and schools of
psychology](history-and-schools-of-psychology.md): it represents behaviorism at
its most ambitious, the dominant American paradigm before the cognitive
revolution reasserted the study of internal representation. Its analysis of
behavior as a function of consequences also underlies applied fields from
behavior therapy to instructional design, and its social-engineering themes
connect to Skinner's utopian novel *Walden Two*. The insistence on observable,
manipulable variables ties it to rigorous [research methods in
psychology](research-methods-in-psychology.md).
