# Personality

Personality is the characteristic, relatively enduring pattern of thinking, feeling, and
behaving that distinguishes one person from another and shows consistency across time and
situations. The field asks three linked questions: *what* is the structure of personality
(its dimensions), *why* it takes the form it does (its origins and dynamics), and *how* it
can be measured. Competing schools answer these very differently; they are surveyed in
[myers-psychology](myers-psychology.md).

## Trait theory and the Big Five

The **trait** approach describes personality as a set of stable dimensions on which people
vary continuously. Allport catalogued thousands of trait words; Cattell and Eysenck reduced
them with factor analysis. The modern consensus is the **Five-Factor Model (Big Five,
"OCEAN")**, derived from the *lexical hypothesis* — that important individual differences
get encoded in language — and recovered repeatedly across cultures:

| Trait | High pole | Low pole |
|---|---|---|
| **O**penness | Curious, imaginative, unconventional | Practical, conventional |
| **C**onscientiousness | Organized, disciplined, dependable | Careless, spontaneous |
| **E**xtraversion | Outgoing, energetic, assertive | Reserved, solitary |
| **A**greeableness | Cooperative, compassionate, trusting | Competitive, skeptical |
| **N**euroticism | Anxious, emotionally reactive | Calm, resilient (emotional stability) |

The Big Five are substantially **heritable** (~40–60%), fairly stable in adulthood while
still shifting predictably with age (conscientiousness and agreeableness tend to rise), and
**predictive**: conscientiousness forecasts job and academic performance and longevity;
neuroticism forecasts vulnerability to anxiety and depression (see
[clinical-and-abnormal-psychology](clinical-and-abnormal-psychology.md)). Traits describe
*what* a person is like without fully explaining *why*.

## Psychodynamic views

Freud's **psychoanalytic** theory framed personality as the dynamic conflict among the
**id** (drives, pleasure principle), **superego** (internalized morality), and **ego** (the
reality-negotiating mediator), much of it unconscious and defended by mechanisms like
repression. Neo-Freudians (Jung, Adler, Horney) de-emphasized sexuality and stressed social
and cultural motives. As a *scientific* theory much of Freud is unfalsifiable and poorly
supported, but psychodynamic thought left durable and now-validated ideas: that much mental
life is **unconscious**, that early relationships shape later ones (cf. attachment in
[developmental-psychology](developmental-psychology.md)), and that people defend against
threatening thoughts.

## Humanistic views

Reacting against both the determinism of psychoanalysis and the mechanism of behaviorism,
the **humanistic** school (Maslow, Rogers) centered growth, agency, and subjective
experience. Maslow's **hierarchy of needs** culminates in **self-actualization**; Rogers
held that people flourish given **unconditional positive regard** and that psychological
trouble arises from a gap between the real and ideal self. Humanistic psychology is the
direct ancestor of [positive-psychology-and-wellbeing](positive-psychology-and-wellbeing.md).

## The person–situation debate

In 1968 Walter Mischel challenged trait theory: behavior, he argued, is far more variable
across situations than "stable traits" imply — a person shy at work may be gregarious with
friends. The resolution is **interactionism**: traits predict *aggregate* behavior well
(averaged over many situations) even when single acts are situation-driven, and people
express traits through characteristic **if–then signatures** (this person becomes hostile
*when* criticized). Both dispositions and situations matter, which is the bridge to
[social-psychology](social-psychology.md), where the power of situations is the central
theme. A related theme is that some seemingly "trait-like" beliefs are malleable —
[../personal-development/mindset-dweck](../personal-development/mindset-dweck.md) argues that
whether people hold a *fixed* or *growth* mindset about their own abilities shapes behavior
as much as any stable disposition.

## Measurement

Personality is assessed by:

- **Self-report inventories** — standardized questionnaires (e.g. the NEO-PI, MMPI). Cheap
  and reliable, but vulnerable to social desirability and limited self-insight.
- **Projective tests** (Rorschach, TAT) — ambiguous stimuli meant to reveal unconscious
  content; low reliability and validity, largely out of favor.
- **Behavioral and informant reports** — observed conduct and ratings by people who know
  the person, which often predict outcomes better than self-report alone.

Good measurement demands **reliability** (consistency) and **validity** (measuring what it
claims), the standards developed in [research-methods-in-psychology](research-methods-in-psychology.md).

## Why it matters

Personality science informs clinical assessment, organizational selection and team design,
relationship compatibility, and self-understanding. Its central lesson — that people are
neither wholly fixed nor infinitely malleable — tempers both fatalism and naïve optimism
about change, and it anchors to the broader survey in [myers-psychology](myers-psychology.md).
