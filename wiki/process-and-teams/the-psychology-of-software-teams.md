# The Psychology of Software Teams

**Cat Hicks** (Routledge/CRC, 2027). A behavioral-science account of what actually makes software
teams resilient, sustainable, and innovative — grounded in empirical evidence from thousands of
developers and engineering managers across 12+ industries, not opinion. Hicks is a psychological
scientist (PhD, Quantitative Experimental Psychology, UC San Diego) and founder of the consultancy
Catharsis; her aim is to open the "black box" of software engineering with real evidence. Charity
Majors calls it *"the Accelerate of the agentic era"* — the point being opinions **backed by
research.**

## The through-line

The real technical risk isn't a shortage of brilliance — it's the **psychological gap** orgs
create when they obsess over short-term individual performance and treat culture as "soft." As
Scott Hanselman puts it, psychological safety, learning, and collaboration **aren't perks, they're
infrastructure**; ignore them and systems fail, slowly or catastrophically. The book's job is to
untangle developer work from pervasive myths and replace them with humane, evidence-based
practice — a direct descendant of [Peopleware](peopleware.md) and [team dynamics &
psychological safety](team-dynamics-and-psychological-safety.md).

## The argument, chapter by chapter

1. **The Brains and Hearts That Build the World** — software is built by whole humans, not
   disembodied intellects; the case for bringing the human back to tech.
2. **Breaking Up with Brains-in-Jars** — rejects the *"brains in jars"* model that treats
   developers as pure cognition detached from social, emotional, and environmental context. That
   framing is what enables toxic environments.
3. **More Bands and Fewer Rockstars** — the receipts that "nuke the 10x-developer and l33tcode
   myths from orbit." Innovation is **social learning**, not solitary genius; over-relying on one
   "rockstar" breeds burnout and bus-factor risk. Build **bands**, not stars.
4. **The Performance Paradox** — chasing short-term performance undermines the learning that
   produces long-term performance. A **performance** orientation crowds out a **learning**
   orientation, so the metric-obsessed team gets slower and more fragile, not faster.
5. **Conflict or Coalitions?** — how teams handle disagreement: productive conflict and coalitions
   for problem-solving vs. the corrosive kind.
6. **Becoming an Organization That Wants to Understand Itself** — orgs that seek honest evidence
   about their own work (human-centered, open-science measurement) instead of flattering vanity
   metrics. Kin to [continuous improvement & retrospectives](continuous-improvement-and-retrospectives.md).
7. **Fighting Dirty for Good Culture** — good culture doesn't survive by default; it must be
   actively defended. Concrete moves for protecting learning, safety, and collaboration.

## Core ideas to carry

- **Brains-in-jars is the enemy.** Developer capability is embodied and social; strip the context
  and you mis-model the work.
- **Social learning > lone genius.** Cognitive scaffolding and thriving *ecosystems* produce
  innovation; the "10x developer" is a myth that misallocates trust and risk.
- **The performance paradox.** Optimizing visible short-term output degrades the learning that
  drives durable results — measure and reward accordingly.
- **Culture is infrastructure.** Psychological safety and collaboration are load-bearing, not
  decorative; treat them like systems you invest in and defend.
