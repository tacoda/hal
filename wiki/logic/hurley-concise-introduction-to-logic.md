# A Concise Introduction to Logic (Hurley)

Patrick J. Hurley's *A Concise Introduction to Logic* is the most widely adopted
undergraduate logic textbook in North America. It is deliberately comprehensive: a
single volume that walks a first-time student from the everyday notion of an argument,
through the classical Aristotelian machinery of the syllogism, and into the symbolic
apparatus of modern propositional and predicate logic. Its reputation rests on breadth
and pedagogy rather than on any single technical result — it is a survey of the whole
discipline pitched at people who have never seen a truth table.

## Scope and approach

The book is organized around one governing distinction: **deductive** versus
**inductive** arguments, and the twin virtues of **validity** (does the conclusion follow
of necessity?) and **soundness** (is it valid *and* are its premises true?). Everything
else is machinery for testing those properties. The material falls into three broad
movements.

- **Informal logic.** The opening part teaches argument recognition — separating premises
  from conclusions, distinguishing arguments from mere explanations or assertions,
  diagramming argument structure, and evaluating natural-language reasoning. This is the
  domain of [informal-logic-and-argumentation.md](informal-logic-and-argumentation.md).
  It culminates in a large, carefully taxonomized treatment of informal
  [fallacies](fallacies.md) — fallacies of relevance (ad hominem, appeal to force,
  straw man), of weak induction (hasty generalization, false cause), and of presumption,
  ambiguity, and grammatical analogy. Hurley's fallacy catalogue is one of the most-cited
  in teaching.

- **Categorical / syllogistic logic.** The middle movement is classical term logic:
  categorical propositions (A, E, I, O forms), the traditional square of opposition,
  conversion/obversion/contraposition, Venn diagrams, and the rules and moods of the
  categorical syllogism. This is logic in the Aristotelian tradition — reasoning about
  classes and quantities ("all," "no," "some") — and it is the historical bridge to the
  modern quantifier.

- **Modern symbolic logic.** The final movement introduces truth-functional
  [propositional logic](propositional-logic.md) — the connectives, truth tables, natural
  deduction, and the notion of a formal proof — and then extends it to
  [predicate logic](predicate-logic.md) with quantifiers and relations, closing the loop
  with the Aristotelian material by re-expressing categorical claims in quantified form.

## Why it matters

Hurley's value is that it treats logic as a *unified* subject rather than picking a camp.
A reader finishes with both the "soft" skills of argument analysis (spotting a fallacy in
a newspaper op-ed) and the "hard" skills of formal derivation (constructing a proof in a
[formal system](formal-systems-and-proof-theory.md)). For HAL it anchors the informal end
of the logic folder and the historical categorical tradition, complementing the more
mathematical treatments elsewhere. It pairs naturally with the general treatment in
[../math/mathematical-proof-and-logic.md](../math/mathematical-proof-and-logic.md) and
underlies the reasoning discipline that recurs throughout
