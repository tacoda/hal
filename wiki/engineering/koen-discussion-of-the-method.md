# Discussion of the Method: Conducting the Engineer's Approach to Problem Solving

Billy Vaughn Koen — Professor of Mechanical Engineering at UT Austin — wrote this 2003
Oxford University Press book (an expansion of his earlier *Definition of the Engineering
Method*) to answer a deceptively simple question: **what, exactly, is the engineering
method?** His answer is a single sustained thesis. The engineering method is *the use of
heuristics to cause the best change in a poorly understood situation within the available
resources.* The book's title deliberately echoes Descartes' *Discourse on the Method* —
and then argues that engineering, not deduction, is the more honest account of how humans
actually solve problems.

## Central ideas

**Engineering is the strategic use of heuristics.** A heuristic, for Koen, is anything
that provides a plausible aid or direction toward the solution of a problem but is in the
final analysis unjustified, incapable of justification, and potentially fallible. It
signals only provisional use. Rules of thumb, safety factors, "make it strong enough and
then double it," design codes, standard practices, even the choice of which method to try
next — all are heuristics. Engineering is not the application of certain scientific truth;
it is the disciplined management of a *toolbox of fallible heuristics* under constraint.

**The state of the art (sota) is the heuristic set.** At any moment an engineer's
practice is defined by the collection of heuristics currently at their disposal — the
sota. Engineering advances not by discovering laws but by improving this heuristic set:
adding better rules of thumb, discarding ones that failed, and refining judgment about
which heuristic fits which situation. Two engineers disagreeing is often just two
different sotas meeting.

**"Best change," "resources," "poorly understood."** Each phrase in the definition does
load-bearing work. *Best* is relative to the client, the era, and the values in play — not
absolute. *Available resources* — time, money, knowledge, materials — are always finite
and always bind; the engineer optimizes within them rather than seeking a perfect answer.
*Poorly understood* is the normal condition: if the situation were fully understood and
had a provably correct solution, it would be mathematics or science, not engineering.

**A universal method, not just an engineering one.** Koen's boldest move is to generalize.
He argues that *all* human problem-solving — medicine, law, everyday life, even science
itself in its actual practice — is heuristic in this same sense. There is no privileged,
certainty-yielding "scientific method" underneath; there is only the engineering method
applied everywhere. This dissolves the usual hierarchy that places pure science above
applied engineering.

## Why it anchors the engineering field

*Discussion of the Method* is the canonical philosophical statement of what
[the engineering method](the-engineering-method.md) *is* — the heuristic-and-sota framing
is the reference definition that concept note builds on. It also gives the sharpest
account of [design under constraints](design-under-constraints.md): the phrase "within
the available resources" is not incidental but constitutive — an engineering solution that
ignores its resource envelope is, by Koen's definition, not an engineering solution at
all. Safety factors, design margins, and standard codes are all understood here as
heuristics whose provisional, fallible status must be respected — a point that connects to
learning from failure and to the humility demanded when systems are only partly understood
(see [how complex systems fail](../systems-thinking/how-complex-systems-fail.md)).
