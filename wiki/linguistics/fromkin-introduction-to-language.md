# An Introduction to Language

The standard undergraduate survey of linguistics, first published in 1974 by Victoria
Fromkin and Robert Rodman and continued in later editions with Nina Hyams. It is the
book most English-speaking students meet first when they take an intro linguistics
course: a single-volume tour that moves level by level through the architecture of human
language, written for readers with no prior background. Its enduring appeal is pedagogical
— dense with cross-linguistic examples, puzzles, cartoons, and exercises — rather than
theoretical novelty. It presents the mainstream generative-influenced consensus of the
discipline as settled knowledge to be learned, not argued.

## What it covers

The book is organized around the idea that language is a **rule-governed mental system**,
not a list of memorized utterances, and that a speaker's unconscious knowledge of these
rules (their *linguistic competence*) is what a grammar tries to describe. From that
premise it walks the classical levels of analysis:

- **Sounds** — [phonetics](phonetics-and-phonology.md): how speech sounds are produced
  (articulation), the IPA, and the acoustic/perceptual side; then **phonology**: how a
  language organizes those sounds into a system of contrastive phonemes and predictable
  allophones governed by phonological rules.
- **Words** — [morphology](morphology.md): morphemes as the minimal units of meaning,
  the distinction between roots, affixes, derivation and inflection, and how words are
  built and analyzed.
- **Sentences** — [syntax](syntax.md): constituency, phrase-structure rules, tree
  diagrams, and the argument that a finite grammar generates an infinite set of
  well-formed sentences (recursion). This is where the book's generative heritage is most
  visible.
- **Meaning** — [semantics](semantics.md): lexical and sentential meaning, sense vs.
  reference, thematic roles, ambiguity, and the beginnings of pragmatics and how context
  shapes interpretation.

Surrounding these core chapters, the book treats the broader life of language: how
children acquire it ([language acquisition](language-acquisition.md)), how it lives in the
brain (neurolinguistics, aphasia), how it changes over time and splits into families
(historical linguistics), sociolinguistic variation and dialect, writing systems, and —
increasingly in recent editions — computers and language, which connects to
[computational linguistics and NLP](computational-linguistics-and-nlp.md).

## Why it matters

*An Introduction to Language* is the shared vocabulary of the field. It fixes the standard
terms (phoneme, morpheme, constituent, competence vs. performance) and the standard mental
map — sound → word → sentence → meaning → use — that later, more specialized study
assumes. Its framing of language as an innate, rule-governed capacity reflects the
post-Chomsky mainstream; students who go on to read *Syntactic Structures* or
*The Language Instinct* find those works filling in the theory this survey introduces.
