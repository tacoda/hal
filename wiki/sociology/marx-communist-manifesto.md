# The Communist Manifesto

Written by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels and published in 1848 on the eve of that year's
European revolutions, the *Manifesto of the Communist Party* is a short political pamphlet that
nonetheless laid down analytical ideas central to sociology. Beneath its revolutionary program
lies a theory of how societies are structured, how they change, and where their conflicts come
from — the seed of what sociologists call **conflict theory**.

## Class struggle as the motor of history

The famous opening thesis is that "the history of all hitherto existing society is the history
of class struggles." Every mode of production, Marx and Engels argue, divides society into
antagonistic classes defined by their relation to the **means of production** — freeman and
slave, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, and now **bourgeoisie** (owners of capital)
and **proletariat** (wage laborers who own only their labor power). Social order is not consensus
(as Durkheim would later emphasize) but a temporary settlement of a structural conflict of
interest. This makes the Manifesto a foundational statement of stratification as *domination
and exploitation* rather than mere ranking — see
[social stratification and inequality](social-stratification-and-inequality.md).

## Historical materialism

The Manifesto popularizes **historical materialism**: the claim that the economic base — the
forces and relations of production — shapes the political, legal, and ideological
"superstructure" of a society, and that changes in the base drive historical transformation.
The bourgeoisie itself, Marx notes with genuine admiration, is a revolutionary class: it
shattered feudal relations, created the world market, and "constantly revolutionizing the
instruments of production" dissolved all fixed traditions — "all that is solid melts into air."
But the same dynamism, they argue, produces crises of overproduction and concentrates an
ever-larger, ever-more-organized proletariat that will eventually overturn it.

## Alienation, ideology, and revolution

Modern industry, the authors contend, strips work of craft and meaning, reducing the worker to
"an appendage of the machine" and turning human relations into "naked, shameless, direct, brutal"
cash exchange. The prevailing ideas of any age are the ideas of its ruling class — an early
statement of **ideology** as a force that legitimates the existing order. The pamphlet closes
with a program (abolition of private property in the means of production, a transitional
proletarian state) and the rallying cry, "Working men of all countries, unite!"

## Significance for sociology

Stripped of its politics, the Manifesto contributes durable analytical tools: the primacy of
material and economic conditions, the conception of society as riven by structured conflict, and
the idea that beliefs and institutions serve class interests. Alongside Durkheim's functionalism
and Weber's interpretive sociology, Marxist thought is one of the three great traditions of
classical [sociological theory](sociological-theory.md), and the wellspring of conflict-oriented
analysis across the discipline. For the economic framework it critiques and extends, see
[../economics/index.md](../economics/index.md).
