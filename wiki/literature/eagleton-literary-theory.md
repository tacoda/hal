# Terry Eagleton — Literary Theory: An Introduction

Terry Eagleton's *Literary Theory: An Introduction* (1983; revised 1996, with an
anniversary edition in 2008) is the standard survey of twentieth-century
[literary theory and criticism](literary-theory-and-criticism.md) in the English-speaking
world. Clear, polemical, and unusually readable for its subject, it has served as the
introduction of choice for students entering the field for four decades. Eagleton writes
from an avowedly **Marxist** standpoint, so the book is both a lucid map of the major
schools and an argument about what theory is *for*.

## The opening move: "What is literature?"

Eagleton's introduction, "What Is Literature?", is a small classic in its own right and a
direct engagement with the question posed in [what is literature](what-is-literature.md).
He runs through and rejects the usual definitions — literature as *fiction* (much
non-fiction reads as literature), as *imaginative* writing, as *foregrounded* or
specially-organized language (the Formalist view), as writing that is non-pragmatic — and
concludes that there is **no stable, essential property** that makes a text "literary."
"Literature," he argues, is a **functional and value-laden category**: it is whatever a
society, at a given moment and through its institutions, decides to treat as valuable
writing. The [canon](the-canon-and-world-literature.md), on this view, is not a discovery
of intrinsic greatness but a construction that reflects — and conceals — the ideology and
power relations of those who make it. This anti-essentialist claim frames the whole book.

## The survey of theory

The core chapters walk through the major twentieth-century movements, treating each
sympathetically before locating its blind spots:

- **The rise of English** — how "English literature" became an academic and quasi-religious
  discipline in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and whose interests it served.
- **Phenomenology, hermeneutics, and reception theory** — Husserl, Heidegger, Gadamer, and
  reader-response criticism (Iser, Jauss).
- **Structuralism and semiotics** — Saussure, Jakobson, and the structuralist project of
  reading literature as a system of signs, connected to
  [narrative and narratology](narrative-and-narratology.md).
- **Post-structuralism** — Derrida's deconstruction, Barthes, and the destabilizing of
  fixed meaning.
- **Psychoanalysis** — Freud and Lacan on desire, the unconscious, and the subject.

## The political conclusion

Eagleton's closing chapter, "Conclusion: Political Criticism," delivers the book's thesis:
that all criticism is *already* political, whether or not it admits it, because it operates
within institutions and ideologies that decide what counts as literature and how it should
be read. "Pure" literary theory, disconnected from social power, is an illusion. He
advocates a criticism openly attentive to gender, class, and power — pointing toward
feminism, and what would become cultural studies and postcolonial criticism.

## Significance and critique

The book democratized literary theory, making structuralism, deconstruction, and
psychoanalytic criticism accessible to non-specialists at the moment "theory" was
transforming the humanities. Its lasting contributions are the anti-essentialist account
of "literature" and the insistence that criticism is never ideologically neutral.
Critics note its evident bias — it is a partisan introduction that channels every school
toward a Marxist conclusion — and that its coverage stops short of later developments
(postcolonialism, ecocriticism, queer theory) which the revised editions and afterwords
only gesture at. It remains, deliberately, an argument as much as a textbook.
