# Harold Bloom — The Western Canon

*The Western Canon: The Books and School of the Ages* (1994) by the American critic
Harold Bloom is a passionate, combative defense of the literary
[canon](the-canon-and-world-literature.md) and of **aesthetic value** as the proper
basis for judging literature. Written against the grain of the academy of its day, it
argues that great books earn their place not through the identity of their authors or
their political usefulness but through a quality Bloom calls **"canonical strangeness"** —
an originality so powerful a work seems both utterly new and permanently familiar.

## The argument: aesthetic autonomy and canonical strangeness

Bloom's core claim is that literature is an **autonomous aesthetic** enterprise whose value
lies in the reader's solitary encounter with sublime and difficult works — not in social
edification. Canonical works, he says, are marked by *strangeness*: a strangeness of
vision and language that resists assimilation, so that a reader is changed by them rather
than merely confirmed. This connects to the question of [what is literature](what-is-literature.md):
for Bloom, literariness is real, intrinsic, and rooted in imaginative power, directly
opposing the anti-essentialist view that "literature" is merely whatever an institution
values. His model of literary history is the **agon** — the "anxiety of influence" from
his earlier work — in which strong writers wrestle with and misread their great
predecessors. All roads in his canon lead to and from **Shakespeare**, whom Bloom treats
as the center of the Western imagination and the inventor of recognizably modern human
inwardness.

## Structure and the list

Bloom organizes the book around **26 authors** he considers central — beginning with
Shakespeare and Dante and running through Chaucer, Cervantes, Montaigne, Milton, Goethe,
Wordsworth, Austen, Whitman, Dickinson, Tolstoy, Ibsen, Freud, Proust, Joyce, Woolf,
Kafka, Borges, Neruda, and Beckett, among others. He frames literary history in four
Vichian ages — Theocratic, Aristocratic, Democratic, and Chaotic — a scheme that overlaps
with [literary periods and movements](literary-periods-and-movements.md). The book also
carries four **appendices** listing hundreds of canonical works from ancient scripture to
Tony Kushner's *Angels in America*. Bloom later **disowned the list**, saying he compiled
it only at his editor's insistence and that it distracted from the book's real argument.

## The "School of Resentment"

Bloom's most quoted (and most polemical) coinage is the **"School of Resentment"** — his
label for the schools of criticism that rose to dominance in the academy after the 1970s:
Marxist, feminist, New Historicist, Lacanian, deconstructionist, and semiotic approaches,
along with movements to expand the canon on grounds of representation. Bloom charges them
with subordinating aesthetic judgment to political and social activism — reading
literature *for* ideology rather than *as* art. This makes the book a central document in
the "canon wars" and a direct antagonist to the ideological view of criticism argued by
figures like Terry Eagleton (see [Eagleton, *Literary Theory*](eagleton-literary-theory.md)).

## Significance and critique

*The Western Canon* was a bestseller and became the emblematic defense of aesthetic
value and the traditional canon in the culture wars of the 1990s. Its enduring
contribution is the forceful case that literary greatness is a real phenomenon worth
defending, and that reading is first a personal, transformative aesthetic experience.

The critiques are equally well known. Bloom's canon is overwhelmingly
white, male, and European, and his dismissal of historical and political criticism as mere
"resentment" strikes many as a caricature that avoids the substance of those arguments —
that canons *are* shaped by power and exclusion, a point about
[the canon and world literature](the-canon-and-world-literature.md) that Bloom largely
refuses to concede. His notion of "aesthetic autonomy" is itself a contestable theoretical
position, not a neutral fact. The book is best read as a brilliant, deliberately
one-sided polemic — the strongest statement of one side of an argument criticism has not
settled.
