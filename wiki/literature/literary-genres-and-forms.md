# Literary Genres and Forms

A **genre** is a category of literary work defined by shared conventions — of form,
subject, tone, and structure. Genres are not a filing system imposed after the fact;
they are a contract between writer and reader. When you know a text is a sonnet, a
tragedy, or a detective novel, you arrive with expectations that shape how you read
it — and skilled writers exploit, bend, and break those expectations for effect.
Genre is thus best understood as **expectation and convention**, not as a rigid
taxonomy.

## The three classical modes

Western genre theory descends from a three-part division, present in embryo in
Aristotle's *Poetics* ([aristotle-poetics.md](aristotle-poetics.md)) and systematized
much later. It is organized by the *mode of presentation* — who is speaking:

| Mode | How it presents | Speaker | Core forms |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lyric / poetry** | A speaker voices thought or feeling directly | The poet's persona | Ode, elegy, sonnet, lyric |
| **Drama** | Characters act and speak; no narrator | The characters themselves | Tragedy, comedy, history |
| **Epic / narrative (prose fiction)** | A narrator tells of events | A narrator | Epic, novel, short story |

Plato and Aristotle distinguished *diegesis* (telling, the narrator's own voice) from
*mimesis* (showing, imitation through characters); lyric, drama, and epic can be seen
as different mixtures of the two. Aristotle's own focus was tragedy, whose anatomy —
plot (*mythos*), character, thought, diction, spectacle, song — became the template for
thinking about dramatic and narrative structure (see [narrative-and-narratology.md](narrative-and-narratology.md)).

Northrop Frye's *Anatomy of Criticism* ([frye-anatomy-of-criticism.md](frye-anatomy-of-criticism.md))
gave the most ambitious modern systematization, sorting literature by *mode* (the
hero's power relative to us), by *myth/season*, and by genre, and insisting that
criticism could be a coherent science of forms rather than a series of value-judgments.

## The rise of the novel

The **novel** — extended prose fiction — is the dominant literary form of the modern
era, and its rise is a historical event, not a timeless given. Emerging in force in
18th-century Europe (Defoe, Richardson, Fielding), it is bound up with the growth of a
literate middle class, print capitalism, and a new interest in the private,
psychological, everyday life of ordinary individuals — what Ian Watt called
**formal realism** and Auerbach's *Mimesis* ([auerbach-mimesis.md](auerbach-mimesis.md))
traced as the widening representation of ordinary reality across Western literature.
The novel is notoriously capacious: it absorbs letters, journalism, dialogue, and
other genres, which is why it resists tidy definition. Its history is a thread through
[literary-periods-and-movements.md](literary-periods-and-movements.md) and it is
deeply social ([literature-and-society.md](literature-and-society.md)).

## The short story, the essay, and nonfiction

- **Short story.** Not merely a small novel: its brevity is a formal principle. Poe
  argued for the "single effect" a story achieves in one sitting; the modern short
  story (Chekhov, Joyce) trades plot resolution for the compressed, revealing moment —
  Joyce's **epiphany**.
- **The essay.** Invented as a self-conscious form by Montaigne (*essai* = "attempt"):
  the exploratory, personal, argumentative prose of a mind thinking on the page. It is
  the clearest case that literariness does not require fiction (see
  [what-is-literature.md](what-is-literature.md)), and it is where literary study meets
  [rhetoric-and-style.md](rhetoric-and-style.md) most directly.
- **Literary nonfiction.** Memoir, travel writing, the "nonfiction novel," and reportage
  apply the techniques of narrative and figuration
  ([literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md](literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md))
  to factual material — further eroding the fiction/literature equation.

## Poetry as a genre-family

**Poetry** is treated in depth in [poetry-and-poetics.md](poetry-and-poetics.md); as a
genre it is marked less by subject than by its heightened attention to the line, to
sound, and to compression — the most concentrated instance of the "literary language"
described in [what-is-literature.md](what-is-literature.md).

## Genre as a living system

Genres mix (*tragicomedy*), split (the novel spawning the detective, the gothic, the
bildungsroman), and migrate. **Subgenres** encode ever-finer conventions; **hybrid**
and **metafictional** works announce and subvert their own genre. Because genres are
expectation, they carry ideology and history: what counts as a "serious" genre versus
a "popular" one is a value-judgment, continuous with the high/popular debate in
[what-is-literature.md](what-is-literature.md) and the canon question in
[the-canon-and-world-literature.md](the-canon-and-world-literature.md). Reading well
means recognizing the genre contract a text signs — and noticing exactly where it
violates it.
