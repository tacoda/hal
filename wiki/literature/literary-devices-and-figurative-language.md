# Literary Devices and Figurative Language

Literary devices are the working parts of literary craft — the patterned uses of
language through which a text produces meaning, emphasis, and pleasure beyond plain
statement. **Figurative language** is the largest family among them: language that
means something other than, or more than, what its words literally denote. Learning the
toolkit is the entry fee to [close reading and interpretation](close-reading-and-interpretation.md),
and the devices themselves are the raw material that [literary theory](literary-theory-and-criticism.md)
argues over.

## Literal vs. figurative meaning

The bedrock distinction. **Literal** meaning is the conventional, dictionary sense of a
word applied to its ordinary referent ("the road was long"). **Figurative** meaning
arises when words are turned — the Greek root of *trope* is "a turn" — to point at
something they do not literally name ("the road was a ribbon of moonlight"). Figuration
is not decoration bolted onto plain thought; it is often the only way to say a thing,
which is why the study of how words attach to meaning belongs as much to
[the philosophy of language](../philosophy/philosophy-of-language.md) and
[semantics](../linguistics/semantics.md) as to literary study. A dead metaphor ("the leg
of a table") has cooled into literal usage; a live one still asks the reader to compute a
resemblance.

## The core tropes

- **Metaphor** — an implicit identification of one thing with another
  ("time is a thief"). Analysts distinguish the **tenor** (the subject, *time*) from the
  **vehicle** (the image carrying meaning to it, *thief*); the meaning lives in the
  tension between them.
- **Simile** — an explicit comparison flagged by *like* or *as* ("brave as a lion").
  Weaker identification than metaphor, but often more precise.
- **Metonymy** — naming a thing by something closely associated with it ("the *crown*
  ruled for forty years" = the monarchy; "the *White House* announced").
- **Synecdoche** — a special metonymy where a part stands for the whole or the whole for
  a part ("all hands on deck"; "*wheels*" for a car). Structuralist critics such as
  Roman Jakobson treated metaphor (substitution by similarity) and metonymy (substitution
  by contiguity) as the two master axes of all language.
- **Symbol** — a concrete image that stands, stably and across a whole work, for an
  abstract idea (the green light in *The Great Gatsby*). Unlike a one-off metaphor, a
  symbol accretes meaning through repetition.
- **Allegory** — an extended, systematic symbolism in which characters and events map
  onto a second, usually moral or political, level of meaning (*Animal Farm*, *Pilgrim's
  Progress*).

## Irony

**Irony** is the gap between what is said and what is meant, or between expectation and
outcome.

| Type | The gap |
| --- | --- |
| Verbal | Between the literal words and the speaker's intent (sarcasm is the crude case) |
| Dramatic | Between what a character knows and what the audience knows (Oedipus hunting himself) |
| Situational | Between expected and actual outcome (a fire station burns down) |

Irony is also the pivot of much [literary theory](literary-theory-and-criticism.md): the
New Critics prized it as a sign of a poem's complexity, while deconstruction pushed it
toward the claim that all language is ironized against itself.

## The wider toolkit

- **Imagery** — language that evokes sensory experience (visual, auditory, tactile,
  etc.); the sensory ground on which most figuration is built.
- **Personification** — attributing human qualities to nonhuman things ("the wind
  *sighed*"); a specialized metaphor.
- **Allusion** — a reference to another text, figure, or event, borrowing its resonance
  ("a Herculean task"). Allusion assumes a shared canon, which is why it links to
  [the canon and world literature](the-canon-and-world-literature.md).
- **Motif** — a recurring image, phrase, or situation that threads through a work and
  accumulates significance; motifs organize meaning at the level of
  [narrative](narrative-and-narratology.md), not just the line.

## Why it matters

Devices are not a checklist to spot and tally. Their point is *effect*: what does the
metaphor let the writer say that plain statement could not, and how does it steer the
reader's response? Naming a device is the beginning of analysis, not the end — the
interpretive work is showing what the device *does*. These tools are densest in
[poetry and poetics](poetry-and-poetics.md), where compression forces every word to
carry figurative weight, but they run through all prose and, in the persuasive register,
through [rhetoric and style](rhetoric-and-style.md). Northrop Frye's
[Anatomy of Criticism](frye-anatomy-of-criticism.md) treated the recurring images and
symbols of literature as a systematic, almost grammatical order rather than a bag of
tricks.
