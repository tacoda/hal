# Erich Auerbach — Mimesis: The Representation of Reality in Western Literature

*Mimesis* (written 1942–45, published in German in 1946; English translation by Willard
Trask, 1953) is Erich Auerbach's sweeping study of how Western literature has represented
everyday reality across roughly three millennia, from Homer and the Hebrew Bible to
Virginia Woolf. It is widely regarded as one of the single greatest works of
[literary criticism](literary-theory-and-criticism.md) of the twentieth century and a
foundational text of comparative literature and the discipline of *philology* (close,
historically grounded reading of texts in their original languages).

## Method: reading from the passage outward

Auerbach's approach is deliberately anti-systematic. Each of the book's twenty chapters
opens with a **single quoted passage** — a scene from Homer, Petronius, Dante, Rabelais,
Montaigne, Shakespeare, Cervantes, Stendhal, Balzac, Flaubert, Woolf, and others — which he
reads closely for its style, syntax, and rhetorical texture, then radiates outward into
claims about the culture and worldview that produced it. Famously, the book contains **no
footnotes**: Auerbach wrote it in exile in Istanbul as a German Jew driven from his
Marburg professorship in 1935, cut off from research libraries. The constraint became a
method — argument grounded in the texts themselves rather than in secondary scholarship.

## The central argument: styles and the rise of realism

The opening chapter, "Odysseus' Scar," contrasts two foundational modes of representation.
Homer's style is **fully externalized**: everything is illuminated in the foreground,
uniformly present, leaving nothing hidden or unexplained. The Hebrew Bible's account of
Abraham and Isaac is the opposite — sparse, "fraught with background," psychologically
deep, demanding interpretation. These become the twin poles from which Western realism
develops.

Auerbach then traces two intertwined threads across the tradition:

- The breaking-down of the classical **doctrine of the separation of styles** (*Stiltrennung*),
  which reserved the elevated tragic style for noble subjects and the low comic style for
  ordinary or base ones. He argues that literature progresses, unevenly, toward
  representing common people and everyday life with full seriousness — a movement he ties
  to Christianity's *sermo humilis* (the humble style capable of expressing the sublime,
  as in the Gospels) and culminating in the nineteenth-century realism of Stendhal, Balzac,
  and Flaubert.
- **Figural realism** — the medieval Christian habit (crystallized in Dante) of reading
  earthly events as both concretely real *and* prefigurations of a divine order — which
  Auerbach sees as uniquely enabling an intense, individualized depiction of reality.

The overall arc is an optimistic, humanist narrative: Western literature tends toward
ever more **democratic and naturalistic** representation, dignifying ordinary human
experience. This makes *Mimesis* a study in [literary periods and movements](literary-periods-and-movements.md)
— antiquity, the medieval Christian world, the Renaissance, and modern realism each get a
distinct stylistic signature — read through the lens of style rather than of literary
schools.

## Significance and critique

*Mimesis* shaped comparative literature as a discipline, modeled the practice of
philological close reading for generations, and stands with Auerbach's essay "Figura" as
his most enduring contribution. Written in the shadow of Nazism, its affirmation of a
shared, humane European literary culture is also a quiet act of resistance against
nationalism and chauvinism. Critics have questioned its selective, chapter-by-chapter
construction (does a chain of brilliant close readings amount to a continuous history?),
its Eurocentric frame, and the teleology of its "progress toward realism." Edward Said,
who wrote the introduction to the Princeton Classics edition, read the book precisely
through Auerbach's condition of exile — the outsider's vantage that made such a synthesis
both possible and poignant.
