# Poetry and Poetics

**Poetry** is language organized for maximum concentration — of sound, rhythm, image,
and meaning packed into the smallest space. **Poetics** is the study of how it achieves
that concentration: the technical anatomy of the poem. Where prose looks *through*
language to its content, poetry makes us attend to language *as* language — the most
concentrated case of the "literary" use of words discussed in
[what-is-literature.md](what-is-literature.md), and the genre-family surveyed in
[literary-genres-and-forms.md](literary-genres-and-forms.md). Poetry's raw materials
are physical: it works on the ear and the body before the intellect, which is why the
sound system studied in [../linguistics/phonetics-and-phonology.md](../linguistics/phonetics-and-phonology.md)
is not decoration but structure.

## Meter and rhythm (scansion)

**Rhythm** is the felt pattern of stress in any speech; **meter** is rhythm regularized
into a repeating measure. **Scansion** is the act of marking a line's pattern of
stressed (´) and unstressed (˘) syllables. In English — a stress-timed language — the
unit is the **foot**:

| Foot | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Iamb | ˘ ´ | *a-**bove*** |
| Trochee | ´ ˘ | ***gar**-den* |
| Anapest | ˘ ˘ ´ | *in-ter-**vene*** |
| Dactyl | ´ ˘ ˘ | ***mer**-ri-ly* |
| Spondee | ´ ´ | ***heart**-**break*** |

Line length is named by the count of feet: *monometer* (1), *dimeter* (2), *trimeter*
(3), *tetrameter* (4), *pentameter* (5), *hexameter* (6). **Iambic pentameter** — five
iambs, ten syllables — is the workhorse of English verse (Shakespeare, Milton). The art
lies in the *tension* between the abstract metrical grid and the actual rhythm of the
words: a spondee or a reversed foot at a key moment lands because it breaks the
expectation the meter has built. This is why scansion matters — it makes the departures
visible.

## Sound: rhyme and its relatives

Rhyme is only one of poetry's sound devices; they bind words by echo and are catalogued
alongside other figures in [literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md](literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md):

- **Rhyme** — matching stressed-vowel-and-after sounds (*light / night*); *slant* or
  *near* rhyme (*eyes / light*) for tension; *internal* rhyme within a line.
- **Alliteration** — repeated initial consonants (*"the **s**oft **s**inking **s**un"*).
- **Assonance** — repeated vowel sounds; **consonance** — repeated consonants.
- **Onomatopoeia** — sound imitating sense (*buzz, hush*).

A **rhyme scheme** (notated *abab*, *aabb*, etc.) organizes rhyme into a pattern that
helps define a form. All of these are effects on the phoneme, tying poetics directly to
[../linguistics/phonetics-and-phonology.md](../linguistics/phonetics-and-phonology.md).

## Form

A poetic **form** is a fixed template of length, meter, rhyme, and stanza:

- **Sonnet** — 14 lines of iambic pentameter. *Petrarchan* (octave + sestet, *abbaabba
  cdecde*, with a turn or **volta** between) or *Shakespearean* (three quatrains + a
  couplet, *abab cdcd efef gg*).
- **Villanelle** — 19 lines, two refrains woven through five tercets and a quatrain
  (Dylan Thomas, "Do not go gentle").
- **Haiku** — three lines, 5-7-5 syllables in the Japanese tradition, an image and a
  seasonal turn.
- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter; the meter of English epic and drama.
- **Free verse** — no fixed meter or rhyme; rhythm and lineation are shaped to each
  poem (Whitman, Williams). Free is not *formless*: it trades inherited constraint for
  invented, poem-specific structure.

## The image

The **image** is a concrete sensory representation — the thing shown, not the idea
stated. Ezra Pound's **Imagist** creed ("the direct treatment of the thing," no wasted
word) made the image poetry's central unit of meaning. An image becomes a **symbol**
when it carries meaning beyond itself, and a **conceit** when an image is elaborated
into an extended, often surprising comparison. Figurative resources — metaphor,
metonymy, personification — are shared with all literary language and treated in
[literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md](literary-devices-and-figurative-language.md);
in poetry they are load-bearing.

## The line

The **line** is what most distinguishes poetry from prose. The poet chooses where each
line ends, and that choice is meaning:

- **End-stopped** line — a grammatical pause coincides with the line break.
- **Enjambment** — the sense runs over the break, creating momentum, surprise, or a
  double reading of the line-final word.
- The **stanza** groups lines (couplet, tercet, quatrain) as the paragraph groups
  sentences.

Lineation gives the poet a second syntax layered over grammar: the eye and ear register
the line as a unit even when the sentence does not stop there. This is the physical
correlate of poetry's compression — meaning distributed across two structures at once.

## The compression and music of poetic language

Everything above serves one end: a poem says more per word than prose because it works
on several channels simultaneously — semantic ([../linguistics/semantics.md](../linguistics/semantics.md)),
rhythmic, sonic, and visual — each carrying meaning, each reinforcing or complicating
the others. This is why a poem resists paraphrase: to restate its content is to
discard the very structures that constitute its meaning. Reading poetry well is the
purest form of the practice described in
[close-reading-and-interpretation.md](close-reading-and-interpretation.md).
