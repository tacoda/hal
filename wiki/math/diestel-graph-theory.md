# Graph Theory (Reinhard Diestel)

Reinhard Diestel's *Graph Theory* is the standard rigorous graduate reference for the
field, published as Volume 173 of Springer's *Graduate Texts in Mathematics* (6th
edition, 2025). It is notable for pairing full mathematical rigor with a genuinely
free electronic edition made available from the author's site, and for carrying the
reader from foundations all the way to the frontier of several active research areas —
most famously the Robertson–Seymour graph minors theory. Where an introductory text
surveys, Diestel proves cleanly and pushes to the deep structural theorems, making it
the book most working graph theorists keep on the shelf.

## Scope and approach

Each chapter opens with the elementary material and closes with results near the
research boundary, so the same text serves a first serious course and a specialist's
reference. The core chapters:

- **The basics** — graphs, degrees, paths, connectivity, trees and forests, bipartite
  graphs, contraction and minors.
- **Matching, covering, and packing** — König, Hall, Tutte, and the Gallai identities.
- **Connectivity** — Menger's theorem, 2- and 3-connected structure, and tree-width
  as a connectivity measure.
- **Planar graphs** — Euler's formula, Kuratowski and Wagner, and planar duality.
- **Colouring** — the five/four-colour theorems, chromatic number and its bounds,
  list colouring, and perfect graphs.
- **Flows** — network flows, the Tutte polynomial, and flow–colouring duality.
- **Extremal graph theory** — Turán's theorem, the Erdős–Stone theorem, and the
  Szemerédi regularity lemma.
- **Ramsey theory**, **Hamilton cycles**, **random graphs** (the probabilistic
  method), and **minors, trees, and well-quasi-ordering** — culminating in the
  Robertson–Seymour theorem that finite graphs are well-quasi-ordered under the minor
  relation.
- **Infinite graphs** — a distinctive chapter treating ends and the topological
  cycle space, reflecting Diestel's own research.

The treatment is proof-first and structural: definitions are minimal and precise, and
the payoff is the ability to follow deep theorems in full. It presumes the proof
maturity and set-theoretic comfort covered in
[../logic/index.md](../logic/index.md). Compared with West's
[west-introduction-to-graph-theory.md](west-introduction-to-graph-theory.md), Diestel
is terser, more advanced, and reaches further into modern structural theory, while
West carries more elementary breadth and a larger exercise base — the two are
complementary, West first and Diestel to go deep.
