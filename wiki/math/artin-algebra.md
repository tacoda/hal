# Algebra (Michael Artin)

Michael Artin's *Algebra* is the linear-algebra-forward introduction to abstract
algebra, grown out of his long-running MIT course. Its defining choice is pedagogical:
rather than opening with abstract group axioms, Artin starts from **matrices and
linear algebra** and lets the concrete example of matrix groups motivate the abstract
theory, so that groups, symmetry, and later structures arrive already grounded in
objects the reader can compute with. The result is a text with an unusually geometric
and example-driven flavor, strong on the concrete instances (symmetry groups,
rotations, lattices, quadratic number fields) that make the abstractions memorable.

## Scope and approach

The book weaves linear algebra through the whole development instead of treating it as
a prerequisite left behind. Its arc:

- **Matrices and linear algebra** — matrix operations, the general linear group,
  vector spaces, bases, and linear operators, all before the group axioms.
- **Groups** — abstract groups motivated by matrix and symmetry groups; subgroups,
  homomorphisms, cosets, and quotient groups.
- **Symmetry and group actions** — isometries of the plane, finite symmetry groups,
  and the orbit–stabilizer relationship, treated geometrically.
- **Bilinear forms and representations** — inner products, the spectral theorem, and
  a substantial early treatment of the **linear representations of finite groups**,
  which the linear-algebra emphasis makes natural.
- **Rings and fields** — factorization, ideals, field extensions, and Galois theory.
- **Number-theoretic threads** — quadratic number fields and Gauss's work, showing
  algebra in service of number theory.

Compared with the encyclopedic
[dummit-foote-abstract-algebra.md](dummit-foote-abstract-algebra.md), Artin is more
selective and more geometric, prioritizing intuition and the linear-algebraic viewpoint
over exhaustive coverage; the two are often paired, Artin for a motivated first pass
and Dummit–Foote as the reference.

## Where it sits

Artin makes the dependency explicit that other algebra texts leave implicit: the
subject is easier to see once [linear-algebra.md](linear-algebra.md) is in hand,
because matrix groups are the first and most tangible groups, and representation theory
turns abstract group elements back into matrices. It anchors
[abstract-algebra.md](abstract-algebra.md) from the concrete, computational side.
