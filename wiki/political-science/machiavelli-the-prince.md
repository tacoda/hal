# The Prince (Machiavelli)

Niccolò Machiavelli's *Il Principe*, written around 1513 and published posthumously in 1532, is a short treatise on how political power is acquired, consolidated, and retained. Cast as a practical handbook for a new ruler — nominally addressed to Lorenzo de' Medici — it breaks sharply with the mirror-for-princes tradition that preceded it, in which advice literature instructed rulers to model classical and Christian virtue. Machiavelli instead asks how rulers *actually* keep power, treating politics as an empirical domain governed by its own logic rather than by moral ideals.

## The central argument

The work's defining move is the separation of political effectiveness from conventional morality. Machiavelli argues that a ruler who always acts as goodness prescribes will be destroyed among the many who do not; therefore a prince "must learn how not to be good" and deploy or withhold virtue as circumstances demand. This is not a celebration of cruelty but an argument that the criterion for political action is its consequences for the security of the state, not its conformity to received ethics. The recurring standard throughout is what preserves the ruler and the political order.

Key threads:

- **Types of principalities and how they are won.** Machiavelli classifies states (hereditary, mixed, new, ecclesiastical) and examines the different difficulties of holding each. New principalities acquired by one's own arms and ability are hardest to win but easiest to keep; those won by fortune or others' arms are the reverse.
- **Virtù and fortuna.** Two organizing concepts. *Fortuna* is the contingent, uncontrollable element of political life — roughly, luck or circumstance. *Virtù* is the ruler's capacity for decisive, adaptive, skillful action that bends circumstance to advantage. Machiavelli suggests fortune governs perhaps half of human affairs and leaves the rest to be seized by the bold.
- **Fear versus love.** In the most cited passage, he asks whether it is better to be loved or feared, and answers that while both is ideal, the two rarely coexist, so it is safer to be feared — provided the prince avoids being hated, which he does by not seizing subjects' property or women.
- **Appearance and reality.** A prince need not possess the classical virtues but must *appear* to, since people judge by outcomes and images. Deception and the strategic management of reputation are treated as legitimate instruments.
- **Arms and self-reliance.** Machiavelli repeatedly warns against mercenary and auxiliary troops, urging rulers to build their own armies; sound military foundations underpin every other kind of power.

## Place in the canon

*The Prince* is conventionally read as the founding text of modern **political realism** — the tradition that analyzes power as it is exercised rather than as it ought to be, and that treats the state's survival as the paramount concern. By severing the analysis of power from theology and ideal ethics, Machiavelli is often credited (with qualification) as an early figure in the emergence of political science as an autonomous field. The adjective "Machiavellian" entered common usage as a byword for manipulative amorality, though scholars note this flattens a more complex thinker: his *Discourses on Livy*, a longer companion work, reveals a committed republican, complicating any reading of *The Prince* as a straightforward endorsement of tyranny. Interpretations range from the cynical (a manual for despots) to the ironic (a covert republican critique) to the pragmatic (disenchanted political science). Its influence extends through Hobbes and the social-contract tradition into modern realist theory in both political philosophy and international relations.
