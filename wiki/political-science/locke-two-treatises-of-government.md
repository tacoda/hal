# Two Treatises of Government (Locke)

John Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* (published anonymously in 1689, dated 1690) is a cornerstone of the liberal tradition and of modern constitutional thought. Written in the years surrounding England's Glorious Revolution, it defends a conception of government founded on natural rights, popular consent, and limited authority answerable to the people.

## The two treatises

- **First Treatise.** A detailed refutation of Sir Robert Filmer's *Patriarcha*, which had defended the absolute divine right of kings by tracing royal authority to Adam's God-given dominion. Locke dismantles the scriptural and logical basis of patriarchal absolutism, clearing the ground for a positive theory of legitimate government.
- **Second Treatise.** The constructive and by far the more influential half, subtitled "An Essay Concerning the True Original, Extent, and End of Civil Government." This is the enduring statement of Lockean political theory.

## The core argument (Second Treatise)

- **State of nature.** Unlike Hobbes's war of all against all, Locke's pre-political state is governed by a law of nature knowable through reason: because all are equal and independent, "no one ought to harm another in his life, health, liberty, or possessions." It is a state of liberty, not license.
- **Natural rights.** Individuals possess rights to life, liberty, and estate (property) prior to and independent of government. Protecting these rights is the purpose of political society.
- **Property.** In one of the work's most distinctive arguments, Locke derives private property from labor: mixing one's labor with the unowned resources of nature makes them one's own, subject (in the natural state) to the provisos that one not waste and that "enough and as good" be left for others. The invention of money later loosens these limits and enables unequal accumulation.
- **Government by consent.** Because the state of nature lacks an impartial judge and reliable enforcement, people consent to form political society and establish government. Legitimate authority derives only from the consent of the governed; government is a **trust** held for the people's benefit.
- **Limited government and separation of powers.** Locke distinguishes legislative, executive, and (federative) powers, holding the legislative supreme but bounded — it cannot rule arbitrarily, tax without consent, or transfer its lawmaking authority.
- **The right of revolution.** When rulers systematically violate the trust — invading the people's rights or governing without law — authority reverts to the people, who may legitimately resist and reconstitute the government. This dissolves government without necessarily dissolving society.

## Place in the canon

The *Two Treatises* reframed the social-contract tradition inherited from Hobbes toward **limited, accountable government protecting individual rights** — the intellectual backbone of classical liberalism and constitutionalism. Its vocabulary of natural rights, consent, and the right to resist tyranny shaped the political language of the eighteenth-century revolutions; the American Declaration of Independence's "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" and its theory of just government echo Locke directly. The labor theory of property influenced later economic and political thought across the ideological spectrum. Locke stands, with Hobbes and Rousseau, as one of the three canonical social-contract theorists, and is often called the father of liberalism.
