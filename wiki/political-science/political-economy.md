# Political Economy

Political economy studies the interaction of **states and markets** — how political power
shapes economic outcomes and how economic interests shape politics. Harold Lasswell's
classic definition of politics as "who gets what, when, and how" captures its core
question: the distribution of resources is decided partly by markets and partly by
political authority, and the two are inseparable. The field bridges political science and
economics (see [economics/index](../economics/index.md)) and treats the boundary between
public and private — what governments provide versus what markets provide — as itself a
political choice rather than a technical given.

## Why politics enters the economy

Markets can allocate efficiently under idealized conditions, but real markets exhibit
[market failures](../economics/market-failure-and-externalities.md): externalities (costs
or benefits that spill onto third parties), public goods, monopoly power, and information
asymmetries. Each failure is a standing invitation for political intervention — regulation,
taxation, public provision. Political economy asks not only *whether* intervention could
improve on the market, but *whether the political process will produce that improvement or
something worse*, since governments have failures of their own.

## Collective action and public goods

A **public good** is non-excludable (you cannot easily keep non-payers from enjoying it)
and non-rivalrous (one person's use does not diminish another's) — clean air, national
defense, a stable legal order. Because non-payers cannot be excluded, everyone has an
incentive to **free-ride** and let others bear the cost, so the good is undersupplied by
voluntary action.

Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* sharpened this into a central insight:
groups do **not** automatically organize to pursue their shared interest. Large, diffuse
groups (consumers, taxpayers) struggle to overcome free-riding, while small, concentrated
groups (a particular industry) organize easily. This asymmetry helps explain why narrow,
well-organized interests often prevail over the broad public in policy — a recurring theme
in [public-policy-and-governance](public-policy-and-governance.md). Solutions include
*selective incentives* (benefits available only to contributors), coercion (mandatory
taxes), and small-group monitoring — mechanisms that also appear in the repeated-game logic
of [game theory](../economics/game-theory.md).

## Rent-seeking and regulation

**Rent-seeking** is the use of political influence to capture value — subsidies, protective
tariffs, exclusive licenses — rather than to create it. It is socially wasteful twice over:
the transfer itself distorts the economy, and real resources (lobbying, litigation) are
burned competing for the transfer. *Regulatory capture* is a related pathology, in which
the agency meant to regulate an industry comes to serve that industry's interests. These
concepts caution that intervention is not automatically corrective; the question is always
whether a given political arrangement improves on the market it replaces.

## Welfare states and redistribution

Modern states redistribute income and insure citizens against risks (unemployment,
illness, old age) through the **welfare state**. Gøsta Esping-Andersen's influential
typology distinguishes three "worlds" of welfare capitalism:

| Regime type | Logic | Illustrative examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Liberal** | Modest, means-tested benefits; market-reliant | US, UK-leaning systems |
| **Social-democratic** | Universal, generous, de-commodifying | Nordic systems |
| **Conservative / corporatist** | Benefits tied to employment status and family | Continental European systems |

The size and design of the welfare state is a core distributive-political question, shaped
by the balance of organized interests, ideology (see
[political-theory-and-ideologies](political-theory-and-ideologies.md)), and the structure
of the economy.

## Varieties of capitalism

Capitalist economies are not all alike. The *varieties of capitalism* framework (Hall &
Soskice) contrasts **liberal market economies** (LMEs), where firms coordinate mainly
through competitive markets and arm's-length contracts, with **coordinated market
economies** (CMEs), where firms rely on non-market coordination — long-term bank finance,
industry associations, cooperative labor relations. Each configuration produces different
strengths (radical innovation in LMEs, incremental high-quality manufacturing in CMEs),
illustrating that "the market" is embedded in political and institutional arrangements.

## Approaches to policy

Political-economy analysis of policy comes in several strands: *public choice* applies
economic assumptions of self-interest to political actors (voters, bureaucrats,
politicians); *institutional* approaches stress how rules and organizations structure
incentives; *international political economy* studies trade, finance, and development across
borders, linking to [international-relations](international-relations.md). The common thread
is that policy outcomes reflect not just what is efficient but what is politically feasible
given the distribution of power and interests.
