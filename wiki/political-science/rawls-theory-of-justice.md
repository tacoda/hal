# A Theory of Justice (Rawls)

John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) is the most influential work of twentieth-century political philosophy in the English-speaking world. It revived normative political theory — then widely thought to have been exhausted — by offering a systematic, constructive account of what makes a society's basic institutions just. Rawls calls his view **justice as fairness**, and develops it as a social-contract theory pitched at a high level of abstraction, explicitly generalizing the contractarian tradition of Locke, Rousseau, and Kant.

## The central apparatus

- **The subject of justice.** Justice applies first to the "basic structure" of society — the major institutions (constitution, economy, family) that distribute rights, duties, and the benefits of social cooperation. The question is how to design fair terms of cooperation among free and equal persons.
- **The original position.** Rawls's signature thought experiment. Parties choose principles of justice from an initial situation designed to be fair, modeling a hypothetical agreement rather than an actual historical contract.
- **The veil of ignorance.** In the original position, the parties are deprived of knowledge of their own particulars — their class, race, sex, natural talents, conception of the good, and even the generation they belong to. Not knowing how any principle would affect them personally, they cannot tailor principles to their own advantage; they must choose principles fair to all. This device converts the problem of justice into a problem of rational choice under an enforced impartiality.
- **The two principles.** Rawls argues the parties would choose, in lexical priority:
  1. **Equal basic liberties** — each person has an equal claim to a fully adequate scheme of basic rights and liberties (speech, conscience, political participation, etc.), and this principle takes priority over the second.
  2. **The difference principle and fair equality of opportunity** — social and economic inequalities are permissible only if (a) they attach to positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity, and (b) they work to the greatest benefit of the least-advantaged members of society.

## Major moves and their significance

Rawls positions justice as fairness against **utilitarianism**, the era's dominant framework, arguing that maximizing aggregate welfare can license sacrificing some individuals for the greater sum and fails to "take seriously the distinction between persons." His theory instead treats the priority of individual rights as inviolable. He argues that parties behind the veil would prefer his principles to utilitarian and other alternatives, reasoning conservatively (a "maximin" strategy) to secure a decent position no matter where they land. The book also develops "reflective equilibrium" as a method — adjusting principles and considered judgments against each other until they cohere.

## Place in the canon

*A Theory of Justice* redefined the field: virtually all subsequent Anglophone political philosophy — libertarian (Nozick), communitarian (Sandel, MacIntyre, Walzer), and capabilities-based (Sen, Nussbaum) — took shape partly as response to it. It reasserted the social-contract tradition against utilitarian and intuitionist rivals, made distributive justice a central philosophical concern, and gave liberal egalitarianism its most rigorous formulation. Rawls later refined the project in *Political Liberalism* (1993), narrowing the theory's claims to accommodate the fact of reasonable moral and religious pluralism.
