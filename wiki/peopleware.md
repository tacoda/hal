# Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams

Tom DeMarco and Timothy Lister's *Peopleware* (first edition 1987; the expanded third
edition, Addison-Wesley, arrived in 2013) is the classic argument that software
development is a **human** activity before it is a technical one. Each chapter presents a
principle and backs it with a story, a study, or a memorable vignette.

## The central thesis: sociological, not technical

The book's opening claim sets its whole agenda: *"The major problems of our work are not
so much technological as sociological in nature."* Managers reflexively look for
technical fixes — a new tool, a new methodology, a new process — when the real failures
are about people, teams, trust, and environment. Treating knowledge workers as
interchangeable production units ("the High-Tech Illusion") is the root error. This is
the same sociological lens [The Mythical Man-Month](the-mythical-man-month.md) applies to
scheduling, and the same people-first orientation as
[The Effective Engineer](the-effective-engineer.md).

## Flow and the cost of interruptions

Meaningful engineering happens in **flow** — a state of deep, immersive concentration
that takes roughly fifteen minutes of ramp-up to reach. An interruption doesn't cost the
two minutes of the interruption; it costs the flow state and the long climb back into it.
DeMarco and Lister introduce **E-Factor** (uninterrupted hours ÷ body-present hours) as a
crude measure of how much of the day an environment actually permits real work. Constant
phone calls, drop-in questions, and noisy open plans quietly destroy productivity while
looking, on paper, like a busy and collaborative office.

## The office environment matters

A recurring theme, grounded in the authors' long-running **Coding War Games**: the best
performers vastly outproduced the worst, and the strongest environmental correlate was
workspace — quiet, private, defensible space with the ability to silence interruptions.
Cramped, noisy, phone-saturated offices are a false economy; the salary cost dwarfs the
real-estate savings, so degrading the environment to save floor space loses money on net.

## Teamicide vs. jelled teams

A **jelled team** is a group that has clicked into a unit more capable than the sum of
its members — shared goals, mutual trust, a sense of eliteness, and momentum. You cannot
*build* jelled teams on demand, but you can create conditions that let them form. What
you can reliably do is *kill* them, and DeMarco and Lister catalog **teamicide** — the
routine managerial acts that prevent jelling:

- defensive management and lack of trust in the team's judgment
- bureaucracy and pointless paperwork
- physical separation of team members
- fragmentation of people's time across too many projects
- quality reduction to hit dates, and phony deadlines
- clique control that breaks up teams that have already jelled

The "Spaghetti Dinner" chapter is the positive counter-image: a manager engineers an easy
early shared success (cooking a meal together) to give a new team its first taste of
winning as a unit.

## Hiring and keeping good people

Because people are the whole game, turnover is enormously expensive — losing an
experienced engineer means losing hard-won, tacit organizational knowledge, not just a
headcount. High turnover also breeds a short-term, transactional culture where no one
invests in the long game. The managerial job is therefore to **hire well and then keep
people** by building an environment worth staying in: professional growth, autonomy, and
teams worth belonging to. Good management, in this view, is mostly about removing
obstacles and protecting people's ability to do good work — not about driving them harder.
