# The Checklist Manifesto

Atul Gawande (2009). A surgeon's argument that in an age of overwhelming
complexity, the failures that hurt us are rarely from ignorance (*we don't know
enough*) and increasingly from ineptitude (*we know, but fail to apply what we
know correctly*). The remedy is embarrassingly humble: the checklist. Not as a
bureaucratic box-tick, but as a discipline that guarantees the stupid,
avoidable steps get done under pressure.

## Core argument

Modern work — surgery, aviation, construction, finance, software — has outgrown the
capacity of any single expert's memory and attention. Volume and complexity have
exceeded the reliability of individual skill. Expertise is necessary but no longer
sufficient. Checklists catch the "dumb but critical" misses that even brilliant
practitioners make when tired, rushed, or distracted.

The book's evidence anchor is the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist Gawande helped
develop. A short list of pause-point checks, tested across eight hospitals
worldwide, cut major complications and deaths substantially — in rich and poor
hospitals alike. Aviation is the model discipline: pilots run checklists for both
routine operations and rare emergencies, and the checklist is authoritative even
for the captain.

## What makes a good checklist

- **Short and focused** — not exhaustive; it targets the killer items that are
  skipped, not everything conceivable. Five to nine items per pause point.
- **Has defined pause points** — moments in the workflow where you stop and run it
  (before anesthesia, before incision, before leaving the room).
- **Two kinds**: *DO-CONFIRM* (do the work from memory/experience, then pause to
  confirm nothing was missed) and *READ-DO* (read each step, then do it).
- **Enables communication, not just verification** — a surprising effect: the
  surgical checklist forced the team to talk (names, roles, concerns), and much of
  the benefit came from that team activation, not the ticks alone.
- **Tested and revised** — a checklist that hasn't been trialed against real work is
  guesswork; good ones are refined until they fit the flow.

## Why it matters

The checklist is the low-tech counterpart to the automated safeguards elsewhere in
HAL. It shares DNA with the blameless, systematized incident practice of
[Site Reliability Engineering](site-reliability-engineering.md) (build the
discipline into the system, not the individual), and with the process rigor of
[Effective DevOps](effective-devops.md). In AI-assisted development, the
checklist-as-discipline idea reappears directly in the
[CRESS context checklist](cress-context-checklist.md) — a checklist applied to
setting up agent context.
