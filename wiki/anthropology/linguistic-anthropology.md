# Linguistic Anthropology

Linguistic anthropology studies language as **culture** and as **social action** — not
as an abstract formal system in the head of an idealized speaker, but as a resource
people use to make sense of the world, to belong to groups, and to accomplish things
together. It is the bridge between anthropology and the field of
[../linguistics/index.md](../linguistics/index.md): where much of theoretical
linguistics treats language as a self-contained structure, linguistic anthropology asks
how language is embedded in social life, worldview, and everyday interaction — a direct
application of [the-culture-concept](the-culture-concept.md) to the medium of speech.

## Linguistic relativity (Sapir–Whorf)

The subfield's most famous idea is **linguistic relativity**, associated with Edward
Sapir and Benjamin Lee Whorf: the proposal that the language a person speaks shapes how
they perceive and think about the world. Two versions are usually distinguished:

- **Strong (linguistic determinism)** — language *determines* thought; what your
  language lacks words for, you cannot think. This version is widely rejected.
- **Weak (relativity)** — language *influences* habitual thought and attention, making
  some distinctions easier or more automatic. This weaker claim has empirical support
  from studies of color categorization, spatial reference (languages that use
  cardinal directions rather than left/right), grammatical gender, and how languages
  encode number, time, or evidentiality.

The lesson is that grammatical and lexical categories are not neutral labels on a
pre-given reality — they package experience, and different languages package it
differently. This links directly to the way vocabularies segment the world in
[kinship-and-social-organization](kinship-and-social-organization.md) and to the study of
meaning in [../linguistics/index.md](../linguistics/index.md).

## Language and worldview

Beyond formal grammar, linguistic anthropology examines how the *content* of a language —
its metaphors, categories, genres, and named distinctions — carries a culture's
worldview. Ways of naming kin, classifying plants and animals, or marking politeness and
status encode what a community treats as important. Language is thus a lens onto how a
people organize and value their world.

## Speech communities and variation

A **speech community** is a group that shares norms for using and interpreting language —
not necessarily the same grammar, but the same expectations about what forms mean and
when they are appropriate. Which variety a person uses, and how they shift between them,
signals identity and social position. This concern with socially patterned variation is
shared with, and largely overlaps, [../linguistics/sociolinguistics.md](../linguistics/sociolinguistics.md);
linguistic anthropology leans toward the ethnographic and interpretive, sociolinguistics
toward the correlational and quantitative, but they study the same reality.

## Language socialization

**Language socialization** captures a double process: children (and newcomers) are
**socialized *through* language** and simultaneously **socialized *into* language use**.
Learning to speak is inseparable from learning to be a competent member of a society —
to greet, to show respect, to tell a story the right way, to take turns. Acquiring
grammar and acquiring culture are the same developmental project, connecting linguistic
anthropology to how language is learned more broadly (see
[../linguistics/index.md](../linguistics/index.md)).

## Language as social action

Speaking *does* things: it promises, apologizes, commands, insults, blesses, and
performs identity. Studying language as social action means analyzing real interaction —
conversation, ritual speech, storytelling, argument — to see how meaning is produced in
context and how talk builds and negotiates social relationships. On this view language is
not merely a mirror of society but one of the primary means by which society is enacted.

## Why it matters

Linguistic anthropology keeps the study of language tethered to the study of people.
It shows that grammar carries culture, that categories shape (though do not imprison)
thought, and that every utterance is a social act. It supplies the human and cultural
grounding for the more formal work in [../linguistics/index.md](../linguistics/index.md).
