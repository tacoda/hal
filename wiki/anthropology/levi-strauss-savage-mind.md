# The Savage Mind

Claude Lévi-Strauss's *La Pensée sauvage* (1962; English *The Savage Mind*, 1966) is a
central statement of **structuralism** in anthropology. The French title puns on
"savage thought" and the wild pansy (*pensée* is both "thought" and the flower), and the
book's argument is that so-called primitive thought is not a lesser or pre-logical form
of the modern scientific mind but an equally rigorous, differently organized way of
ordering the world. As Lévi-Strauss insists, *la pensée sauvage* names not the mind of
"savages" but mind in its untamed state — thought as it operates before it is
domesticated for a particular technical return.

## The science of the concrete

Lévi-Strauss argues that peoples without writing or laboratories nonetheless build vast,
precise classifications of plants, animals, and natural phenomena — a "science of the
concrete." Where modern science abstracts and quantifies, this mode of thought reasons
through sensible, perceptible qualities: colors, textures, tastes, directions, animal
behaviors. Totemism, on his account, is not a crude religion of animal worship but a
**logic of classification**: natural species furnish a ready-made code of differences
that can be mapped onto social differences between groups. Nature is "good to think with"
because the contrasts among species model the contrasts among clans.

## Bricolage

The book's most influential image is the **bricoleur**. The engineer (the figure of
scientific thought) designs tools and concepts precisely for the problem at hand and
constrains the project by available means only reluctantly. The bricoleur, by contrast,
works with a closed stock of heterogeneous materials left over from previous uses,
recombining "whatever is at hand" into new structures. **Mythical and magical thought is
bricolage**: it assembles meaning from the finite repertoire of a culture's existing
signs, reorganizing them rather than inventing from scratch. This account of how meaning
is generated from combination and contrast — from position within a system rather than
intrinsic content — is the core structuralist move, and it links directly to
[kinship and social organization](kinship-and-social-organization.md), where
Lévi-Strauss had earlier argued that marriage systems function as structures of exchange
governed by binary rules (as in his analysis of Australian Aranda and Arabana marriage
classes).

## The polemic with Sartre and the critique of history

The final chapter is a pointed argument against Jean-Paul Sartre's existentialist,
history-centered humanism. Lévi-Strauss denies that historical, progressive
consciousness is the privileged form of reason; he treats history as one code among
many, no more foundational than the "cold" classificatory systems of societies without
writing. The aim of the human sciences, he provocatively wrote, is not to constitute man
but to dissolve him — to locate the unconscious mental structures beneath the varied
surface of cultures. This decentering of the individual subject made the book a founding
text for structuralism far beyond anthropology.

## Significance

*The Savage Mind* reframed **[anthropological theory](anthropological-theory.md)** by
shifting attention from function and history to the underlying logical structures of the
mind, which Lévi-Strauss held to be universal even as their contents vary. Its concepts —
the science of the concrete, bricolage, the classificatory reading of totemism — passed
into philosophy, literary theory, and cultural studies, and its structuralist program
set the terms that later post-structuralist thinkers would react against.
