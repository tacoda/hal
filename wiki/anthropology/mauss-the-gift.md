# The Gift

Marcel Mauss's *Essai sur le don* (1925; English *The Gift*) is the founding text of
**[economic anthropology](economic-anthropology.md)** and one of the most influential
essays in the social sciences. Drawing on ethnographic and historical material from
Polynesia, Melanesia, the Pacific Northwest, and archaic Roman, Germanic, and Hindu law,
Mauss asks a deceptively simple question: in societies without markets or money, what
force obliges a person who has received a gift to give one in return?

## Gift exchange and the three obligations

Mauss's answer is that gift-giving in these societies is never truly "free." Gifts appear
voluntary and disinterested, but they are in fact obligatory and interested, governed by
a triple obligation: **the obligation to give, the obligation to receive, and the
obligation to reciprocate.** To refuse to give, to accept, or to repay is to refuse the
social bond itself — an act tantamount to declaring war. Exchange thus creates and
sustains alliances, hierarchies, and peace. Because the objects given carry the identity
and honor of the giver, the relationship they establish is between persons, not merely
things; the gift is a vehicle of moral and social life, not just economic transfer.

Two ethnographic cases anchor the argument. Among the Maori, Mauss seizes on the concept
of **hau**, the "spirit" of the gift: a valued object (*taonga*) is animated by a force
that longs to return to its origin, so failing to pass a gift onward or reciprocate is
dangerous. Among the peoples of the Pacific Northwest coast, the **potlatch** shows
exchange in an extreme, agonistic form — feasts of competitive giving and even
destruction of property, in which rivals fight for prestige (**mana**) by out-giving one
another. Mauss also links his account to Malinowski's Kula ring (see
[Argonauts of the Western Pacific](malinowski-argonauts-of-the-western-pacific.md)) as a
further instance of ceremonial, obligation-laden circulation.

## The total social fact

Mauss's most far-reaching concept is the **total social fact** (*fait social total*),
built on his uncle Émile Durkheim's notion of the social fact. Gift exchange is "total"
because it engages every dimension of society at once — economic, legal, political,
religious, kinship, and aesthetic — and so cannot be reduced to any one of them. It is a
single institution through which an entire social order expresses and reproduces itself.
The related idea of **total prestation** names the system of obligatory reciprocal
exchange integrated across a society's institutions. This holism became a durable
methodological principle: to understand a practice, follow how it binds the whole social
fabric together rather than isolating it as "the economy."

## Significance

*The Gift* dismantled the assumption that self-interested market exchange is the natural
baseline of economic life and revealed reciprocity as a distinct, foundational logic of
human society. It shaped **[anthropological theory](anthropological-theory.md)** across
generations: Claude Lévi-Strauss read Mauss's exchange principle as the key to
[kinship and social organization](kinship-and-social-organization.md), treating marriage
as the reciprocal exchange of women between groups; Marshall Sahlins built his typology
of reciprocities on it; and Karl Polanyi's economic history echoes its critique of the
market as universal. The essay remains the reference point for any analysis of gifts,
debt, obligation, and non-market exchange.
