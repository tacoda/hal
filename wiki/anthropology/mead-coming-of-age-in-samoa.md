# Coming of Age in Samoa

Margaret Mead's *Coming of Age in Samoa: A Psychological Study of Primitive Youth for
Western Civilisation* (1928) is among the most widely read works of anthropology ever
published. Based on fieldwork on the island of Taʻū in American Samoa, primarily among
adolescent girls, it argues that the turbulence Western societies treat as a biological
feature of adolescence is not universal but culturally shaped.

## The argument: culture over biology

The book's guiding question, set by Mead's mentor Franz Boas, was whether the storms of
adolescence — the emotional conflict, rebellion, and anxiety treated in 1920s America as
an inevitable consequence of puberty — are rooted in human biology or in the specific
demands of a culture. Mead reported that Samoan girls passed into adulthood with
comparatively little stress: a society with relaxed attitudes toward premarital sexuality,
diffuse child-rearing across extended households, and few high-stakes choices produced
few of the crises that beset American teenagers. Her conclusion was strongly
**culturally deterministic**: if adolescence can be smooth in one society and fraught in
another, then culture, not biology, is the leading force shaping psychosexual
development. The finding was aimed squarely at a Western audience — the subtitle names it
a study "for Western Civilisation" — and Mead used Samoa as a mirror in which American
readers might see their own arrangements as contingent choices rather than natural
necessities.

## Place in the nature–nurture debate

The book was a landmark contribution to the **[culture concept](the-culture-concept.md)**
and to the nature–nurture debate. It carried forward the Boasian program of cultural
relativism and its critique of biological determinism — the doctrine that race,
temperament, and life course are fixed by heredity. By locating the sources of
personality and behavior in learned cultural patterns, Mead helped make "culture" the
central explanatory concept of American anthropology and popularized the discipline far
beyond the academy.

## The Freeman controversy

In 1983, five years after Mead's death, the New Zealand anthropologist Derek Freeman
published *Margaret Mead and Samoa: The Making and Unmaking of an Anthropological Myth*,
charging that Mead had fundamentally misdescribed Samoan society — which he portrayed as
sexually restrictive, competitive, and prone to violence — and that she may have been
deliberately misled by young informants. The dispute became a public spectacle, front-page
news, and a proxy war between cultural and biological accounts of human behavior.

The consensus response has been to treat both parties as overstating their cases. Critics
noted that Mead and Freeman studied different places and eras (a small outer island in the
1920s versus a colonized, urbanizing setting decades later) and that Freeman's own
sociobiological commitments shaped his reading as much as Mead's Boasian ones shaped hers.
His reliance on public declarations of virginity and morality, rather than private
conduct, was itself questioned. The episode is now taught less as a verdict on who was
right than as a case study in the difficulty of ethnographic verification, the influence
of theory on observation, and the limits of a single fieldworker's authority — themes
central to **[ethnography and fieldwork](ethnography-and-fieldwork.md)**.

## Significance

Whatever the empirical verdict on Taʻū, *Coming of Age in Samoa* framed a durable
question — how much of the human life course is made by culture rather than given by
nature — and made anthropology a public science. Its legacy is inseparable from the
controversy it eventually provoked, which turned the book into the discipline's standing
lesson about the fragility and stakes of ethnographic claims.
