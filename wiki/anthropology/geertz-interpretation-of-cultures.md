# The Interpretation of Cultures

Clifford Geertz's 1973 collection of essays is the defining statement of **interpretive
(or symbolic) anthropology**. Against the search for universal, law-like generalizations
about human behavior, Geertz argued that culture is a system of meanings and that the
anthropologist's task is not explanation in the mode of natural science but
interpretation — reading a culture as one reads a text.

## Culture as webs of meaning

Geertz redefined the **[culture concept](the-culture-concept.md)** itself. Culture is not
a set of behaviors, traits, or customs that can be objectively tallied; it is a
"web of significance" that people themselves have spun and in which they are suspended.
On his famous semiotic formulation, the analysis of culture is therefore "not an
experimental science in search of law but an interpretive one in search of meaning."
Meaning is public, not private: it lives in shared symbols — words, rituals, gestures,
objects — through which people make sense of their lives, so it is available to the
observer who learns to read it.

## Thick description

The book's central methodological idea is **thick description**, a term Geertz borrows
from the philosopher Gilbert Ryle. Ryle's example: the same physical motion — a rapid
contraction of the eyelid — may be an involuntary twitch, a conspiratorial wink, a parody
of a wink, or a rehearsal of one. "Thin" description records only the muscular movement;
**thick description** captures the layered structure of meaning that makes the gesture
intelligible within its context, distinguishing the twitcher from the winker from the
parodist. Ethnography, for Geertz, is thick description: sorting out the stratified
hierarchy of significances embedded in social action. The interpretation is inescapably
the ethnographer's own construction — a reading of a reading — and it is judged not by
predictive power but by how deeply it lets an outsider grasp what is at stake for the
people themselves.

## Religion and the reading of a culture

Several essays extend this program. In treating **[ritual, symbolism, and
religion](ritual-symbolism-and-religion.md)** as a cultural system, Geertz argues that
religion works by fusing a people's *ethos* — their moral and aesthetic mood — with their
*worldview* — their picture of how things really are, so that each renders the other
compelling. Symbols make life's ambiguities, suffering, and paradoxes bearable by
ordering them. The companion essay "Deep Play: Notes on the Balinese Cockfight" (often
read alongside this volume) is the classic demonstration: the cockfight is interpreted
not as gambling or sport but as a text Balinese men tell themselves about status, rivalry,
and masculinity — "a story they tell themselves about themselves."

## Significance

*The Interpretation of Cultures* reoriented **[anthropological
theory](anthropological-theory.md)** toward hermeneutics and away from both functionalism
and the search for behavioral laws. Its influence spread well beyond anthropology into
history, sociology, cultural studies, and literary theory, and it helped launch the
broader "interpretive turn" in the human sciences. Later critics charged that thick
description risks conflating the ethnographer's literary artistry with the natives'
reality and offers few checks on interpretive validity — a critique that fed the
reflexive, "writing culture" debates of the 1980s. Even so, culture-as-meaning and thick
description remain foundational vocabulary for the discipline.
