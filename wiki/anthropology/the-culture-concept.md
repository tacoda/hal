# The Culture Concept

Culture is anthropology's foundational and most contested idea. In its broadest
anthropological sense, culture is the whole learned, shared, and symbolic system through
which a group of people makes sense of the world and acts in it — knowledge, beliefs,
values, practices, institutions, and the meanings attached to them. The classic definition
comes from E. B. Tylor (1871): culture is "that complex whole which includes knowledge,
belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as
a member of society." Later anthropologists sharpened it, but three features have proved
durable: culture is **learned**, **shared**, and **symbolic**.

## Learned, shared, symbolic

- **Learned.** Culture is transmitted socially, not carried in the genes. A child acquires
  a language, a cuisine, and a moral world through **enculturation** — the same process
  sociologists call socialization; see
  [../sociology/culture-and-socialization.md](../sociology/culture-and-socialization.md).
  Because it is learned, culture varies enormously across groups and can change within a
  lifetime.
- **Shared.** Culture is a property of groups, not isolated individuals. It is what lets
  people coordinate, understand one another, and expect the same things — while also
  marking the boundaries between "us" and "them." Sharing is never perfect: variation,
  subcultures, and disagreement exist inside every culture.
- **Symbolic.** Culture works through symbols — things (words, flags, gestures, foods) whose
  meaning is assigned by convention rather than fixed by nature. Clifford Geertz built his
  whole program on this: humans are "suspended in webs of significance" they themselves
  have spun, and the study of culture is the interpretation of those webs. See
  [geertz-interpretation-of-cultures](geertz-interpretation-of-cultures.md).

## Relativism versus ethnocentrism

Two attitudes toward cultural difference define the ethical and methodological core of the
field.

- **Ethnocentrism** is judging another culture by the standards of one's own, treating the
  familiar as natural and the foreign as deficient. It is the default human posture and the
  thing anthropology trains people out of.
- **Cultural relativism** is the corrective: the principle that a practice must first be
  understood in the context of the culture that produces it, on its own terms, before it is
  evaluated. As a *methodological* rule — suspend judgment to understand — relativism is
  near-universally accepted. As a *moral* claim — that no cross-cultural judgment is ever
  possible — it is genuinely controversial, since it seems to disarm any critique of harmful
  practices. Most anthropologists hold the methodological version firmly and treat the moral
  version as a hard, unresolved problem (see [../philosophy/ethics.md](../philosophy/ethics.md)).

## Emic and etic

Borrowed from linguistics by Kenneth Pike, the **emic/etic** distinction names two
standpoints for describing a culture.

| | Emic | Etic |
|---|---|---|
| Standpoint | Insider's — the categories the people themselves use | Outsider's — the analyst's comparative, scientific categories |
| Analogy | *Phon**emic*** (meaningful distinctions within a language) | *Phon**etic*** (universal, observer-measurable sounds) |
| Goal | Grasp the meaning of a practice as lived | Compare practices across cultures on common terms |
| Risk | Going native; losing comparability | Imposing alien categories; missing the meaning |

Good ethnography moves between the two: it takes the emic view seriously (the point of
[ethnography-and-fieldwork](ethnography-and-fieldwork.md)) while retaining etic concepts
that make [comparison](what-is-anthropology.md) possible.

## Is "a culture" a real thing?

The mid-twentieth-century picture of the world as a patchwork of bounded, internally
uniform "cultures" — Culture A here, Culture B there — has come under heavy criticism. Real
groups are internally divided, constantly borrowing, migrating, and changing; boundaries are
fuzzy and often drawn by outsiders for political reasons. Anthropologists like Lila
Abu-Lughod urged "writing against culture," warning that reifying culture can freeze living
people into timeless types and erase power and dissent. The reflexive turn in
[anthropological-theory](anthropological-theory.md) pushed the field from treating cultures
as *things* toward treating culture as an ongoing, contested *process* of meaning-making —
which links directly to how [../sociology/culture-and-socialization.md](../sociology/culture-and-socialization.md)
and [../philosophy/epistemology.md](../philosophy/epistemology.md) handle the making and
knowing of shared reality.

## Why it matters

The culture concept is anthropology's single most influential export — it reshaped how
psychology, sociology, history, and everyday speech think about human difference. It
delivered a powerful lesson: much of what feels natural, universal, or obvious is in fact
learned and local. Holding that lesson without collapsing into "anything goes" relativism is
the discipline's permanent balancing act.
