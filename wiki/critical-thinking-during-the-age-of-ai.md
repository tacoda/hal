# Critical Thinking During the Age of AI

Addy Osmani argues that as AI generates code, designs, and "occasionally
plausible answers" on demand, **human critical thinking matters more, not less.**
Automation can't ask the right question, challenge an assumption, or think
independently — those stay the engineer's job. He structures the guidance with
the classic **Who / What / Where / When / Why / How** framework.

## The checklist

- **Who — don't treat AI as an oracle.** It's a confident intern, not an
  authority. Verify its output; check who (or what) the claim actually rests on.
  This is the "verify everything, like an inexperienced intern" stance echoed in
  [learning the craft](learning-the-craft.md).
- **What — know what you're actually asking for.** Be precise about the problem;
  a vague prompt gets a plausible-but-wrong answer you then have to catch.
- **Where — mind the context and its boundaries.** Where does this apply, and
  where does it break? AI generalizes past the edges of what's safe.
- **When — judge timing and currency.** Is the answer current? Is now the right
  moment to automate this, or does it need a human first pass?
- **Why — demand the reasoning.** Understand *why* a solution works, not just
  that it runs. Understanding the "why" is what separates using AI as a learning
  tool from using it as a crutch.
- **How — keep your hands in.** Know how it works underneath so you can spot when
  the [abstraction leaks](law-of-leaky-abstractions.md).

## The through-line

The skill under pressure is **judgment** — the thing AI can't supply and the
thing that erodes fastest if you outsource it. Skepticism, precise questions,
and demanding the reasoning are the antidotes to
[comprehension debt](comprehension-debt-osmani.md) and to the cognitive
offloading described in [does AI make us stupid](does-ai-make-us-stupid.md).
