# The Rise of the AI Engineer

swyx's 2023 essay naming a new engineering subdiscipline. The thesis: a **"shift right"
of applied AI**, driven by the emergent capabilities and API/open-source availability of
foundation models. Tasks that in 2013 took *five years and a research team* now take
**API docs and a spare afternoon**. That collapse in cost creates a new role between the
model researchers and the product.

## Why the role emerges

- **Emergent capabilities.** Even the people who *train* the models don't fully know what
  they can do. Non-researchers find and exploit capabilities just by spending time with
  the models and applying them to domains research undervalues (e.g. Jasper with
  copywriting).
- **Supply constraint.** ~5,000 LLM researchers exist against ~50,000,000 software
  engineers. The labs corner the scarce research talent and rent it out as "AI research
  as a service" APIs. An **in-between class** must rise to wield those APIs — you can't
  hire the researchers, but you can build on what they ship.
- Karpathy: *"there's probably going to be significantly more AI Engineers than there are
  ML engineers... One can be quite successful in this role without ever training
  anything."*

## Where they sit

Between the API line and the product. ML researchers/engineers own *pretrain-scale* data
and *general-benchmark* evals; the AI Engineer owns **product-specific data and evals**,
plus model selection, tool/chaining/retrieval stacks, and keeping pace with a firehose of
daily papers. The API boundary is **permeable** — AI Engineers can drift left to
finetune, research engineers drift right to build on APIs. swyx calls it *"the
highest-demand engineering job of the decade."*

This is the applied end of the spectrum described in
