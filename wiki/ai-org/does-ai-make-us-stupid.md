# Does AI Make Us Stupid? (Cognitive Offloading and the Flynn Effect)

A JCON 2026 conversation with **Jan Thierolf** (interviewed on the AI Native Dev
channel) on whether AI is making us less capable. Thierolf's reframe: not
"dumber," but **changing how we think and learn** — by offloading cognitive work
to tools, the way earlier tools already reshaped us.

## Every tool is a cognitive trade

Learning has been disrupted before, and each disruption offloaded some cognitive
task:

- **GPS / navigation** — studies on the hippocampus show we no longer build the
  spatial maps we used to when we just follow "left, right, left." We traded
  wayfinding skill for convenience.
- **Spell-check and autocorrect** — traded spelling.
- **Books → search engines (~2005)** — the skill shifted from *knowing* to
  *finding information efficiently*, and brought a new problem: information
  overload, deciding what you need.
- **Generative AI (now)** — the newest shift in the same lineage.

The term for this is **cognitive offloading**: handing a mental task to an
external tool. It's not inherently bad — but each offload is a skill you stop
exercising.

## The Flynn effect is reversing

For most of the 20th century, measured IQ rose generation over generation (the
**Flynn effect**) — attributed to better nutrition, education, and tools. Recent
studies suggest it has **stalled and begun to reverse**: on the traditional
measures, we're scoring slightly *lower*. Thierolf hedges honestly — maybe we're
changing rather than declining, and the old tests measure the wrong thing (IQ
tests may simply adapt). But the signal is real enough to take seriously.

**And the disruption always hits the youngest the most** — the same
disproportionate exposure of juniors described in
[learning the craft](learning-the-craft.md) and
[the ironies of automation](../systems-thinking/ironies-of-automation.md).

## What to do about it

Thierolf's practical stance — do the hard, formative work yourself before you
offload it:

- **Do the math on paper first, then reach for the calculator.** For the simple
  steps you already know, offloading is fine — it's just tedious. For the *hard,
  important* tasks, do them yourself, because "we grow where we learn."
- **Keep doing the tasks that teach you** before floating them to a tool.
- **Understand the model and how systems interact.** As the skill set shifts,
  the durable focus becomes understanding structure and systems — and verifying
  whether the tool did the right thing (or the worst thing). This is the
  [leaky-abstraction](../software-engineering/law-of-leaky-abstractions.md) discipline and the
  verification stance of
  [critical thinking during the age of AI](critical-thinking-during-the-age-of-ai.md).
