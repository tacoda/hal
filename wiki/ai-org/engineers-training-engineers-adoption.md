# Engineers Training Engineers — Getting to 93% AI Adoption

Tomasz Maj, interviewed at AI DevCon London 2026 (AI Native Dev / Tessl),
describes how deliberate training — rather than just handing out tools — drove
AI adoption in a large organization to **93%**. His framing inverts the usual
assumption: *adoption is not the problem*. Everybody wants to use AI. The problem
is **doing it well**.

## The core insight: tools without training amplify bad practice

Give people tools with no onboarding and the outcome is uneven — **bad practices
get worse and good practices don't spread**. The bet was to invest in training
that positions engineers to get better at what they actually have to do, in a
structured, organized way, rather than letting everyone improvise.

## What worked

- **Engineers teaching engineers.** The key structural choice: find a partner
  where *engineers* teach *engineers*, walking them through concrete methods
  (e.g. Gastown, [BMAD](../agentic-coding/bmad-method.md)) from "zero to hero." Peer instruction
  creates a **safe space** to exchange, "nerd out," and compare how people apply
  the tools — which matters when nobody is sure they're doing the right thing.
- **In-person kickoff.** Trainings were deliberately run in person, not remote.
  Remote sessions lose attention ("your eyes go everywhere, you're doing ten
  things at once"); bringing people physically together to work on the material
  set the tone.
- **Start with the basics, not the flashy stuff.** They did *not* jump straight
  into "Claude Code, go go go." Covering the fundamentals first "massively
  helped."
- **Mix experience levels in the room.** Forward-leaning early adopters sat
  alongside colleagues doing basic things with GitHub Copilot, so learning flowed
  both directions.

## The psychological angle

A recurring theme: engineers see others "going 200 km an hour" and feel behind.
The shared training reframes that anxiety — the day after a session, people feel
grounded rather than left behind. Making adoption a collective, peer-led activity
turns FOMO into momentum.

This is a concrete instance of the enablement side of [driving
adoption](driving-adoption.md): the constraint on value is rarely the tool, it's
the human workflow and the confidence to change it.
