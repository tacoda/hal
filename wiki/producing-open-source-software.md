Karl Fogel's *Producing Open Source Software: How to Run a Successful Free
Software Project* (2nd ed., rewrite completed 2020) is a practitioner's manual for
the **human side** of open source — not the code, but how successful projects
operate: the expectations of users and developers, and the culture of free
software. It is released under an open copyright and can be read free online or
bought in print from O'Reilly.

## The premise

Running an open project is fundamentally a coordination and communication problem,
not a technical one. Fogel draws on his experience (Subversion, CVS) to describe
what actually makes a volunteer-driven project sustain itself: getting started,
the technical infrastructure that shapes collaboration, communication norms,
managing volunteers, governance and decision-making, money and paid developers in
a volunteer economy, licensing, and the social dynamics that keep — or lose — a
community.

## Recurring themes

- **Communications infrastructure is culture.** The mailing lists, bug trackers,
  version control, and their defaults quietly set the tone and norms of the whole
  project. Choosing and configuring them is a governance act.
- **Transparency and low barriers to participation.** Successful projects make it
  easy for a newcomer to observe how decisions get made and to start contributing;
  opacity kills momentum.
- **Governance and decision-making.** Projects need legible ways to reach
  decisions — from benevolent-dictator to consensus-based models — and to handle
  disagreement without stalling.
- **Managing volunteers and money.** Volunteer motivation is fragile; paid
  developers change the dynamics of a volunteer economy and must be integrated
  carefully.
- **Handling difficult people.** A companion talk by Fogel's collaborators —
  *How Open Source Projects Survive Poisonous People* — covers spotting and
  peacefully defusing individuals who quietly poison a community's atmosphere.

## Relevance beyond OSS

The book's lessons on legible governance, low-friction contribution, and
communication-as-culture map onto internal engineering organizations too — the
same forces that make an open project thrive apply to how a company handles
[adoption at org scale](ai-adoption-at-org-scale.md) and to the coordination
problems Brooks described in [The Mythical Man-Month](the-mythical-man-month.md).
