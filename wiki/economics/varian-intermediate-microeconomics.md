# Intermediate Microeconomics: A Modern Approach

Hal Varian's *Intermediate Microeconomics: A Modern Approach* is the standard
intermediate undergraduate [microeconomics](microeconomics.md) text — the book most
students meet after the survey course (e.g. [Mankiw](mankiw-principles-of-economics.md))
and before graduate-level analysis. Varian, a Berkeley economist who later served as
Google's chief economist, wrote it to teach the modern, model-first way of doing micro:
build economic behavior up from optimization by individual agents, then aggregate.

## The "modern approach"

The distinguishing choice is to treat microeconomics as applied optimization from the
first chapter rather than a catalog of results. The book develops the theory
constructively:

- **Consumer theory.** Budget constraints, preferences and indifference curves, utility,
  and the consumer's constrained maximization problem — yielding individual demand and,
  by aggregation, market demand. Comparative statics (income and substitution effects,
  Slutsky decomposition) show how choices shift as prices and income change.
- **Firm theory.** Technology and production functions, cost minimization, cost curves,
  and profit-maximizing supply.
- **Market structure.** Competitive equilibrium, monopoly, oligopoly, and the welfare
  analysis of market outcomes — where the individual-agent models resolve into the
  aggregate [supply-and-demand](supply-and-demand.md) picture and its efficiency
  properties.
- **Beyond the basics.** Exchange and general equilibrium, externalities and public
  goods, asymmetric information, and an introduction to strategic interaction via
  [game theory](game-theory.md).

## Why it matters

Varian's text is rigorous but stops short of heavy calculus, using geometry and careful
algebra so the economic logic stays visible. Its companion, *Workouts in Intermediate
Microeconomics*, made it a problem-driven course staple. The pedagogy — derive demand and
supply from optimizing agents rather than asserting the curves — is precisely what makes
the intermediate course a bridge to formal economic analysis. Varian also wrote the
graduate-level *Microeconomic Analysis*, the fully mathematical sibling of this book.
