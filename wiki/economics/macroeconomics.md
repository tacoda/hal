# Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies the economy as an aggregate system rather than the choices of
individual buyers and sellers. Where [microeconomics](microeconomics.md) zooms in on a
single market's [supply and demand](supply-and-demand.md), macroeconomics asks about the
sum: how fast is the whole economy growing, how many people are out of work, how quickly
are prices rising, and what can policy do about it. The two views are complementary — the
macro aggregates are built from millions of micro decisions, and the [marginal thinking
and incentives](marginal-thinking-and-incentives.md) that drive individuals still operate
underneath the totals. The founding text of the modern discipline is
[Keynes's General Theory](mankiw-principles-of-economics.md); the standard survey is
[Mankiw's *Principles of Economics*](mankiw-principles-of-economics.md).

## The core aggregates

Three headline numbers define the state of the macroeconomy:

| Measure | What it counts | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **GDP** | Market value of all final goods and services produced in a period | The single best proxy for the size of the economy and the material standard of living |
| **Inflation** | The rate at which the general price level rises | Erodes the purchasing power of [money](money-and-finance.md); a stable, low rate is the usual policy target |
| **Unemployment** | Share of the labor force wanting work but without it | Idle capacity and lost output; a social cost that policy tries to minimize |

GDP can be measured three equivalent ways — as total spending (C + I + G + NX:
consumption, investment, government, net exports), as total income, or as total value
added — because every dollar spent is a dollar earned. **Real** GDP strips out price
changes so that growth reflects more *stuff*, not just higher prices; **nominal** GDP does
not.

## The business cycle

Economies do not grow smoothly. Around the rising long-run trend of [economic
growth](economic-growth.md), output fluctuates in a **business cycle**: expansion, peak,
contraction (recession), trough, and recovery. Recessions are the visible cost of these
fluctuations — unemployment rises, incomes fall, and capacity sits idle.

Cycles are self-reinforcing in the short run: falling spending cuts firms' revenue, which
cuts jobs, which cuts spending again — a [feedback loop](../systems-thinking/feedback-loops.md)
that can amplify a small shock into a deep slump (and, in reverse, a boom into a bubble).
Much of macroeconomic policy is an attempt to damp that loop.

## Aggregate demand and aggregate supply

The workhorse model plots the price level against real output. **Aggregate demand (AD)**
slopes down: a lower price level raises real wealth and spending. **Short-run aggregate
supply (SRAS)** slopes up: higher prices, with wages sticky, make production more
profitable. **Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)** is vertical at the economy's potential
output — in the long run, output is set by real factors (labor, capital, technology, per
[economic growth](economic-growth.md)), not the price level. Shifts in AD move output and
prices in the short run but only prices in the long run; this is the bridge between the
cycle and the trend.

## The two policy levers

- **Fiscal policy** — the government's taxing and spending. In a downturn, higher
  spending or tax cuts raise aggregate demand; the effect is amplified by the
  *multiplier*, since one person's spending is another's income.
- **Monetary policy** — the central bank's control of interest rates and the money
  supply (see [money and finance](money-and-finance.md)). Lower rates make borrowing
  cheaper, encouraging investment and consumption; raising rates cools an overheating
  economy and fights inflation.

## The Keynesian–monetarist debate

The central disagreement of 20th-century macroeconomics was over how much to intervene.

- **Keynesians** argue that demand shortfalls are real and persistent — wages and prices
  are *sticky*, so markets do not clear quickly — and that active fiscal and monetary
  policy should fill the gap. Left alone, an economy can get stuck below potential.
- **Monetarists** (Milton Friedman most prominently) reply that the economy is more
  self-correcting than Keynesians think, that fine-tuning is destabilizing because of long
  and variable lags, and that steady, rule-based control of the money supply is the safer
  course. "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."

Modern practice borrows from both: central banks actively manage rates (a Keynesian
instinct) but anchor policy to explicit inflation targets and rules (a monetarist
instinct). The debate is really about the same [feedback
loops](../systems-thinking/feedback-loops.md) — whether policy stabilizes them or, by
acting too late, becomes another source of oscillation.

## Why it matters

Macroeconomic conditions are the weather that every business and household lives in.
Recessions destroy jobs and firms that would have been viable in normal times; runaway
inflation wipes out savings; sustained [growth](economic-growth.md) is what lifts living
standards across generations. Understanding the aggregates and the levers is what lets a
society aim for full employment and stable prices at once — and understand why that
balance is so hard to hold.
