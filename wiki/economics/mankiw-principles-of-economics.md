# Principles of Economics (Mankiw)

N. Gregory Mankiw's *Principles of Economics* is the standard introductory textbook
for American economics departments — first published in 1997 and now in its tenth
edition. Its ambition is pedagogical rather than novel: to hand a first-year student
the core intuitions of the field in plain language, organized around a small set of
memorable ideas before any formal machinery arrives.

## The Ten Principles

The book's organizing device is the "Ten Principles of Economics," grouped into three
themes that recur throughout the text:

- **How people make decisions.** People face tradeoffs; the cost of a thing is what you
  give up to get it (its [opportunity cost](opportunity-cost-and-scarcity.md)); rational
  actors think at the margin; and people respond to incentives. This is the microfoundation
  of everything that follows — choice under [scarcity](opportunity-cost-and-scarcity.md).
- **How people interact.** Trade can make everyone better off; markets are usually a good
  way to organize economic activity; and governments can sometimes improve market outcomes
  (addressing market failures and inequality).
- **How the economy as a whole works.** A country's standard of living depends on its
  productivity; prices rise when the government prints too much money; and society faces a
  short-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment. These seed the
  [macroeconomics](macroeconomics.md) half of the book.

## Scope and structure

The text moves from these principles into the workhorse tools. On the
[microeconomics](microeconomics.md) side it develops
[supply and demand](supply-and-demand.md), elasticity, consumer and producer surplus,
the welfare effects of taxes and price controls, externalities and public goods, and
the theory of the firm across market structures (competition, monopoly, oligopoly,
monopolistic competition). On the [macroeconomics](macroeconomics.md) side it covers
national income accounting (GDP), the price level and inflation, growth, the loanable
funds market, unemployment, money and banking, and aggregate demand / aggregate supply.

Mankiw's expository choices — the ten principles, heavy use of everyday examples, and
deliberately gentle formalism — are the reason it became the dominant intro text and,
for many economists and policymakers, the shared vocabulary of the discipline. It is a
synthesis of mainstream neoclassical economics as taught, not a research contribution.
