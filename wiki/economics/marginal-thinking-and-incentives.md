# Marginal Thinking and Incentives

If scarcity is economics' founding premise, **marginal analysis** is its core method
and **incentives** are its core explanation. Together they turn "people make choices
under scarcity" into a machine for predicting behaviour.

## Thinking at the margin

A *marginal* change is one small increment — one more hour, one more unit, one more
customer. Most real decisions are not all-or-nothing ("should this firm exist?") but
marginal ("should we make one more?"). The rational rule is simple and general:

> Take an action up to the point where its **marginal benefit (MB) equals its marginal
> cost (MC)**. Keep going while MB > MC; stop before MB < MC.

Examples of the same rule wearing different clothes:

- A firm expands output until the revenue from the last unit equals the cost of making
  it — the profit-maximising condition in [microeconomics](microeconomics.md).
- You keep studying until the extra grade points from one more hour aren't worth the
  sleep you sacrifice — its opportunity cost, per
  [opportunity-cost-and-scarcity](opportunity-cost-and-scarcity.md).
- Diamonds cost more than water not because they are more useful in total, but because
  the *marginal* diamond is scarce while the *marginal* glass of water is abundant. This
  **diamond–water paradox** dissolves the moment you think at the margin rather than in
  totals.

Marginal thinking is the same "set the derivative to zero" logic that
[../linear-optimization/optimization-problems.md](../linear-optimization/optimization-problems.md)
makes explicit: an optimum is where the last unit's gain and cost balance.

### The sunk-cost trap

A **sunk cost** is a cost already incurred and unrecoverable. Because marginal decisions
concern only *future* benefits and costs, **sunk costs should be ignored**. Finishing a
bad movie "because you paid for the ticket," or pouring money into a failing project
"because we've come this far," is the sunk-cost fallacy — letting spent, irrecoverable
resources drive a forward-looking choice. Rational actors ask only: from *here*, does
MB exceed MC?

## Incentives shape behaviour

An **incentive** is anything that rewards or penalises an action at the margin — a
price, a tax, a subsidy, a fine, a bonus, a reputation. The central behavioural claim of
economics is that **people respond to incentives**, often in ways designers don't
intend:

- A tax on sugary drinks raises their marginal cost, so consumption falls.
- Mandating airbags lowers the marginal cost of driving recklessly, so some drivers take
  more risk (the "Peltzman effect") — an incentive working against the policy's goal.
- Paying blood donors can *reduce* supply by crowding out the intrinsic motive to give.

Because incentives operate at the margin, good policy and good management are largely the
craft of aligning marginal rewards with desired outcomes.

## The rational-actor model — and its limits

The engine behind all of this is the **rational-actor model**: agents have stable, ordered
preferences and choose the option that best satisfies them given their constraints and
information. It is a powerful *first approximation* — it yields sharp, testable
predictions and underpins [microeconomics](microeconomics.md) and
[game-theory](game-theory.md).

But it is a model, not a photograph. Real humans use heuristics, are swayed by how choices
are framed, over-weight losses, and fall for exactly the sunk-cost trap the model says to
ignore. Cataloguing and modelling these systematic departures is the project of
[behavioral-economics](behavioral-economics.md). Marginal thinking remains the normative
benchmark — how a rational agent *should* choose — even where behavioural economics shows
how real agents actually do.

## Why it matters

Nearly every applied result in economics is an MB = MC condition in disguise, and nearly
every policy is an attempt to shift someone's marginal incentives. Master these two ideas
and most of [microeconomics](microeconomics.md), and much of
[macroeconomics](macroeconomics.md), becomes a set of variations on a single theme.
