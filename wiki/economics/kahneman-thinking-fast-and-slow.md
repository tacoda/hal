# Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) is a popular synthesis of decades of
research in judgment and decision-making — much of it done with Amos Tversky — that became
the accessible summary of [behavioral economics](behavioral-economics.md). Kahneman, a
psychologist, won the 2002 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics for showing that human choice
systematically departs from the rational, [expected-utility-maximizing](von-neumann-morgenstern-theory-of-games.md)
agent that classical theory assumes.

## Two systems

The book's central metaphor divides thinking into two modes:

- **System 1** is fast, automatic, intuitive, and effortless. It jumps to conclusions,
  reads emotions, and answers easy questions — often by substituting an easier question for
  a hard one.
- **System 2** is slow, deliberate, effortful, and analytic. It handles reasoning that
  System 1 cannot, but it is lazy and easily depleted, so it often ratifies System 1's
  snap judgments rather than checking them.

Most everyday judgment is System 1, and its shortcuts are usually good enough — but they
fail in predictable, systematic ways.

## Heuristics and biases

The middle of the book catalogs the recurring errors that flow from System 1 shortcuts:
**anchoring** (estimates pulled toward an arbitrary starting number), **availability**
(judging probability by how easily examples come to mind), **representativeness** (ignoring
base rates in favor of stereotype-matching), **overconfidence** and the **illusion of
validity**, and the **sunk cost fallacy** (throwing good money after bad to avoid regret).
Statistical intuitions are especially weak — people neglect base rates, misread regression
to the mean ([regression](../statistics/index.md)), and see patterns in noise.

## Prospect theory

The most consequential contribution is **prospect theory** (Kahneman and Tversky, 1979),
the descriptive alternative to expected-utility theory. Its core claims:

- People evaluate outcomes as **gains and losses relative to a reference point**, not as
  final states of wealth.
- **Loss aversion**: losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains.
- The value function is **concave for gains and convex for losses** (diminishing
  sensitivity), and probabilities are weighted nonlinearly — small probabilities
  overweighted, large ones underweighted.

These features explain framing effects, the endowment effect, and many "irrational"
choices that expected-utility theory cannot. The book closes with work on the "two selves"
— the experiencing self versus the remembering self — and how remembered utility diverges
from experienced well-being.
