# The Wealth of Nations

*An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776), by Adam Smith, is
the founding text of modern economics. Written on the eve of industrialization, it asks a
single question — what makes a nation prosperous? — and answers it not with a nation's gold
reserves (the mercantilist view Smith attacks) but with the productive capacity of its
labor. It is the second most cited economics book published before 1950.

## Central ideas

- **The division of labor.** Smith opens with the pin factory: one worker making pins
  alone might produce a handful a day, but ten workers each specializing in one step of the
  process produce tens of thousands. Specialization is the primary engine of productivity
  growth — and it is limited by the extent of the market, since you can only specialize as
  finely as you can sell.
- **The invisible hand.** Individuals pursuing their own gain, guided "as if by an
  invisible hand," are led to promote the general good more effectively than when they
  deliberately try to. Self-interested exchange, not benevolence, coordinates a complex
  economy — "it is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that
  we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."
- **Markets and prices.** Smith distinguishes the *market price* (set by supply and demand
  in the moment) from the *natural price* (the cost of production toward which competition
  drives it). This is the intellectual seed of [supply and demand](supply-and-demand.md)
  and of the idea that competitive markets allocate resources efficiently — later formalized
  as the equal-rate-of-return equilibrium at the heart of resource-allocation theory.
- **Value and labor.** He separates *value in use* from *value in exchange* and grounds
  exchange value in the labor a good commands — an early labor theory of value.
- **The proper role of government.** Smith favors free trade and limited intervention, but
  he assigns the state real duties: national defense, the administration of justice, and
  public works and institutions that markets will not supply on their own.

## Structure

The work spans five books: (I) the division of labor, prices, wages, profit, and rent;
(II) capital, saving, and stock; (III) the historical development of European economies;
(IV) a critique of mercantilism and the physiocrats; and (V) public finance — the revenue
and expenditure of the state.

## Legacy

Smith did not invent every idea in the book, but he assembled them into the first
systematic account of how a market economy works, giving economics its central puzzle: how
decentralized, self-interested choices under [scarcity](opportunity-cost-and-scarcity.md)
can produce coherent, prosperous order without central direction. Every later school —
neoclassical, Keynesian, Austrian — argues with or builds on him.
