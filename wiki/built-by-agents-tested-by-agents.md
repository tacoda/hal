# Built by Agents, Tested by Agents, Trusted by Whom?

A Stanford Law **CodeX** piece responding to StrongDM's February 2026
announcement that its AI team ships software with a charter that **code must not
be written by humans** and **must not be reviewed by humans**. When agents both
build *and* verify the software, the essay asks the question the factory model
leaves open: **trusted by whom?**

## The setup

StrongDM's [software factory](strongdm-software-factory.md) replaces human code
review with agent-run **scenario validation against a Digital Twin Universe** —
agents prove each change satisfies modeled real-world behavior, and that
convergence is the acceptance gate. CTO Justin McCarthy's provocation — *"if you
haven't spent at least $1,000 on tokens today per human engineer, your software
factory has room for improvement"* — signals how far they've removed humans from
the loop.

## The trust gap

The essay's core tension: when the **builder** and the **tester** are the same
kind of system, the traditional independent check disappears. Human review wasn't
only about catching bugs — it was the point where **accountability** attached to a
named person and where an outside party could place trust. A [dark
factory](dark-factory.md) that turns specs into software as a black box relocates
the question from *"is the code correct?"* to *"on what basis, and by whom, is the
whole factory trusted?"* — a legal and governance problem, not just an
engineering one. The agents' validation may be sound, but validation designed and
run by the same organization that builds the code is not the independent
assurance that regulators, customers, and courts have historically relied on.
