# Entrepreneurship and the Lean Startup

Entrepreneurship is the practice of building a new venture under **uncertainty** — where
the product, the customer, and even the problem are hypotheses rather than knowns. This
is what separates it from ordinary management: a manager executes a known plan to hit a
forecast; an entrepreneur runs *experiments* to discover whether a viable business exists
at all. Steve Blank's definition is the sharpest: a startup is a temporary organization
built to *search* for a repeatable, scalable business model. When the search succeeds,
the startup graduates into a company that *executes* that model.

## Startup vs small business

The words are often confused. A **small business** (a restaurant, a consultancy) uses a
proven model in a known market; its goal is a stable, profitable operation, and its
growth is roughly linear. A **startup** is defined by its ambition to find a *new*,
repeatable, and *scalable* model — one where growth can be exponential because serving the
next customer costs far less than the last (see
[business models and unit economics](business-models-and-unit-economics.md)). The
distinction is about the model's novelty and scalability, not the company's size or age.

## The Lean Startup

Eric Ries's *The Lean Startup* — anchored here by [Ries, *The Lean Startup*](ries-lean-startup.md)
— reframes the startup as a machine for turning uncertainty into knowledge as fast and
cheaply as possible. Its core loop is **Build–Measure–Learn**.

```mermaid
flowchart LR
  I[Ideas / hypotheses] --> B[Build: MVP]
  B --> P[Product]
  P --> M[Measure: real customer behavior]
  M --> Da[Data]
  Da --> L[Learn: validated learning]
  L --> I
```

Key ideas:

- **Validated learning.** The unit of progress for a startup is not features shipped or
  hours worked but *learning* — empirically confirming or refuting a hypothesis about the
  business. Everything that does not produce learning is waste.
- **Minimum Viable Product (MVP).** The smallest thing you can build to run the next
  experiment — just enough to get real behavior from real customers. Its purpose is to
  *learn*, not to impress; a landing page or a concierge (manual behind-the-scenes)
  service often qualifies.
- **Innovation accounting.** Because vanity metrics (raw signups, page views) can rise
  while the business goes nowhere, Ries insists on *actionable* metrics — cohort
  retention, conversion, unit economics — that reveal whether the model is actually
  improving.
- **Pivot or persevere.** At each turn of the loop the founder faces a decision: the data
  validates the hypothesis, so *persevere*; or it refutes it, so *pivot* — a structured
  change of strategy (new customer, new problem, new channel) that keeps one foot on what
  has been learned. A pivot is a change in direction without a change in vision.

## Customer development and product–market fit

Blank's **customer development** is the outward-facing complement to Build–Measure–Learn:
"get out of the building" and test your assumptions against actual customers before
scaling. It runs in phases — customer *discovery* (do they have the problem?), *validation*
(will they buy the solution?), *creation* (build demand), and *company-building* (scale the
org). Its foundation is deep understanding of the customer's need — see
[customer empathy and jobs-to-be-done](customer-empathy-and-jobs-to-be-done.md): you are
searching for a job the customer is already trying to get done, not inventing a desire.

**Product–market fit** (Marc Andreessen's term) is the milestone the whole search aims at:
being in a good market with a product that market pulls out of you. Before fit, nothing
else matters and growth is a grind; after it, demand outruns your ability to supply it.
The signals are behavioral — strong retention, organic word-of-mouth, users angry when the
product breaks — not a single number. It is the point at which the startup can safely
switch from searching to scaling and start pouring fuel on
[marketing and positioning](marketing-and-positioning.md) and growth.

## Relationship to disruption

Many of the most valuable startups win not by out-competing incumbents head-on but by
[disruptive innovation](disruptive-innovation.md) — starting in a low-end or new-market
foothold the incumbent is happy to cede, then moving upmarket as the technology improves.
Lean methods are how a resource-poor entrant finds that foothold before running out of
runway.

## Why it matters

The default outcome of a startup is failure, and the dominant cause is building something
nobody wants — spending the whole runway perfecting a product before discovering the
market isn't there. The lean approach attacks exactly this: it drives the *cost of being
wrong* toward zero by learning in small, cheap loops, so that the venture reaches
product–market fit (or a clean pivot) while it still has money in the bank.
