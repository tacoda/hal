# Competing Against Luck (Clayton Christensen)

Clayton Christensen's *Competing Against Luck: The Story of Innovation and Customer Choice*
(2016, with Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, and David Duncan) sets out the **Jobs to Be Done**
theory as an antidote to innovation-by-luck. The argument: most companies innovate by
correlation — collecting data about customers and products and hoping a pattern predicts
success — when what they need is *causation*. The causal mechanism behind a purchase is the
"job" the customer is trying to get done.

## Customers "hire" products for a job

The central metaphor is that people don't simply buy products; they **"hire"** them to make
progress in a particular circumstance. Just as you hire a contractor to build a house or a
lawyer to build a case, you pull a product or service into your life when a job arises.
Understand the job — the progress a person is trying to make in a specific situation — and
you understand why they choose one thing over another, and what they'd fire it for.

## The three dimensions of a job

A job is never purely practical. Every job has:

- a **functional** dimension — the concrete task to accomplish;
- a **social** dimension — how the choice makes the person look to others;
- an **emotional** dimension — how it makes the person feel.

Christensen's canonical example is the fast-food chain that discovered a surprising share
of milkshakes were "hired" in the morning by commuters — not for flavor, but to make a long,
boring drive more bearable and stave off mid-morning hunger. Demographic and product-
attribute analysis missed this entirely; the *circumstance* revealed it. Jobs are also not
the same as asking customers *why* they bought something — the method uncovers the
underlying story and the circumstances common across a group.

Because jobs are tied to circumstances, and circumstances change, the job a product is
hired for can shift over time — which is why innovation is a continuing discipline rather
than a one-time insight.

This book is the definitive treatment of
[customer empathy and jobs-to-be-done](customer-empathy-and-jobs-to-be-done.md), and it
directly reshapes [marketing and positioning](marketing-and-positioning.md): you position a
product around the job it does, not the demographic that buys it. It complements the
disruption theory of [The Innovator's Dilemma](christensen-innovators-dilemma.md) by
supplying the *demand-side* mechanism, connects to consumer-choice ideas in
[economics](../economics/index.md), and guides how [AI businesses](../ai-business/index.md)
frame the real job a customer hires an AI product to do.
