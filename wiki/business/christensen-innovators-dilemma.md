# The Innovator's Dilemma (Clayton Christensen)

Clayton Christensen's *The Innovator's Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to
Fail* (1997) explains a paradox: well-managed, market-leading companies can do everything
that conventional good management prescribes — listen to their best customers, invest in
higher-margin products, allocate capital to the highest returns — and *precisely because of
that discipline* be overtaken by inferior new entrants. The book coined and developed the
concept of **disruptive technology** (later refined to disruptive *innovation*).

## Sustaining vs. disruptive innovation

Christensen distinguishes two kinds of technological change:

- **Sustaining innovations** improve products along the dimensions mainstream customers
  already value. Incumbents almost always win these battles — they have the resources,
  customer relationships, and incentives to push the existing curve.
- **Disruptive innovations** start out *worse* on the mainstream metrics but are cheaper,
  simpler, or more convenient. They take root in low-end or entirely new markets that
  established firms rationally ignore because the margins and volumes are unattractive.

## Why good management dooms incumbents

The dilemma has a structural logic:

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    A[Disruptor enters<br/>low-end / new market] --> B[Incumbent ignores it<br/>low margin, small volume]
    B --> C[Disruptor improves<br/>along S-curve]
    C --> D[Product becomes<br/>good enough]
    D --> E[Disruptor captures<br/>mainstream market]
    E --> F[Incumbent displaced]
```

Value from innovation follows an **S-curve**: early iterations add little, the middle adds
the most, and late iterations plateau. A disruptor working in a niche needs far less
annual revenue than the incumbent and can iterate freely; meanwhile the incumbent's large
customer base keeps demanding more of the *existing* product, so serving it well is the
rational choice — right up until the disruptor's technology becomes "good enough" and
sweeps the mainstream. Christensen's recommendation is that large firms spin up small,
autonomous divisions to pursue disruption internally, insulated from the parent's margin
and volume expectations.

This is the canonical account of [disruptive innovation](disruptive-innovation.md) and a
cornerstone of how [business strategy](business-strategy.md) accounts for technological
change. It reframes [competitive advantage](competitive-advantage.md) as fragile against
new-market entry, and its resource-allocation logic connects to
[industry economics](../economics/index.md). The pattern is acutely relevant to
[AI businesses](../ai-business/index.md), where cheap, initially-crude AI tooling repeatedly
enters at the low end and climbs.
