# Grit

Angela Duckworth, a psychologist, argues that the best predictor of high achievement is
not talent but **grit** — the combination of *passion* and *perseverance* for very
long-term goals. Across settings (West Point cadets, National Spelling Bee finalists,
teachers in tough schools, salespeople), grit forecasts who stays and succeeds better than
talent, IQ, or credentials do.

## Passion + perseverance

Grit is a durable, years-long commitment, not intensity. **Passion** here means consistency
of direction — a top-level goal that organizes lower goals beneath it — rather than fleeting
enthusiasm. **Perseverance** is the resilience to keep working through setbacks, boredom,
and plateaus. Duckworth pictures goals as a hierarchy: gritty people subordinate many
mid- and low-level goals to a single overarching purpose and hold to it over time.

## Grit beats talent — and effort counts twice

Duckworth's core equations show why relying on talent is a mistake:

```
talent × effort = skill
skill  × effort = achievement
```

Effort appears **twice**: it builds skill in the first place, and it turns skill into
real-world achievement. So effort counts twice while talent counts once. Overrating talent
(the "naturalness bias") quietly disparages the sustained effort that actually produces
mastery — a caution that echoes the identity-and-systems view in
[atomic-habits.md](atomic-habits.md).

## Deliberate practice

Grit is expressed through **deliberate practice** — not mere repetition, but a demanding
cycle: set a stretch goal just beyond current ability, concentrate fully, seek immediate and
informative feedback, and reflect and refine. This is how the gritty use their time, and it
connects directly to **mastery** as described in [drive-daniel-pink.md](drive-daniel-pink.md):
effortful, gradual, never fully arrived at.

## The growth-mindset link

Grit is grounded in belief. Duckworth builds explicitly on Carol Dweck's research: a
**growth mindset** — the belief that ability can be developed — sustains the optimistic
self-talk and resilience that let people persevere through failure, whereas a fixed mindset
invites giving up. See [mindset-dweck.md](mindset-dweck.md).

## Growing grit

Duckworth argues grit is not fixed. It grows from the inside out — cultivating **interest**
(discovering and deepening a passion), **practice** (deliberate practice as a daily habit),
**purpose** (connecting the work to others' well-being), and **hope** (a rising-to-the-
occasion resilience) — and from the outside in, through gritty cultures, mentors, and
environments that expect and model perseverance.
