# The Histories (Herodotus)

Herodotus of Halicarnassus wrote *The Histories* in the mid-fifth century BC, and it stands
as the **founding work of history-writing in the Western tradition** — the reason Cicero
called Herodotus "the Father of History." Its ostensible subject is the **Greco-Persian Wars**
of the early fifth century BC, culminating in the Persian invasions repelled by the Greeks at
Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis, and Plataea. But its real scope is far larger: the rise of the
Achaemenid Persian Empire and a wide-ranging survey of the peoples, customs, and geography of
the known world.

## Method and the meaning of *historíē*

The Greek word Herodotus uses for his project, *historíē*, means **"inquiry"** or
"investigation" — and it is from this word that "history" descends. His opening declares his
purpose: to record the great and marvelous deeds of both Greeks and non-Greeks **so that they
will not be forgotten by time**, and to explain **why** the two peoples came into conflict.
This is a genuine methodological departure:

- He gathers evidence through **travel, observation, and interview** across Egypt,
  Mesopotamia, Scythia, and the Aegean, rather than relying on myth or divine genealogy alone.
- He often **names his sources and weighs competing accounts**, sometimes reporting a story
  while explicitly declining to vouch for it ("I am obliged to record the things I am told,
  but I am certainly not required to believe them").
- He seeks **causes** — the human and political origins of events — even as oracles, dreams,
  and divine retribution remain part of his explanatory world.

His famous willingness to include hearsay, ethnographic digression, and tall tales also earned
him, from antiquity onward, the rival title "Father of Lies." That double reputation itself
opens the central questions of the discipline: what counts as evidence, how to handle
testimony, and where the line falls between recording and believing.

## Structure and themes

The nine books (later named for the nine Muses) braid a central war narrative with extended
**ethnographic and geographic digressions** on Egypt, Persia, Scythia, and others. Recurring
themes give the sprawling work its shape: the framing of the conflict as a struggle between
**freedom (Greek self-government) and slavery (Persian autocracy)**; the instability of
fortune and the fall of the proud (*hubris* met by *nemesis*); and the sense that great human
achievements deserve preservation against oblivion.

## Significance

Herodotus effectively **invented history as a prose genre of secular inquiry into the human
past** — distinct from epic poetry and myth. He established durable conventions: naming
sources, comparing versions, explaining causation, and taking foreign cultures seriously as
objects of study rather than mere barbarian foils. Every later development in
[historiography-and-historical-method.md](historiography-and-historical-method.md) — including
the more austere, war-focused method of his younger contemporary Thucydides, often positioned
as history's other founder — writes back to Herodotus. As the anchoring literary source for the
Greco-Persian Wars and the fifth-century Greek world, *The Histories* is also a primary window
onto [classical-antiquity.md](classical-antiquity.md). The text is in the public domain and
freely available (in G. C. Macaulay's translation) via Project Gutenberg.
