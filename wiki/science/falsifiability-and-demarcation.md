# Falsifiability and Demarcation

The **demarcation problem** asks: what distinguishes science from non-science and pseudoscience? The
most influential answer, from Karl Popper, is **falsifiability** — a claim is scientific only if it
could, in principle, be proven wrong by some possible observation. This deceptively simple criterion
reshaped how science understands itself.

## Popper's insight

Popper attacked the intuitive idea that science works by *verifying* theories through accumulating
confirmations. His objection: confirmations are cheap. A theory vague or flexible enough will find
"support" everywhere — every event can be read as fitting it. What is hard, and therefore
informative, is to make a **risky prediction** that could fail. A theory that forbids some
observations sticks its neck out; if the forbidden observation never comes, the theory has genuinely
survived. So the mark of a scientific theory is not how much confirms it but **how much could refute
it**.

- "All swans are white" is scientific: one black swan refutes it.
- "Events happen as the stars ordain" (in its vaguest form) is not: no observation is ruled out, so
  nothing could count against it.

This is why the [scientific method](the-scientific-method.md) centers on tests that risk failure, and
why an **unfalsifiable** claim — however true-sounding — sits outside science.

## Marks of pseudoscience

Falsifiability sharpens a family of warning signs that a body of claims is pseudoscientific:

- **Unfalsifiable or endlessly rescued** — every apparent refutation is absorbed by an ad-hoc excuse
  rather than counting against the theory.
- **Vague, shifting predictions** — after the fact it "explains" anything; before the fact it commits
  to nothing checkable.
- **Reliance on confirmation and anecdote**, avoidance of controlled tests and disconfirming
  evidence (**confirmation bias** institutionalized).
- **Isolation from [peer scrutiny](scientific-community-and-peer-review.md)** and hostility to
  [replication](uncertainty-error-and-reproducibility.md).
- **No self-correction** — the claims don't change as evidence accumulates.

Carl Sagan's [baloney-detection kit](sagan-demon-haunted-world.md) is a practical toolkit built
around exactly these signs.

## The criterion's own limits

Falsifiability is powerful but not the last word, and later philosophers qualified it:

- **The Duhem–Quine thesis** — a hypothesis is never tested alone but always bundled with auxiliary
  assumptions (that the instrument works, background conditions hold). A failed prediction shows
  *something* in the bundle is wrong, not necessarily the target hypothesis, so falsification is
  rarely clean.
- **Degrees, not a switch** — real theories are not simply falsifiable or not; they are defended,
  adjusted, and sometimes rightly held onto through anomalies during [normal
  science](paradigms-and-scientific-revolutions.md).
- **Some sciences don't fit neatly** — historical and observational sciences (evolution, cosmology)
  make risky predictions but not by lab falsification, showing demarcation is more a cluster of
  features than one rule.

Because of these limits, most philosophers now treat demarcation as drawn by a *combination* of
marks — testability, self-correction, openness to scrutiny, explanatory fertility — rather than by
falsifiability alone.

## Why it matters

Falsifiability is the single most useful idea for telling robust science from impressive-sounding
nonsense. Asking "what would prove this wrong, and has anyone tried?" cuts through pseudoscience,
conspiracy thinking, and untestable claims faster than any other question — the everyday payoff of
[science as a way of thinking](science-and-society.md).
