# The Logic of Scientific Discovery

Karl Popper (German 1934 as *Logik der Forschung*; English 1959). The foundational statement of
**falsificationism** — the view that what makes a theory scientific is not that it can be confirmed
but that it can be **refuted**. It reframed the philosophy of science and gave working scientists
their most portable criterion for distinguishing science from non-science.

## The argument

Popper's target is the **problem of induction**: no number of confirming observations can prove a
universal law, since the next case might break it. He turns the asymmetry to advantage — a single
counterexample *can* decisively refute a universal claim. So science should proceed not by piling up
confirmations but by advancing **bold, falsifiable conjectures** and subjecting them to severe
attempts at **refutation**. Theories that survive genuine tests are *corroborated* (tentatively
trusted), never proven.

From this follows his solution to the **demarcation problem**: a theory is scientific only if it
forbids something — if some possible observation would count against it. Theories compatible with
*every* possible outcome (he cited some forms of astrology, and psychoanalytic and Marxist theory as
he encountered them) explain too much and risk nothing, and so are not scientific, however
interesting.

## Significance and limits

Falsifiability became the single most influential idea about scientific method and remains an
excellent first-pass test ("what would prove this wrong?"). Its limits are equally important: the
**Duhem–Quine thesis** shows hypotheses are tested only in bundles with auxiliary assumptions, so
falsification is rarely clean; and [Kuhn](kuhn-structure-of-scientific-revolutions.md) argued that
scientists rightly cling to productive paradigms through anomalies rather than abandoning them at the
first refutation. Modern demarcation treats falsifiability as one central mark among several.
