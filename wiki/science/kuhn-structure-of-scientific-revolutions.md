# The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

Thomas S. Kuhn (1962; University of Chicago Press). One of the most cited and influential academic
books of the twentieth century, it overturned the received view of science as a steady accumulation
of truths and replaced it with a picture of alternating stability and upheaval. It gave the language
of **paradigms** and **paradigm shifts** to science and to the wider culture.

## The argument

Kuhn distinguishes **normal science** — routine puzzle-solving within an accepted **paradigm** (the
shared framework of assumptions, methods, and exemplary problems that defines a scientific
community) — from **revolutionary science**. Normal science does not seek novelty; it articulates and
extends the paradigm, treating its fundamentals as settled. Over time it produces **anomalies**,
findings the paradigm cannot accommodate. When anomalies accumulate and resist, the field enters
**crisis**, and eventually a **scientific revolution** replaces the old paradigm with a new one.

Kuhn's provocative claims about these revolutions:

- Progress across a revolution is **not cleanly cumulative** — the new paradigm reframes what
  questions and facts matter, discarding some rather than answering them.
- **Incommensurability** — competing paradigms lack a fully neutral common measure; their key terms
  and standards differ, so the choice between them isn't settled by logic and data alone.
- Paradigm change resembles a **gestalt switch** and has a generational, social character.

## Influence and controversy

The book reshaped the philosophy and history of science and seeded [science and technology
studies](../sociology/index.md). "Paradigm shift" escaped into everyday speech. It was also
controversial: critics charged that incommensurability made science look irrational or merely
sociological. Kuhn resisted that reading, and the mature consensus is a middle path — revolutions are
driven by genuine explanatory and predictive gains, even if not by the tidy
[falsification](falsifiability-and-demarcation.md) Popper described.
