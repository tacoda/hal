# Science and Society

Science is not done in a vacuum. It is funded, prioritized, applied, regulated, and contested by the
society around it, and its findings feed back to reshape that society through technology and policy.
This note covers the human side of the enterprise: the values embedded in scientific choices, the
ethics of research, the science–technology relationship, and the modern problem of distinguishing
real science from its counterfeits in public life.

## Values in science

The ideal of a wholly value-free science is a useful fiction with real limits. Method should be
neutral — evidence and logic don't care who's asking — but **choices around the method** are shaped
by values: which problems get funded, which questions seem important, how risks and benefits are
weighed, how findings are framed. Acknowledging this is not relativism; it's realism. The safeguards
are transparency about assumptions and funding, diverse participation (so blind spots differ), and
the [community scrutiny](scientific-community-and-peer-review.md) that keeps *conclusions* answerable
to evidence even when *agendas* vary.

## Research ethics

Because science acts on people, animals, and the environment, it operates under ethical constraints
built largely in response to past abuses (the Nuremberg Code and Belmont Report after wartime and
mid-century atrocities). Core principles:

- **Informed consent** — human subjects must understand and freely agree to participate.
- **Beneficence and non-maleficence** — maximize benefit, minimize harm; justify risk.
- **Justice** — distribute the burdens and benefits of research fairly, not on the vulnerable alone.
- **Integrity** — honesty in data and reporting; fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism are the
  cardinal sins, policed by the community and by [retraction](uncertainty-error-and-reproducibility.md).

Institutional review boards (ethics committees) enforce these before research proceeds.

## Science and technology

Science and technology are distinct but entangled. **Science** seeks understanding; **technology** —
and [engineering](../engineering/index.md) — applies it to make things work. The relationship runs
both ways: science enables new technology (electromagnetism → electronics), and technology enables
new science (the telescope, the microscope, the particle accelerator, computational
[simulation](models-and-theories-in-science.md)). Crucially, technology is **dual-use** and
value-laden in application even when the underlying science is neutral — the same nuclear physics
yields reactors and weapons. Deciding what to *build* is a social and ethical question science can
inform but not settle.

## The public understanding of science

A scientifically literate public is a practical necessity, because citizens vote on and live with
science-shaped decisions (vaccines, climate, AI, energy). Two chronic difficulties:

- **Communicating uncertainty.** Science speaks in probabilities and provisional conclusions; public
  discourse craves certainty. "Scientists changed their minds" is often *science working*
  (updating on evidence), not failure — but it reads as untrustworthy if the provisional nature was
  never explained.
- **Misinformation and manufactured doubt.** [Pseudoscience](falsifiability-and-demarcation.md),
  cherry-picked data, false balance, and deliberate doubt-mongering (as with tobacco and climate)
  exploit the gap between how science actually works and how the public imagines it. The defenses are
  the skills this whole folder teaches — demanding evidence, checking for
  [controls](experiments-and-controls.md) and [replication](uncertainty-error-and-reproducibility.md),
  distinguishing [correlation from causation](correlation-and-causation.md), and asking what would
  falsify a claim. Carl Sagan's [baloney-detection kit](sagan-demon-haunted-world.md) is the classic
  popular toolkit.

## Why it matters

The most consequential decisions of the era are science-entangled, and they are made by societies,
not laboratories. Understanding how values, ethics, technology, and communication shape the use of
science — and how to defend genuine science against its imitations — is where scientific literacy
stops being academic and becomes civic. It connects to [AI governance](../ai-governance/index.md),
[political science](../political-science/index.md), and [sociology](../sociology/index.md).
