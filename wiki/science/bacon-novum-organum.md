# Novum Organum

Francis Bacon (1620). A founding document of the scientific method and of modern **empiricism**. Its
title ("New Instrument") signals its ambition: to replace Aristotle's *Organon* — deductive logic
from accepted premises — with a new method for producing knowledge, grounded in systematic
observation and experiment. Bacon is often called the prophet of the scientific revolution for laying
out, in program form, how science ought to work.

## The argument

Bacon attacks the science of his day for spinning grand systems out of a few observations and
inherited authority. In its place he proposes disciplined **induction**: gather many careful
observations, organize them, and ascend cautiously to general laws — testing along the way — rather
than leaping to sweeping conclusions. Knowledge should be built *upward* from evidence, not deduced
*downward* from dogma.

His most enduring contribution is the diagnosis of systematic biases in human cognition, the **Idols
of the Mind**, that corrupt inquiry:

- **Idols of the Tribe** — biases inherent in human nature (seeing more order than exists, being
  swayed by what confirms our expectations — a strikingly early statement of
  [confirmation bias](scientific-reasoning.md)).
- **Idols of the Cave** — the individual's personal quirks, upbringing, and pet ideas.
- **Idols of the Marketplace** — confusions bred by language, where loose or empty words mislead
  thought.
- **Idols of the Theatre** — dogmas absorbed from philosophical systems and tradition received as if
  staged.

Recognizing and correcting for these idols is, for Bacon, a precondition of honest science — an
ancestor of everything from [blinding and controls](experiments-and-controls.md) to
[peer review](scientific-community-and-peer-review.md).

## Significance

Bacon didn't do much experimental science himself and his specific method is dated, but the *spirit*
he articulated — knowledge from evidence, distrust of authority, awareness of the mind's biases, and
science as a collective, cumulative, practical project for "the relief of man's estate" — became the
ethos of the modern scientific enterprise. It underwrites the HAL notes on
[empiricism and evidence](empiricism-and-scientific-evidence.md), [the scientific
method](the-scientific-method.md), and [scientific reasoning](scientific-reasoning.md).
