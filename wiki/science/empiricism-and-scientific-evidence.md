# Empiricism and Scientific Evidence

**Empiricism** is the principle that knowledge of the world comes from sensory experience —
observation and evidence — rather than from pure reason, authority, or revelation. It is the
philosophical bedrock of science: a scientific claim is worth only as much as the evidence that can
be marshaled for or against it. This note is about what *counts* as good evidence and why raw
observation is never quite as simple as it looks.

## Empiricism versus rationalism

The classical contrast is with **rationalism**, the view that significant knowledge can be reached
by reason alone (as in [mathematics](../math/index.md), where truths are *proved*, not observed).
Science is thoroughly empiricist about the natural world: however elegant a hypothesis, it stands or
falls on evidence. This is why the [formal sciences](what-is-science.md) — which *are* validated by
proof — sit apart from the empirical natural and social sciences. In practice science blends both:
reason builds and connects hypotheses; experience judges them.

## What makes evidence good

Not all observations carry equal weight. Strong scientific evidence tends to be:

- **Relevant** — it bears directly on the hypothesis, ideally on a risky prediction the hypothesis
  makes.
- **Reliable / reproducible** — independent observers, using the same methods, get the same result
  (see [reproducibility](uncertainty-error-and-reproducibility.md)).
- **Controlled** — rival explanations have been ruled out by [design](experiments-and-controls.md),
  so the observed effect can be attributed to the proposed cause.
- **Quantified** — expressed with [units and stated uncertainty](observation-and-measurement.md),
  not vague impression.
- **Representative** — drawn from a sample that actually reflects the population or conditions the
  claim is about, guarding against selection bias.

A single striking observation is weak; a **convergence** of independent lines of evidence
("consilience") is strong. Much of science's confidence in, say, evolution or climate change comes
not from one experiment but from many disciplines pointing the same way.

## Theory-ladenness: why observation isn't neutral

A subtle but important lesson from the philosophy of science: observation is **theory-laden**. What
scientists notice, how they interpret an instrument reading, even which measurements seem worth
taking, are all shaped by the theories they already hold. A blip on a screen is "a particle track"
only within a theoretical framework. This does not make science subjective — the discipline of
[controlled testing](experiments-and-controls.md) and [independent
replication](scientific-community-and-peer-review.md) keeps observation honest — but it means the
naïve picture of the mind as a blank recorder of pure facts is wrong. It also helps explain why deep
change can require a [paradigm shift](paradigms-and-scientific-revolutions.md), not just more data.

## Evidence and inference

Evidence never speaks for itself; it must be *interpreted* through
[reasoning](scientific-reasoning.md). The same data can support different conclusions, so scientists
weigh which hypothesis the evidence favors most — **inference to the best explanation**. The strength
of a conclusion is a joint product of the quality of the evidence and the soundness of the inference
drawn from it.

## Why it matters

Empiricism is what makes science accountable to reality rather than to opinion. Understanding what
separates strong evidence from weak — controls, reproducibility, quantified uncertainty,
convergence — is the practical core of scientific literacy and the best defense against
[misinformation and pseudoscience](science-and-society.md).
