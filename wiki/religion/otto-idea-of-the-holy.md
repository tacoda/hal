# The Idea of the Holy

Rudolf Otto's *The Idea of the Holy* (*Das Heilige*, 1917) is a landmark of the
phenomenology of religion and the source of one of the discipline's most durable concepts:
the **numinous**. Otto argues that at the core of religion lies a distinctive, irreducible
experience that cannot be reduced to ethics, reason, or feeling in any ordinary sense.

## The argument

Otto observes that the word "holy" is usually taken to mean *moral perfection*. He grants
that the holy has come to carry a moral meaning, but insists this ethical sense is a later
overlay on something more basic and *non-rational*. To isolate that residual, pre-ethical
core he coins the term **numinous** (from the Latin *numen*, "divine power"). The numinous
is the specifically religious element of an experience — a felt encounter with what Otto
calls the **"wholly other"** (*ganz Andere*), a reality qualitatively unlike anything in
ordinary experience and irreducible to any other concept.

Because it is *sui generis*, the numinous cannot be strictly defined; Otto can only point
to it and try to evoke it in a reader who has felt something like it. He characterizes it
through the formula **mysterium tremendum et fascinans** — the numinous experience has
three intertwined moments:

- **Mysterium** — the sense of encountering an overwhelming *mystery*, the wholly other,
  before which ordinary understanding fails and one falls into stupor and awe.
- **Tremendum** — the element of *dread* or "religious awe": the overpowering majesty and
  energy of the numinous object, which reduces the person to the feeling of being a mere
  creature ("creature-feeling," the sense of one's own nothingness before an absolute
  power).
- **Fascinans** — the paradoxical, simultaneous *attraction*: the numinous also fascinates,
  entrances, and allures, drawing the person toward it even as it terrifies.

Otto holds that the capacity to receive the numinous is a **religious *a priori*** — a
built-in disposition of the human mind, awakened rather than created by experience.

## Method and significance

Otto's project is **anti-reductive**: he defends the autonomy of religious experience
against attempts to explain it away as disguised morality, psychology, or social function,
and thereby helps found the "religion as *sui generis*" tradition in religious studies. The
numinous is a phenomenological account of the *feeling-content* of an encounter with the
holy, complementing Otto's own view that this core must then be schematized by rational and
moral concepts to become a full religion.

The book directly shaped [Mircea Eliade](eliade-sacred-and-profane.md), whose analysis of
[the sacred and the profane](the-sacred-and-the-profane.md) presupposes Otto's numinous as
the felt quality of the sacred, and it stands as a central document in the study of
[religious experience and mysticism](religious-experience-and-mysticism.md) alongside
William James's psychological account. It occupies the phenomenological pole within the
broader [theories of religion](theories-of-religion.md).
