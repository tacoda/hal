# The Elementary Forms of Religious Life

Émile Durkheim's *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (*Les formes élémentaires de la
vie religieuse*, 1912) is the founding text of the sociology of religion and Durkheim's
final major work. It argues that religion is a fundamentally **social** phenomenon and that,
in worshipping the divine, a society is in effect worshipping itself.

## The argument

Durkheim seeks the *elementary* — the simplest, most basic — form of religious life, on the
premise that studying religion in its most rudimentary case will expose the essential
features hidden beneath the elaborations of the great world faiths. He takes as his case
study **totemism** among Aboriginal Australian clans, which he (following the ethnography of
his day) treats as the most primitive religion available.

His famous definition frames religion around a binary classification of the world:

- Every religion divides all things into two mutually exclusive domains — the **sacred**
  and the **profane**. The sacred is set apart and forbidden, surrounded by prohibitions;
  the profane is the domain of ordinary utility. This distinction, not belief in gods or
  the supernatural, is the defining mark of religion.
- Religion is then "a unified system of beliefs and practices relative to sacred
  things... which unite into one single moral community called a Church all those who
  adhere to them." The social, collective character is built into the definition. This
  binary is the sociological root of what later phenomenologists reframe as
  [the sacred and the profane](the-sacred-and-the-profane.md).

The heart of the analysis is the totem. Durkheim observes that the totemic emblem is
treated as more sacred than either the animal it depicts or the individual clan members —
which tells him the real object of worship is neither the beast nor the person but the
**emblem itself**, and behind it, the clan. His conclusion: **the totem is the clan, and
the god of the clan is the clan divinized.** Religion is society representing itself to
itself in symbolic form.

The mechanism is **collective effervescence**. When a dispersed clan gathers for ritual,
the heightened, contagious emotional energy of the assembled group generates a felt sense
of a power greater than any individual — a force that is genuinely real (it is the force of
society) but that participants misrecognize as a supernatural presence. Ritual, then, does
not merely express beliefs; it periodically *regenerates* the collective solidarity on
which the society depends.

## Method and significance

Durkheim's move is **functionalist and reductive**: the truth of religion lies not in its
doctrines but in the social solidarity it produces and expresses. He also advances a bold
epistemological claim — that the basic categories of human thought (time, space,
causation, class) originate in social organization, so that religion is the womb of both
morality and knowledge.

The book is a cornerstone of [theories of religion](theories-of-religion.md), the
paradigmatic *social* account against which individualist (James) and phenomenological
(Otto, Eliade) accounts are measured. It extends the concern with social solidarity
Durkheim developed in [The Division of Labour in Society](../sociology/durkheim-division-of-labor.md),
now locating the source of the collective conscience in ritual life. Its themes of emblem,
rite, and collective symbol feed directly into
[myth, ritual, and symbol](myth-ritual-and-symbol.md) and the anthropology of
[ritual, symbolism, and religion](../anthropology/ritual-symbolism-and-religion.md).
