# The World's Religions

Huston Smith's *The World's Religions* — first published in 1958 as *The Religions of Man*
and substantially revised under its current title in 1991 — is the best-selling classic
survey of the major religious traditions and a standard introductory textbook for
university courses in world religions. It is the anchor case of the sympathetic,
appreciative approach to the [comparative study of religion and world
traditions](comparative-religion-and-world-traditions.md).

## The argument and approach

The book grew out of a television series Smith presented, then expanded into ten chapters:
an introduction, one chapter each on **Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, Daoism (Taoism),
Islam, Judaism, and Christianity**, a chapter on the "primal" (oral, indigenous)
religions, and a closing chapter on the relations among the traditions.

Smith's method is deliberately **sympathetic** rather than critical or reductive. He aims
to present each tradition *at its best* — as its most thoughtful and committed adherents
understand and live it — rather than cataloguing its historical failures, sectarian
disputes, or institutional abuses. He is explicit that he is describing the *religions as
values and visions of the good life*, not writing a history of religious institutions or a
sociological analysis of power. The reader is invited to enter each tradition's world and
grasp what it feels like from the inside.

Underlying the survey is Smith's **perennialist** conviction: that beneath the genuine and
important differences of doctrine and practice, the great traditions converge on shared
"wisdom" about the human situation and its ultimate ground. He treats the world's
religions as complementary windows onto a single reality rather than as competing and
mutually exclusive claims. Where these traditions are traced by region, they map onto the
[Abrahamic traditions](abrahamic-traditions.md) (Judaism, Christianity, Islam) and the
[dharmic and Eastern traditions](dharmic-and-eastern-traditions.md) (Hinduism, Buddhism,
Confucianism, Daoism).

## Significance and critique

The book's enduring appeal lies in its clarity, warmth, and readability; it has introduced
generations of students to traditions outside their own and modeled a posture of respectful
attention. In the discipline of religious studies it stands for the *humanistic,
insider-sympathetic* pole, in contrast to the explanatory [theories of
religion](theories-of-religion.md) that seek to reduce religion to social, psychological,
or evolutionary causes (Durkheim, Freud, Frazer).

That same sympathetic stance is the basis of the standard critique. Scholars note that by
presenting each tradition "at its best," Smith idealizes, harmonizes, and de-historicizes
his subjects — smoothing over internal conflict, doctrinal incompatibility, and the messy
political realities of lived religion, and importing a perennialist thesis that many
practitioners and scholars would reject. Read critically, it is both an excellent map of
the traditions' self-understandings and a case study in the assumptions of mid-century
comparative religion.
