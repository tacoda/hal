# The Varieties of Religious Experience

William James's *The Varieties of Religious Experience: A Study in Human Nature*
(1902) collects the Gifford Lectures he delivered at the University of Edinburgh in
1901–1902. It is a foundational text of the psychology of religion and remains one of
the most cited works in the academic study of [religious experience and
mysticism](religious-experience-and-mysticism.md).

## The argument

James deliberately narrows his object of study. He sets aside theology, doctrine, and
institutions, and treats them as *second-hand* religion — vehicles that transmit
insights originally won by others. His subject is instead **first-hand, personal
religious experience**: "the feelings, acts, and experiences of individual men in their
solitude, so far as they apprehend themselves to stand in relation to whatever they may
consider the divine." He examines these as psychological facts, using a large collection
of confessional documents (conversion narratives, mystical reports, testimonies of saints
and ordinary believers) as raw data.

Several distinctions organize the book:

- **The healthy-minded** vs. **the sick soul.** The healthy-minded temperament
  experiences the world as fundamentally good and minimizes evil; the sick soul cannot
  escape the reality of evil, suffering, and finitude. James presents this as
  value-neutral but plainly favors the sick soul as the deeper religious type — the
  melancholy testimony in the relevant lectures is his own, given anonymously.
- **The divided self and its unification.** Religious conversion, treated
  psychologically, is the process by which a divided, unhappy self becomes unified and at
  peace, whether gradually or in a sudden crisis. James is agnostic about the metaphysical
  source of this change while insisting the transformation is real.
- **Mysticism.** James identifies four marks of mystical states: *ineffability* (they
  resist description), a *noetic quality* (they feel like knowledge), *transiency*, and
  *passivity*. He treats mystical states as authoritative for the person who has them, but
  not binding on outsiders.
- **Saintliness** and its practical fruits — the ethical and characterological changes
  religion produces in a life.

## Method and significance

James's stance is **empirical and pragmatic**: religion is to be judged not by its origin
or its metaphysics but by its *fruits for life* — the "pragmatic" test he would develop
fully in *Pragmatism* (1907). He refuses "medical materialism," the reductive move of
dismissing a religious experience because it can be correlated with a nervous condition;
the value of an experience is independent of its physiological cause. He concludes with a
tentative hypothesis that the sense of contact with a "More" beyond the self, reported so
consistently across traditions, points to something real in human nature even if its
theological interpretation stays open.

The book effectively founded the modern psychology of religion, treating personal faith
as legitimate empirical data, and it anticipates later phenomenological approaches to
[the sacred and the profane](the-sacred-and-the-profane.md) — Rudolf Otto and Mircea
Eliade both work downstream of James's insistence that religious experience is *sui
generis* and cannot be explained away. It anchors [theories of
religion](theories-of-religion.md) on the psychological-individualist side, in contrast to
Durkheim's social theory. Methodologically it extends James's own broader project in
[psychology](../psychology/james-principles-of-psychology.md).
