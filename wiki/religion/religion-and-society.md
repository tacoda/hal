# Religion and Society

Religion is not only a matter of private belief and experience (see
[religious-experience-and-mysticism](religious-experience-and-mysticism.md)); it is a
powerful **social force** that organizes communities, legitimates authority, and shapes
conflict and cooperation. The sociology of religion studies these dynamics descriptively and
comparatively, asking what religion *does* in societies rather than whether its claims are
true. Its classical founders set the terms: Émile Durkheim saw religion as the social binding
that generates solidarity and marks the sacred; Max Weber traced how religious ideas (the
Protestant ethic) can shape economic and social life; Karl Marx read religion as both an
expression of and a consolation for material suffering. These remain the reference points for
the topics below.

## Religion as a social institution

Religion is one of the enduring **social institutions** — a durable, patterned set of roles,
norms, and organizations meeting recurrent social needs, alongside family, economy, and state
(see [../sociology/social-institutions.md](../sociology/social-institutions.md)). As an
institution it does identifiable social work: it fosters **solidarity** and collective
identity (Durkheim's core insight — worship binds a group to itself as much as to its god); it
provides **legitimation**, sanctifying rulers, laws, and social arrangements; it offers
**meaning and consolation** in the face of suffering and death; and it exercises **social
control** through shared norms. Sociologists also classify religious organizations along a
spectrum — *church* (broad, established, into which one is born), *sect* (smaller, stricter,
higher-tension breakaway), *denomination*, and *cult/new religious movement* — a typology
descended from Weber and Ernst Troeltsch.

## Civil religion

**Civil religion** (a term Robert Bellah drew from Rousseau and applied to the United States)
names the quasi-religious dimension of national life: shared myths, sacred symbols, rituals,
and beliefs that sacralize the political community itself. Founding narratives, revered
documents, memorial days, oaths, and the veneration of martyred leaders function much as
religious symbols do (see [myth-ritual-and-symbol](myth-ritual-and-symbol.md)), binding
citizens into a moral community independent of any particular church. Civil religion shows
that the sacred can attach to secular objects — nation, revolution, constitution — and helps
explain why even highly secularized societies retain ritual and myth.

## Religion and politics

Religion and political power interact continuously, and traditions arrange the relationship
differently: from **theocracy** (religious authority governs directly), through
**establishment** (an official state religion), to constitutional **secularism** and the
separation of religious and state authority. Religion can legitimate a regime or mobilize
opposition to it; it has fueled both conservative defense of the social order and radical
movements for justice (abolition, anticolonial struggles, liberation theology, civil-rights
mobilization). These questions belong equally to political science (see
[../political-science/index.md](../political-science/index.md)) and connect to the broader
debate over secularization in
[religion-science-and-secularism](religion-science-and-secularism.md).

## Fundamentalism and revival

**Fundamentalism** is best understood not as mere traditionalism but as a *modern* reaction —
a militant defense of what adherents take to be essential truths (often including scriptural
inerrancy; see [scripture-and-sacred-texts](scripture-and-sacred-texts.md)) against perceived
threats from secular modernity, pluralism, and historical-critical scholarship. It appears
across traditions, not only in Christianity, and is characteristically selective, embracing
modern technology and organization while rejecting modern values it opposes. **Revival**
movements — periodic surges of intensified religious fervor and recommitment — recur widely
and remind analysts that secularization is neither uniform nor irreversible.

## New religious movements

**New religious movements** (NRMs), the neutral scholarly term preferred over the pejorative
"cult," are religions that have emerged recently or entered a society as novelties. Studying
them descriptively lets scholars watch religious formation in real time: charismatic
authority, the drawing of tight boundaries, conversion and defection, routinization, and the
often fraught relationship with surrounding society. NRMs raise the recurring analytic
question of what distinguishes a "religion" from a "cult" — a distinction that is frequently
social and political rather than theological (see [what-is-religion](what-is-religion.md)).

## Religion, violence, and peace

Religion's relation to conflict is genuinely two-sided, and honest scholarship resists both
apologetics and blanket blame.

- **Religion and violence**: religious identity, myths, and symbols can sacralize conflict,
  raise its stakes to the absolute, mark in-group and out-group, and legitimate war
  (holy war, crusade, martyrdom). Yet analysts caution against reading conflicts as *purely*
  religious; religion typically intertwines with ethnicity, territory, economics, and
  political grievance, and can serve as a marker or amplifier of disputes whose roots lie
  elsewhere.
- **Religion and peace**: the same traditions supply powerful resources for nonviolence,
  reconciliation, forgiveness, and social justice — from principled pacifism to
  religiously grounded movements for civil rights and truth-and-reconciliation processes.

The sober comparative conclusion is that religion is an **amplifier**: it intensifies whatever
human projects it is enlisted into, for war or for peace, which is precisely why its social
force merits careful study.
