# The Sacred and the Profane

Mircea Eliade's *The Sacred and the Profane: The Nature of Religion* (1957; English
translation 1959) is a compact, programmatic statement of his phenomenology of religion.
It is the most accessible entry point to Eliade's larger comparative project — the "history
of religions" school he led at the University of Chicago — and a canonical account of what
it means for something to be experienced as [the sacred and the
profane](the-sacred-and-the-profane.md).

## The argument

Eliade's organizing claim is that human beings inhabit two radically different modes of
experience: the **sacred** and the **profane**. The profane is ordinary, homogeneous,
neutral space and time — the world of everyday, undifferentiated experience. The sacred is
qualitatively other: charged, real, oriented, saturated with being. Religion, for Eliade,
is fundamentally the drama of how the sacred breaks into and structures the profane world.

The key concept is the **hierophany** (from Greek, "the sacred shows itself"): any
manifestation of the sacred in an object, place, person, or event. A stone, a tree, or a
mountain becomes sacred not by ceasing to be a stone or tree but by *revealing* something
wholly other through itself. The paradox of the hierophany — the sacred showing itself
*in and through* the profane — is the engine of Eliade's whole analysis.

He then works through the consequences across the dimensions of lived experience:

- **Sacred space.** For *homo religiosus* (religious humanity), space is not uniform. A
  hierophany founds a fixed point, a *center* — the *axis mundi* (world axis: cosmic
  mountain, tree, pillar, or temple) around which the world becomes orientable and real.
  To found a settlement or a temple is to repeat the gods' original act of creating an
  ordered cosmos out of chaos.
- **Sacred time.** Ordinary time is irreversible and profane; sacred time is cyclical and
  reversible, recovered through ritual and festival. By re-enacting the myths of origin,
  religious people abolish profane duration and return to the primordial "time of the
  beginnings" — Eliade's theme of the *eternal return*.
- **The sacralized cosmos** and **human existence** — nature, the body, work, and life's
  passages are all read as openings onto the sacred.

## Method and significance

Eliade is a **phenomenologist**: he brackets the question of whether the sacred is *real*
and studies instead how it appears to religious consciousness, insisting the sacred is an
*irreducible* structure of that consciousness, not a disguised expression of something else
(society, neurosis, economics). This puts him squarely against the reductive
[theories of religion](theories-of-religion.md) of Durkheim, Marx, and Freud, and directly
downstream of Rudolf Otto, whose "numinous" he presupposes and extends. His approach is
comparative and morphological, drawing symbols and patterns from across cultures.

The book has been enormously influential and heavily criticized. Its power is in giving
readers a vocabulary — hierophany, sacred space and time, *axis mundi*, *homo religiosus* —
for the [comparative study of religion and world traditions](comparative-religion-and-world-traditions.md)
and for [myth, ritual, and symbol](myth-ritual-and-symbol.md). Critics charge that Eliade
generalizes too freely across cultures, smooths over historical difference, and quietly
imports a theological premise (that the sacred is genuinely *sui generis*) under a
scholarly guise. It connects to anthropological treatments of the same terrain in
[ritual, symbolism, and religion](../anthropology/ritual-symbolism-and-religion.md).
