# AI Adoption at Org Scale — and the Backlog You Run Out Of

Cecilia Borg, an interim CTO, interviewed at AI Fokus 2026 (AI Native Dev /
Tessl) in Stockholm, describes driving AI adoption at Acast — a **remote-first,
distributed org with ~110 people in product development**, spread across many
teams and not co-located. The challenge she took on: how do you get full
penetration of AI adoption across such an org, given the fear and skepticism many
developers feel, and the need to keep product, design, and engineering
collaborating?

## Seeing is believing — but skepticism is often earned

The turning point is social proof: developers start believing when they see
*other* developers succeed with the tools. But she is careful not to dismiss the
holdouts. A lot of the skepticism from **senior developers is well earned**:

- The **technical debt mounting now is very real** — bloated code, moving
  bottlenecks.
- There's a **pride** dimension: senior engineers who were fast at writing code
  by hand don't enjoy being "deprived of that by bots."
- If you **don't tame your bots** on testing and code review, you inherit a pile
  of toil you didn't want — the work just shifts, it doesn't disappear.

This is the "backlog you run out of" idea: AI clears the easy backlog fast, and
what's left is the hard, judgment-heavy work — plus the new debt the bots
generated. The bottleneck **moves**; it doesn't vanish.

## Her organizational approach

- **Name the hype honestly.** There may be real productivity upside, but AI is
  *also* a hype cycle — so talk openly about the **risks** and how to attack
  them, then **follow up and move in small steps.**
- **Listen and take fear seriously.** Take what people are scared about *for
  real*, rather than steamrolling skeptics.
- **Provide guiding principles.** A big problem is simply *not knowing how to use
  the tool* — she likens it to "eating with your non-dominant hand": at first
  everything feels bad. Give people explicit principles for how to approach it.
- **Give time to learn.** Engineering managers must **negotiate with product to
  slow velocity** temporarily so engineers can actually learn the tool, explore,
  run AI hackathons, and have fun with it — then apply it professionally.
- **Tame the bots.** Don't skip AI-driven testing and code review, or the toil
  comes back.

The open question she raises: does **personal** productivity (more code, faster)
actually translate to **organizational** productivity? Not automatically.
