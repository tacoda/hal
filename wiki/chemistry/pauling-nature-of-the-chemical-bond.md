# The Nature of the Chemical Bond

Linus Pauling's foundational treatise on the chemical bond, subtitled *An
Introduction to Modern Structural Chemistry*. First published in 1939 and issued
in its definitive third edition by Cornell University Press in 1960, it is the
single most influential book in structural chemistry — the work that translated
the newly-arrived quantum mechanics into a working chemist's vocabulary for how
and why atoms hold together. Pauling was awarded the 1954 Nobel Prize in
Chemistry substantially for this body of work.

## What it did

Before Pauling, quantum mechanics could in principle describe bonding but was
inaccessible to chemists. Pauling's achievement was to build a **largely
non-mathematical, physically intuitive theory** — grounded in quantum mechanics
but expressed in terms chemists could reason with. The book introduced or
systematized ideas that are now taught as basic vocabulary:

- **The covalent bond as electron sharing**, explained through the
  quantum-mechanical resonance of valence-bond structures rather than as a
  classical electrostatic effect. See [chemical-bonding.md](chemical-bonding.md).
- **Hybridization of atomic orbitals** (sp, sp², sp³), which reconciled the
  spherical/dumbbell shapes of s and p orbitals with the observed tetrahedral,
  trigonal, and linear geometries of real molecules — a bridge between
  [atomic-structure.md](atomic-structure.md) and molecular shape.
- **Electronegativity**, the Pauling scale, quantifying an atom's pull on shared
  electrons and thereby placing ionic and covalent bonding on a single
  continuum of bond polarity rather than as two separate categories.
- **Resonance**, the idea that a real molecule can be a hybrid of several Lewis
  structures (as in benzene), lowering its energy below any single contributor.
- **The hydrogen bond** and its structural consequences, including the role
  Pauling identified in the folding of proteins and, later, nucleic acids.
- Treatments of **electron-deficient bonding** (boranes, ferrocene), the
  **metallic bond and intermetallic compounds**, and the **electroneutrality
  principle**, added or expanded in the third edition.

## Why it anchors the field

The book is the origin point of valence-bond theory as chemists use it and of
the qualitative bonding language — hybridization, resonance, electronegativity,
polarity — that every later general-chemistry text inherits. It is the anchor
Reference for [chemical-bonding.md](chemical-bonding.md); its ideas about how
electron configuration and orbital shape dictate bonding tie directly back to
[atomic-structure.md](atomic-structure.md).
