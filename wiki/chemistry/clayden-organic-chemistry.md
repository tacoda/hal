# Organic Chemistry (Clayden)

The leading modern organic-chemistry textbook, by Jonathan Clayden, Nick
Greeves, and Stuart Warren, published by Oxford University Press (2nd edition,
2012). It broke with the traditional "one functional group per chapter"
organization and, in a single edition, became the student's-choice text for a
full undergraduate organic course.

## Scope and approach

The book's defining feature is that it is **mechanism-first and evidence-based**.
Instead of asking students to memorize the reactions of alcohols, then of
alkenes, then of carbonyls as isolated catalogs, it teaches the small set of
underlying principles that generate all of those reactions, and then shows how
each specific transformation follows. Its organizing ideas:

- **Structure and bonding first** — molecular orbitals, conjugation, and
  aromaticity are established up front, because a molecule's shape and electron
  distribution are what determine how it reacts. This rests directly on
  [chemical-bonding.md](chemical-bonding.md).
- **Reactivity as the interaction of nucleophiles and electrophiles** — the
  central mental model is that electron-rich sites attack electron-poor sites,
  and curly (curved) arrows track the movement of electron pairs through every
  step of a mechanism.
- **Evidence over assertion** — the text foregrounds *how we know*: it teaches
  NMR, IR, and mass spectrometry early and returns to spectroscopic and kinetic
  evidence throughout to justify why a given mechanism is the operating one.
- **Mechanistic families** — substitution, elimination, addition, and the
  chemistry of the carbonyl group (the single most important functional group)
  are developed as recurring patterns, then extended to conjugate addition,
  enolate chemistry, pericyclic reactions, and the aromatic chemistry of
  heterocycles.
- **Synthesis and stereochemistry** — retrosynthetic thinking, control of
  stereochemistry, and the strategy of building complex molecules, drawing on
  co-author Stuart Warren's work on synthetic disconnection.

The whole enterprise sits on top of the physical picture of the bond: why
carbon forms four bonds, why π systems are reactive, and why electronegativity
polarizes a carbonyl are questions answered by the bonding concepts in
[chemical-bonding.md](chemical-bonding.md), and the field as a whole is surveyed
in [organic-chemistry.md](organic-chemistry.md).

## Why it anchors the field

Clayden is the reference that taught a generation of chemists to *reason* about
organic reactions rather than recall them — to look at an unfamiliar molecule and
predict its chemistry from electron flow. It is the anchor Reference for
[organic-chemistry.md](organic-chemistry.md).
