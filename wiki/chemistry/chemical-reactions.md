# Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction is the rearrangement of atoms from one set of substances into another:
old bonds break, new bonds form, and the atoms are conserved but recombined. The nuclei are
untouched (that would be nuclear physics) — reactions are entirely a story about the outer
**electrons** and the [chemical bonds](chemical-bonding.md) they form. What we observe as a
color change, a gas evolving, heat released, or a precipitate falling out is the macroscopic
shadow of countless molecular collisions succeeding at rearrangement.

## What a reaction is, molecularly

For a reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide with enough energy and the right
orientation to reach a strained, high-energy **transition state**, from which they relax into
products. The energy required to reach that peak is the **activation energy**; how the
population of collisions clears it, and how fast, is the subject of
[chemical kinetics](chemical-kinetics.md). Whether the products are more stable than the
reactants — whether the reaction *tends* to go at all — is the subject of
[chemical thermodynamics](chemical-thermodynamics.md). The two questions are independent: a
reaction can be strongly favorable yet immeasurably slow (a diamond is thermodynamically
unstable relative to graphite, but kinetically stuck).

## Reaction notation

Reactants are written on the left, products on the right, joined by an arrow. Coefficients
balance the atoms (see [stoichiometry-and-the-mole.md](stoichiometry-and-the-mole.md)); phase
labels (s, l, g, aq) and conditions (Δ for heat, a catalyst above the arrow) add detail. A
double arrow ⇌ marks a reaction that reaches [equilibrium](chemical-equilibrium.md) rather
than going to completion.

## The classic reaction types

Introductory chemistry sorts reactions into a small number of recurring patterns. They are
not mutually exclusive — combustion is also a redox reaction — but the patterns make products
predictable.

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Synthesis (combination) | A + B → AB | $\mathrm{2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O}$ |
| Decomposition | AB → A + B | $\mathrm{2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2}$ |
| Single replacement | A + BC → AC + B | $\mathrm{Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2}$ |
| Double replacement | AB + CD → AD + CB | $\mathrm{AgNO_3 + NaCl \rightarrow AgCl + NaNO_3}$ |
| Combustion | fuel + O₂ → oxides | $\mathrm{CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O}$ |
| Acid–base (neutralization) | acid + base → salt + water | $\mathrm{HCl + NaOH \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O}$ |

Two of these patterns cut deeper and get their own notes:

- **Acid–base** reactions transfer a proton (H⁺) from a donor to an acceptor — see
  [acids-and-bases.md](acids-and-bases.md).
- **Redox** (oxidation–reduction) reactions transfer *electrons*, one species losing them
  (oxidized) while another gains them (reduced) — see
  [redox-and-electrochemistry.md](redox-and-electrochemistry.md). Combustion and single
  replacement are both redox in disguise.

## Energy changes

Every reaction absorbs or releases energy as bonds are broken (costs energy) and formed
(releases energy). If more is released than absorbed, the reaction is **exothermic** (ΔH < 0,
it warms the surroundings — combustion); if more is absorbed, **endothermic** (ΔH > 0, it
cools them — a cold pack). This enthalpy change, together with the entropy change, decides
spontaneity through the Gibbs free energy, developed in
[chemical-thermodynamics.md](chemical-thermodynamics.md).

## Why it matters

Reactions are the verb of chemistry — everything chemistry *does* is a reaction. Recognizing
the type lets you predict products; the balanced equation lets you quantify them; and the
energy and rate framing lets you ask the two questions that matter in practice: *will it go?*
and *how fast?* Those questions organize the rest of the chemistry notes.
