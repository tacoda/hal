# Chemistry: The Central Science

The standard year-long general-chemistry textbook for science and engineering
majors, by Theodore E. Brown, H. Eugene LeMay, Bruce E. Bursten, Catherine J.
Murphy, Patrick M. Woodward, and Matthew W. Stoltzfus. Now in its 15th edition
(Pearson), it earned its subtitle because chemistry sits between physics on one
side and biology, geology, and materials science on the other — the book is
built to serve that bridging role for students headed in any of those directions.

## Scope and approach

The text is a broad, self-contained survey of first-year college chemistry, and
its defining editorial trait is **conceptual clarity backed by disciplined
quantitative problem-solving**. Rather than front-loading quantum mechanics, it
follows a largely macroscopic-to-microscopic arc: it establishes what matter is
and how we measure it, then works down to atoms, then back up to how atoms
combine and react. The sequence, roughly:

- **Matter, measurement, and stoichiometry** — units, significant figures,
  classification of matter, then the mole concept, empirical/molecular formulas,
  limiting reagents, and the arithmetic of reactions. See
  [stoichiometry-and-the-mole.md](stoichiometry-and-the-mole.md).
- **Reactions in solution** — aqueous chemistry, precipitation, acid–base and
  redox reactions, and solution concentration.
- **Thermochemistry** — energy, enthalpy, calorimetry, and Hess's law, the
  entry point to [chemical-thermodynamics.md](chemical-thermodynamics.md).
- **Atomic and electronic structure** — light and quantized energy, the quantum
  model of the atom, orbitals, and electron configurations. See
  [atomic-structure.md](atomic-structure.md).
- **Periodicity** — how electron configuration drives periodic trends in size,
  ionization energy, and electron affinity. See
  [the-periodic-table.md](the-periodic-table.md).
- **Bonding and molecular geometry** — Lewis structures, VSEPR, valence-bond and
  molecular-orbital pictures. See [chemical-bonding.md](chemical-bonding.md).
- **States of matter, kinetics, equilibrium** — gases, intermolecular forces,
  solids, reaction rates, and the equilibrium framework.
- **Aqueous equilibria, thermodynamics, electrochemistry** — acid–base and
  solubility equilibria, entropy and free energy, and cells.
- **Descriptive and applied chapters** — nuclear chemistry, the chemistry of the
  nonmetals and transition metals, organic and biological chemistry, and
  environmental/materials topics.

## Why it anchors the field

The book's pedagogical hallmark is its **worked-example structure**: each concept
is paired with a solved sample exercise and a paired practice exercise, and the
end-of-chapter problem sets are large, graded in difficulty, and integrative
across chapters. This makes it the reference point most U.S. general-chemistry
courses (and their AP-level equivalents) are calibrated against. For HAL it is
the anchor Reference for the general-chemistry concept notes — most core
concepts in this folder can be traced back to a treatment here.
