# The Upanishads

The **Upanishads** (composed from ~800 BCE onward) are the philosophical culmination of the Vedas —
sometimes called *Vedanta*, "the end of the Vedas." Where the earlier Vedic texts concern ritual, the
Upanishads turn inward to metaphysics: the nature of ultimate reality, the self, and their
relationship. They are the seedbed of [Hindu philosophy](hindu-philosophy.md) and among the most
influential texts in world thought.

## Core teaching

The Upanishads' central discovery is the identity of the innermost self with ultimate reality:

- **Brahman** — the one infinite reality underlying and pervading all things.
- **Atman** — the true self, the deepest consciousness within each being.
- **Their identity** — *"tat tvam asi"* (**"that thou art"**) and *"aham brahmasmi"* ("I am
  Brahman"). The self, rightly understood, is not separate from the ground of all being. Realizing
  this identity is [liberation (moksha)](karma-samsara-and-moksha.md); mistaking the ego and the
  changing world for the real self is bondage.

The texts pursue this through dialogue, analogy, and negation — most famously **"neti neti"** ("not
this, not this"), the method of approaching Brahman by denying every limited description, since the
infinite exceeds all concepts.

## Influence

The Upanishads ground all schools of [Vedanta](hindu-philosophy.md) — Shankara's non-dualism reads
them as teaching strict oneness; Ramanuja and Madhva read them as affirming a personal God and real
selves. Their non-dual vision reached the West through translation and struck thinkers from
Schopenhauer to the Transcendentalists. They remain the philosophical heart of the Hindu tradition.
