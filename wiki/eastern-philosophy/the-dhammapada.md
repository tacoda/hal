# The Dhammapada

The **Dhammapada** ("verses of the Dhamma/path") is the most famous and beloved text of early
[Buddhism](buddhism.md) — a collection of some 423 short verses, in the Pali Canon, traditionally
gathered from the Buddha's teachings. Compact, aphoristic, and practical, it distills the path into
memorable lines and has served for over two millennia as an entry point to Buddhist thought.

## Core teaching

The Dhammapada emphasizes the practical and psychological heart of the path over metaphysical system:

- **The primacy of mind.** It opens by declaring that we are shaped by our thoughts: *"All that we
  are is the result of what we have thought."* Mental cultivation is the root of the path.
- **Craving and its overcoming.** Suffering springs from craving and attachment; the verses
  repeatedly urge letting go, since clinging to the impermanent binds one to
  [suffering and rebirth](karma-samsara-and-moksha.md).
- **Ethical self-mastery.** Restraint, mindfulness, patience, non-hatred (*"hatred is never appeased
  by hatred; it is appeased by love"*), and heedfulness are its recurring counsels — the
  [Eightfold Path](buddhism.md) in lived, everyday form.
- **The sage.** It draws a portrait of the awakened one: calm, free of craving and ill-will,
  unmoved by praise or blame.

## Influence

For its clarity and universality, the Dhammapada is among the most translated Buddhist texts and a
common first encounter with the tradition for readers worldwide. It carries the shared ground of
early Buddhism from which the later [schools](buddhist-schools.md) — Theravada, Mahayana, Zen —
diverged.
