# The Bhagavad Gita

The **Bhagavad Gita** ("Song of the Lord," ~2nd c. BCE), an episode within the epic *Mahabharata*, is
the most widely read and beloved text of [Hindu philosophy](hindu-philosophy.md). It stages a
dialogue on a battlefield: the warrior **Arjuna**, paralyzed by the prospect of fighting his own kin,
is counseled by his charioteer **Krishna** (an incarnation of the divine). Out of this crisis of duty
comes a compact synthesis of Hindu thought.

## Core teaching

The Gita's genius is to reconcile worldly duty with spiritual liberation:

- **Do your dharma.** Arjuna must act according to his duty and station rather than flee it;
  withdrawal is not the path.
- **Karma yoga — action without attachment.** The key teaching: act, but **renounce attachment to the
  fruits** of action. Perform your duty as an offering, without craving reward or fearing failure.
  This purifies action of ego and karmic bondage, letting one remain free *within* activity rather
  than escaping into renunciation. It resolves the tension in
  [karma and moksha](karma-samsara-and-moksha.md): one can pursue liberation without abandoning the
  world.
- **The paths converge.** The Gita presents and harmonizes the great yogas —
  **karma** (selfless action), **jnana** (knowledge), **bhakti** (loving devotion), and
  **dhyana** (meditation) — as different routes to the same goal, with special warmth for *bhakti*,
  devotion to a personal God.

## Influence

The Gita became the practical heart of Hinduism, commented on by every major
[Vedanta](hindu-philosophy.md) school. Its teaching of disciplined, non-attached action shaped
**Gandhi**, who called it his spiritual dictionary and read its "battle" allegorically as the moral
struggle within. It remains the most quoted Hindu scripture worldwide.
