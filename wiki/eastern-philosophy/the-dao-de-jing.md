# The Dao De Jing

The **Dao De Jing** ("Classic of the Way and its Power," also *Tao Te Ching*), attributed to the
semi-legendary **Laozi** (~6th–4th c. BCE), is the founding text of [Daoism](daoism.md) and, after
the Bible, among the most translated books in the world. In roughly 81 short, poetic, often paradoxical
chapters it sketches a philosophy of the **Dao** — the ineffable Way of nature — and how the sage and
the ruler should align with it.

## Core teaching

- **The ineffable Dao.** It opens with a warning against its own project: *"The Dao that can be told
  is not the eternal Dao."* Ultimate reality exceeds language and concepts; naming and dividing lead
  the mind away from the seamless whole.
- **Wu wei — effortless action.** The central practical teaching: accomplish by *not* forcing. Act
  with, not against, the grain of things; like **water**, overcome the hard by yielding, and achieve
  much through non-contrivance.
- **The power of the soft, low, and empty.** The Dao De Jing inverts ordinary values, praising
  weakness over strength, emptiness over fullness, the valley over the peak — because the yielding
  outlasts the rigid.
- **Govern least.** Much of the text addresses the ruler: the best government is minimal and
  unobtrusive, leaving the people to flourish of themselves (*ziran*); heavy-handed laws and ambition
  breed disorder — a pointed contrast with [Confucian](confucianism.md) activism and
  [Legalist](mohism-and-legalism.md) control.

## Influence

The Dao De Jing shaped Chinese religion, art, medicine, and statecraft, fed directly into
[Chan/Zen Buddhism](buddhist-schools.md), and has become a global classic read for its wisdom on
nature, humility, leadership, and the limits of striving. Its idea of **wu wei** — mastery as
effortless attunement — is its most enduring gift.
