# The Analects

The **Analects** (*Lunyu*, "selected sayings") is the foundational text of
[Confucianism](confucianism.md): a collection of the sayings, brief dialogues, and actions of
**Confucius (Kongzi, 551–479 BCE)**, compiled by his disciples after his death. Rather than a
systematic treatise, it is a mosaic of aphorisms and anecdotes from which the Confucian vision of the
good life and the good society is assembled.

## Core teaching

The Analects circles a set of interlocking ideas developed in the [Confucianism](confucianism.md)
note:

- **Ren (humaneness)** — the central virtue, an active care for others; the text offers the
  reciprocity rule, *"do not impose on others what you do not desire for yourself."*
- **Li (ritual propriety)** — the forms, manners, and rites through which virtue is cultivated and
  expressed; character is shaped by practicing proper conduct.
- **The junzi (exemplary person)** — the ideal of the cultivated, virtuous person, contrasted with
  the *xiaoren* ("small person") who is ruled by profit and self-interest. Nobility is moral, not
  hereditary.
- **Filial piety (xiao)** and the ordering of relationships — virtue begins with reverence for
  parents and radiates outward to a well-ordered society.
- **Rule by virtue** — the ruler should govern by moral example, not force: *"govern by virtue, and
  you will be like the north star, which the other stars orbit."*
- **Learning** — the Analects opens with the joy of study and returns constantly to lifelong
  self-cultivation through learning and reflection.

## Influence

The Analects became the core text of East Asian education for two millennia and the backbone of the
Chinese civil-service examinations. Reinterpreted through the metaphysics of
[Neo-Confucianism](neo-confucianism.md), it shaped the ethics, family life, and governance of China,
Korea, Japan, and Vietnam. It is the single most influential book in East Asian civilization.
