# Hindu Philosophy

Hindu philosophy is not one doctrine but a family of systems that accept the authority of the
**Vedas** — the oldest Indian scriptures — and their philosophical culmination, the
[Upanishads](the-upanishads.md). Classically it is organized into **six schools (darshanas,
"viewpoints")**, and its most influential later development, **Vedanta**, gives the picture most
people mean by "Hindu philosophy": a metaphysics of the ultimate reality *Brahman* and the true
self *Atman*.

## The six orthodox schools (darshanas)

The *astika* schools each take a distinct angle, and were often studied in complementary pairs:

- **Nyaya** — logic and epistemology; a rigorous theory of valid knowledge and inference (a major
  contributor to Indian [logic](../logic/index.md)).
- **Vaisheshika** — atomism and natural philosophy; reality as configurations of atoms.
- **Samkhya** — a dualism of *purusha* (pure consciousness) and *prakriti* (matter/nature), whose
  entanglement is the root of bondage.
- **Yoga** — the practical discipline (Patanjali's eight limbs) for stilling the mind and freeing
  *purusha*; paired with Samkhya's metaphysics.
- **Mimamsa** — hermeneutics of Vedic ritual and duty (*dharma*).
- **Vedanta** — the "end of the Vedas," interpreting the Upanishads; the dominant school.

## Brahman, Atman, and maya

Vedanta's central claims:

- **Brahman** — the single, infinite, ultimate reality underlying all things; not a personal
  creator so much as the ground of being itself (*sat-chit-ananda*: being, consciousness, bliss).
- **Atman** — the true self, the innermost consciousness. The Upanishadic revelation is the
  identity **Atman = Brahman** ("*tat tvam asi*," *that thou art*): the self at its deepest is not
  separate from ultimate reality.
- **Maya** — the power by which the one appears as the many; the world of separate selves and
  objects is not simply illusion but a lower, dependent order of reality that conceals the
  underlying unity.
- **Avidya** (ignorance) — mistaking the ego and the changing world for the real self is the root
  bondage; **liberation** ([moksha](karma-samsara-and-moksha.md)) is the direct realization of the
  Atman–Brahman identity.

## Schools of Vedanta

Vedanta itself splits over how to read Atman and Brahman:

- **Advaita (non-dualism, Shankara, ~8th c.)** — strictly *one*: only Brahman is ultimately real,
  the plural world is maya, and liberation is knowledge dissolving the illusion of separateness.
- **Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism, Ramanuja)** — Brahman is real and personal (identified
  with God), and selves and world are real *parts* of it; liberation is loving devotion (*bhakti*),
  not the erasure of the individual.
- **Dvaita (dualism, Madhva)** — self and God remain genuinely distinct; the path is devotion to a
  fully personal God.

## The paths to liberation

Hinduism recognizes multiple **yogas** (disciplines) suited to different temperaments, famously set
out in the [Bhagavad Gita](the-bhagavad-gita.md): **jnana** (knowledge), **bhakti** (devotion),
**karma** (selfless action), and **raja/dhyana** (meditation). All aim at the same
[moksha](karma-samsara-and-moksha.md).

## Why it matters

Hindu philosophy offers one of humanity's most fully developed **non-dual** metaphysics — the claim
that the deepest self and ultimate reality are one — and a sophisticated account of how a single
reality can appear as a world of many. Its concept of a permanent, real self (*Atman*) is also the
sharpest foil to the [Buddhist](buddhism.md) denial of exactly that (*anatta*), a debate that
structures much of Indian philosophy.
