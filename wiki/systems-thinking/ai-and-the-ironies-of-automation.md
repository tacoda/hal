# AI and the Ironies of Automation

Uwe Friedrichsen takes Lisanne Bainbridge's 1983
[Ironies of Automation](ironies-of-automation.md) and maps it, point for point,
onto today's push to have humans "supervise" AI agents. His conclusion: the
currently fashionable model — turn subject-matter experts into overseers who
watch the AI and intervene when it errs — is **not sustainable**, and the
reasons were spelled out four decades ago.

## The observer paradox, restated for AI

Bainbridge's paradox — *supervision demands the very skill the automation
destroys* — applies cleanly. You can only judge an AI's output and catch its
mistakes if you could do the work yourself, at the level the AI is doing it, all
day. But once the AI does the work, you don't — so the competence erodes.
Friedrichsen's phrase: experts are being "degraded to AI chaperons."

## Four ways it bites

- **Monitoring fatigue.** Humans can't stay vigilant over a source where little
  happens. AI is right most of the time, so attention drops — and AI errors
  arrive "disguised in an overly self-confident presentation," which makes the
  rare mistake *harder* to spot, not easier.
- **The unlearning dilemma.** Skills decay without use. An expert reduced to
  watching an AI slowly becomes "a beginner who once was an expert." The
  background knowledge lingers, but doing real work takes longer and longer.
  This is [comprehension debt](../ai-org/comprehension-debt.md) at the level of the
  individual's own competence.
- **The next-generation dilemma.** Today's overseers ride on skills built by
  doing the work before AI. The people coming up behind them will never build
  those skills — they'll have neither the expertise to supervise nor the chance
  to acquire it. (Same missing-rung worry as
  [AI won't kill junior devs](../ai-org/ai-wont-kill-junior-devs.md) and
  [learning the craft](../ai-org/learning-the-craft.md).)
- **Delayed and invisible.** None of this shows yet, because people only recently
  started, and still do much work themselves. By the time deskilling is obvious,
  effective countermeasures may be out of reach.

## Why the easy answers don't hold

- *"The AI will get good enough that no human oversight is needed."* Mostly said
  by people whose interests are served by you believing it; and LLMs, by
  construction, are unlikely to become reliably error-free. Fine for low-stakes
  work (a rough draft, some market research), not for software that must run in
  production.
- *"An AI-fixer profession will emerge"* — people who keep their skills sharp by
  doing the work themselves and get called in when the AI fails. Plausible, and
  early versions are appearing, but it concedes the point: someone still has to
  stay genuinely skilled.
