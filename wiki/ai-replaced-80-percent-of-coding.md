# AI Replaced 80% of Coding, Only These 7 Skills Are Left

**Naveen Gaddam** argues that the common prediction — AI writes the code, so engineers are
finished — mistook *typing* for *engineering*. AI did compress the mechanical part of the
job: it generates syntax, boilerplate, tests, and imitations of design patterns faster
than any human. But as that generated code moved from demos into production, a harder
truth surfaced.

## The real story

> "Writing code faster is not the same as engineering better systems. Syntax can be
> automated. Engineering cannot."

Companies adopted AI to speed up code generation and, on the surface, it worked. What did
*not* get cheaper is the discipline underneath the syntax: **designing robust systems,
making trade-offs under pressure, debugging real failures, protecting reliability, and
aligning technical work with the actual goals of the business.** The act of typing code is
becoming a commodity; the judgement around it is not. Software engineering, in Gaddam's
framing, is not dying — it is *evolving*, and the value migrates to the skills that AI
cannot automate.

## The shape of the argument

The premise — mechanical coding automated, engineering judgement left standing — is the
same one running through much of the wiki:

- It is the "80% of coding is cheap now, the remaining discipline is everything" version
  of the role shift in [From Coder to Orchestrator](from-coder-to-orchestrator.md) and
  [Five Engineering Archetypes (Boris Cherny)](five-engineering-archetypes.md).
- It supplies the *why* behind [The Middle Loop](the-middle-loop.md): if code generation
  is automated, the human's remaining work is directing, evaluating, and correcting — the
  supervisory skills Gaddam is enumerating.
- It answers the "are juniors doomed?" panic the way
  [AI Won't Kill Junior Devs](ai-wont-kill-junior-devs.md) and
  [Revenge of the Junior Developer](revenge-of-the-junior-developer.md) do: the skills
  that survive are learnable, and they are what training should now target.

The durable skills cluster around system design and architecture, debugging and reasoning
about failures, trade-off decision-making under real constraints, safeguarding
reliability and quality, and connecting engineering work to business outcomes — precisely
the parts of the job that require holding context and judgement rather than producing
tokens.
