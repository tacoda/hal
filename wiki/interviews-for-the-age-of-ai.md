# Software Engineer Interviews for the Age of AI

Swizec Teller's argument starts from why you still need great engineers when AI writes
plausible code: **someone has to own the running system.** His merge policy — "we have
your phone number; when you write code with AI, it's still your phone number" — captures
it. Customers pay for a reliable service, not a pile of code. The future of software
engineering is **SRE**: great engineers *own* the systems they build, accountability and
all. AI generates lots of plausible code, but a human signs their name to production.

## What to look for: knowledge expanding fractally

Great engineers have knowledge that "expands fractally" — the closer you look at any
detail, the more real detail is there. Demos are easy; production is hard. The interview
should probe for that fractal depth, which reading, managing, or vibe-coding gives only
at a high level, and only *doing the work* fills in.

**System design interviews** are the sharpest tool for this. Draw boxes and arrows, watch
how the candidate thinks, and — crucially — pay attention to the **questions they ask.**
The job is to "get us over the water, not build us a bridge": if everything could be
specified up front, you'd just ask AI to write the code. How a candidate pushes back,
questions assumptions, clarifies details, and raises operational concerns is the signal.
You're hunting for the **illusion of explanatory depth** — we feel we understand familiar
things until we try to explain them and hit the edge of our knowledge. System design also
levels candidates cleanly: it reveals where someone has hands-on experience versus where
they've only read.

This is the same durable-signal thesis as [Hiring in the AI Era](hiring-in-the-ai-era.md)
— judgment, systems thinking, and taste survive when code volume stops meaning output —
seen through the lens of ownership and operability.
