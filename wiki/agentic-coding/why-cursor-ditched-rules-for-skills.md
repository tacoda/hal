# Why Cursor Ditched Rules for Skills

A short clip (AI Native Dev / Tessl) on why **agent skills** caught on across the
industry. Anthropic introduced skills late in the prior year, and — much as happened
with **MCP** — the rest of the industry (Cursor among them) lined up behind the format
fast.

## The origin and the flywheel

Skills were first introduced in Claude **less as a developer feature and more as a way
for non-developers to write instructions** — e.g. telling Claude how to build an Excel
sheet. That's significant: it handed non-devs a taste of a software-development idea,
**reusable artifacts you can share across a team**, without needing to code. Combine
developer adoption with that non-developer on-ramp and you get a **flywheel** of
excitement.

## The underlying belief

The clip frames the excitement around a conviction the speaker's team (Tessl) has held
all along: **context should be reusable** — you shouldn't reinvent the wheel each time.
And the sharpest line: there's a **difference between intelligence and knowledge**. The
model supplies intelligence; a skill packages *knowledge* so it can be reused and
shared rather than re-derived on every task. That reuse angle is the same one behind
[context engineering](../harness-engineering/context-engineering.md), and skills are the packaging format for
it.
