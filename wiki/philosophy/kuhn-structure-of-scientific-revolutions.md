# The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (Kuhn)

Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) is the most influential work
in twentieth-century philosophy of science, and the book that made "paradigm" and "paradigm
shift" household terms. Trained as a physicist, Kuhn turned to the history of science and
argued — against the prevailing view of science as steady, cumulative progress — that
science advances through long stretches of stability punctuated by sudden, discontinuous
revolutions. See [philosophy-of-science.md](philosophy-of-science.md).

## Normal science and paradigms

Most science, Kuhn says, is **normal science**: research firmly grounded in a **paradigm** —
a shared framework of exemplary achievements, theories, methods, and standards that a
scientific community takes for granted. Within a paradigm, scientists do not test the
framework; they do **puzzle-solving**, applying it to new problems and refining its fit with
nature. The paradigm defines which problems are legitimate and what counts as a solution.

## The revolutionary cycle

```mermaid
flowchart LR
  P[Pre-paradigm: competing schools] --> N[Normal science under a paradigm]
  N --> A[Anomalies accumulate]
  A --> C[Crisis: confidence in the paradigm erodes]
  C --> R[Revolution: a new paradigm displaces the old]
  R --> N2[New normal science]
  N2 -.-> A
```

- **Anomalies** — persistent puzzles the paradigm cannot solve — accumulate. Individually
  they are tolerated; collectively they can precipitate a **crisis**.
- In crisis, the community's faith in the paradigm weakens and rival frameworks appear.
- A **revolution** occurs when one candidate wins out and the community's worldview shifts —
  Ptolemaic to Copernican astronomy, Newtonian to relativistic and quantum physics.

## Incommensurability

Kuhn's most radical and contested claim is **incommensurability**: competing paradigms lack
a common measure. Their key terms shift meaning ("mass" means something different in
Newtonian and relativistic physics), they disagree on which problems even matter, and
theory choice is not settled by a neutral algorithm. A paradigm shift is less like being
argued into a conclusion and more like a **gestalt switch** — scientists come to *see* the
world differently. Kuhn still held that shared values (accuracy, consistency, scope,
simplicity, fruitfulness) guide choice, but these are values weighed by judgment, not rules
that dictate a verdict.

## Why it matters

Kuhn challenged the idea that science converges monotonically on truth by pure logic and
observation, foregrounding the community, its history, and its shared commitments. This
reframed debates in epistemology about theory-ladenness of observation, relativism, and
scientific rationality — and its vocabulary now pervades far beyond science. See
[epistemology.md](epistemology.md).
