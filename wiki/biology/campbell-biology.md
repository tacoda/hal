# Campbell Biology

*Campbell Biology* — begun by Neil Campbell in 1987 and carried on with Jane Reece and
a rotating team of co-authors after Campbell's death — is the standard comprehensive
introductory-biology textbook for majors in North America. It aims to cover the whole
discipline in a single volume, from atoms and molecules up through ecosystems and the
biosphere, at the depth expected of a first-year university course.

## Scope

The book is organized to walk a student up the levels of biological organization:

- **The chemistry of life** — water, carbon, and the four classes of biological
  macromolecules, providing the chemical grounding the rest of the book depends on
  (see [biochemistry-and-metabolism.md](biochemistry-and-metabolism.md) and
  [../chemistry/organic-chemistry.md](../chemistry/organic-chemistry.md)).
- **The cell** — membrane structure, organelles, cellular respiration, photosynthesis,
  the cell cycle, and cell communication (see [the-cell.md](the-cell.md)).
- **Genetics** — Mendelian inheritance, the chromosomal basis of heredity, molecular
  genetics, gene expression and regulation, and biotechnology (see
  [genetics-and-heredity.md](genetics-and-heredity.md) and
  [molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md](molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md)).
- **Evolution** — descent with modification, the evidence for evolution, population
  genetics, speciation, and the history of life (see
  [evolution-by-natural-selection.md](evolution-by-natural-selection.md)).
- **Diversity, plant and animal form and function, and ecology** — the surveys of the
  domains and kingdoms, physiology, and interactions among organisms and their
  environment.

## Central idea: evolution as the unifying theme

The book's organizing thesis is the one Dobzhansky made famous: nothing in biology
makes sense except in the light of evolution. Evolution is presented not as one chapter
among many but as the thread that ties the levels together — why cells share a common
biochemistry, why the genetic code is nearly universal, why anatomy and physiology take
the forms they do. Later editions foreground a small set of recurring themes
(organization, information, energy and matter, interactions, evolution) and emphasize
scientific inquiry, teaching biology as a process of hypothesis and evidence rather than
a catalog of facts.

Because of its breadth and adoption, *Campbell Biology* functions as the shared baseline
vocabulary for the field — the reference against which the more specialized canonical
works (Alberts on the cell, Lehninger on biochemistry, Darwin and Dawkins on evolution)
add depth.
