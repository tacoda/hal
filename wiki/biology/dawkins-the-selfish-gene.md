# The Selfish Gene

Richard Dawkins's *The Selfish Gene* (1976) is a work of popular science that reframed
Darwinian evolution around the gene rather than the organism or the group. Building on
George C. Williams's *Adaptation and Natural Selection* and W. D. Hamilton's theory of
kin selection, it argues that the fundamental unit on which natural selection acts is
the gene — the *replicator* that persists across generations — while organisms are the
transient *vehicles* (or "survival machines") that genes build to propagate themselves.

## The gene's-eye view

The central move is one of perspective. Natural selection favors whatever traits leave
the most copies of the responsible genes in future generations. "Selfish" is a
metaphor: genes have no intent, but those that happen to build bodies and behaviors that
spread copies of themselves come to dominate the gene pool. This reframing dissolves
several puzzles Darwin's organism-level account left awkward
(see [evolution-by-natural-selection.md](evolution-by-natural-selection.md)):

- **Altruism and kin selection.** An animal that helps relatives can be favored because
  relatives carry copies of the same genes. The more genes two individuals share, the
  more a gene "gains" by promoting cooperation between them — Hamilton's rule, made
  intuitive.
- **Family and sexual conflict.** Parent–offspring conflict, sibling rivalry, and the
  battle of the sexes all follow from each party's genes pursuing their own replication,
  drawing on Trivers's parental-investment theory and Fisher's sex-ratio argument.
- **Evolutionarily stable strategies.** Borrowing game theory from Maynard Smith,
  Dawkins shows how populations settle into stable mixes of behaviors (hawk/dove) that
  no mutant strategy can invade.

This all rests on inheritance working at the level of discrete genes, tying the book to
[genetics-and-heredity.md](genetics-and-heredity.md) and the molecular basis of the
replicator in
[molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md](molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md).

## The meme

In its final chapter the book introduces the *meme* — a unit of cultural transmission
(a tune, an idea, a fashion) that propagates from mind to mind by imitation. Dawkins
proposed it as a second kind of replicator, subject to its own selection, and the idea
spawned the field of memetics. Later editions add the chapter "Nice Guys Finish First"
and the concept of the *extended phenotype*, in which a gene's effects reach beyond the
body that carries it.
