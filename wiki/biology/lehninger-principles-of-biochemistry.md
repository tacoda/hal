# Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry

*Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry* is the standard undergraduate biochemistry text.
It descends from Albert Lehninger's *Biochemistry* (1970) — Lehninger, who with Eugene
Kennedy showed that mitochondria are the site of oxidative phosphorylation, was a
founder of modern bioenergetics — and has been maintained since his death by David
Nelson and Michael Cox. The book is prized for teaching biochemistry as a set of
comprehensible principles rather than a mass of pathways to memorize.

## Scope

The text moves from molecular foundations to the chemistry of the living cell:

- **Foundations.** The chemical logic of life — water and the weak interactions that
  govern biomolecular structure, acid–base behavior and buffers, and the organic
  chemistry of the major building blocks (see
  [../chemistry/organic-chemistry.md](../chemistry/organic-chemistry.md)).
- **Structure and catalysis.** Amino acids and the three-dimensional structure of
  proteins, enzymes and enzyme kinetics, carbohydrates, lipids, nucleotides, and
  membranes — how form determines function.
- **Bioenergetics and metabolism.** Its intellectual core: the thermodynamics of living
  systems, the central role of ATP, and the pathways that extract, store, and spend
  energy — glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, photosynthesis,
  and the synthesis and breakdown of carbohydrates, fats, and amino acids. This anchors
  [biochemistry-and-metabolism.md](biochemistry-and-metabolism.md).
- **Information pathways.** DNA replication, transcription, translation, and their
  regulation, treated as the chemistry underlying
  [molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md](molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md).

## Central ideas

Two themes recur throughout. First, **energy coupling**: cells obey thermodynamics, and
metabolism works by coupling energetically unfavorable reactions to favorable ones,
most often through the hydrolysis of ATP. Second, **molecular recognition and
catalysis**: enzymes make life's chemistry fast and specific by stabilizing transition
states, and the same weak, reversible interactions that fold proteins also let them bind
their substrates and partners. The book consistently grounds these principles in the
underlying chemistry, making it the biochemical bridge between organic chemistry and
cell and molecular biology.
