# The Double Helix

James D. Watson's *The Double Helix* (1968) is a first-person memoir of the discovery of
the structure of DNA, made with Francis Crick at Cambridge in 1953. It is not a textbook
but a candid — and famously controversial — account of how one of the central results in
biology was actually reached: through competition, guesswork, borrowed data, and a race
against rival labs.

## What was discovered

Watson and Crick determined that DNA is a *double helix*: two antiparallel sugar–
phosphate backbones winding around a common axis, with the nitrogenous bases paired on
the inside — adenine with thymine, guanine with cytosine. Two features made the model
immediately momentous, as Watson recounts:

- **Complementary base pairing** means each strand specifies the other, so the molecule
  carries a built-in mechanism for its own copying — the physical basis of heredity.
- **The linear sequence of bases** can encode information, pointing directly toward the
  genetic code.

The book stresses how the answer came from synthesizing others' work: Chargaff's rules
on base ratios, Pauling's model-building methods, and — decisively — Rosalind Franklin's
and Maurice Wilkins's X-ray diffraction images (notably "Photo 51"), which revealed the
helical geometry. Watson's dismissive portrayal of Franklin is the memoir's most
criticized aspect, and later accounts have restored the centrality of her data. Watson,
Crick, and Wilkins shared the 1962 Nobel Prize; Franklin had died in 1958.

## Significance

The structure launched molecular biology. Complementary pairing explained replication
and set up the account of information flow from DNA to RNA to protein — the material this
note anchors in
[molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md](molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md).
As a personal narrative it is also a rare, unvarnished portrait of the messy, human
process of scientific discovery, which is why it endures as a canonical read.
