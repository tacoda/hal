# Genetics and Heredity

Genetics is the study of how traits are passed from parents to offspring and how the
underlying information varies within populations. It sits between two other topics here: it
is the observable, whole-organism face of
[molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma](molecular-biology-and-the-central-dogma.md) (a
gene is a stretch of DNA, an allele a variant sequence), and it supplies the heritable
variation that
[evolution-by-natural-selection](evolution-by-natural-selection.md) acts on.

## Mendel's insight

Gregor Mendel, breeding pea plants in the 1860s, discovered that inheritance is
**particulate** — traits pass as discrete units (now called **genes**), not by blending. His
two laws still frame the subject:

- **Law of segregation** — each organism carries two copies of each gene and passes one at
  random to each offspring. The physical basis is the separation of homologous chromosomes
  in meiosis (see [cell-division-and-reproduction](cell-division-and-reproduction.md)).
- **Law of independent assortment** — genes on different chromosomes are inherited
  independently of one another.

## The core vocabulary

- **Gene** — a unit of heredity, a DNA sequence coding for a product.
- **Allele** — a specific version of a gene (e.g. a "brown" vs. "blue" variant).
- **Genotype** — the alleles an organism actually carries.
- **Phenotype** — the observable trait that results, from genotype plus environment.
- **Dominant / recessive** — a dominant allele shows its phenotype with just one copy; a
  recessive one shows only when both copies match. **Homozygous** means two identical
  alleles, **heterozygous** two different ones.

A **Punnett square** predicts offspring ratios. Crossing two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa) gives
the classic 3:1 dominant-to-recessive phenotype ratio:

|        | A     | a     |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **A**  | AA    | Aa    |
| **a**  | Aa    | aa    |

Three of four offspring show the dominant phenotype (AA, Aa, Aa); one shows the recessive
(aa).

## Chromosomes and linkage

Genes ride on **chromosomes**, and their positions matter. Genes far apart or on different
chromosomes assort independently (Mendel's second law), but genes physically close on the
same chromosome tend to be inherited together — they are **linked**. **Recombination**
(crossing over during meiosis) shuffles linked genes, and the frequency with which two genes
separate is proportional to the distance between them — the basis of genetic mapping.

Reality is messier than Mendel's clean ratios: many traits are **polygenic** (height, skin
color — controlled by many genes and so continuously variable), alleles can be
**codominant** or show incomplete dominance, and sex-linked genes follow their own patterns.

## Population genetics

Zoom out from families to populations and genetics becomes statistical. **Allele
frequencies** — how common each variant is in a gene pool — are the state variable. The
**Hardy–Weinberg principle** describes a population *not* evolving: with random mating and no
selection, mutation, migration, or drift, allele and genotype frequencies stay constant
across generations. It is a null hypothesis; when real frequencies deviate from it, some
evolutionary force is at work. The forces that change allele frequencies — natural
selection, genetic drift, gene flow, mutation — are exactly the mechanisms of
[evolution-by-natural-selection](evolution-by-natural-selection.md).

## Why it matters

Genetics is predictive: knowing genotypes lets you forecast offspring, trace disease alleles
through pedigrees, and design breeding programs. It also unifies scales — the abstract "gene"
of a Punnett square is the same DNA sequence read by the central dogma and the same variant
counted in a population's allele frequencies. That continuity from molecule to family to
population is what makes modern medical genetics and
[genomics-and-biotechnology](genomics-and-biotechnology.md) possible.
